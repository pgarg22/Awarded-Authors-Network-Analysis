'''Tracy S. Letts''' (born July 4, 1965) is an American actor, playwright, and screenwriter. He received the [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]] and the [[Tony Award for Best Play]] for his production, ''[[August: Osage County]]'' (2007), and the [[Tony Award for Best Actor in a Play]] for his portrayal of George in the revival of ''[[Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?]]'' (2013).<ref name="Pulitzer">{{cite web|url=http://www.pulitzer.org/citation/2008-Drama|title=The 2008 Pulitzer Prize Winners – Drama|publisher=pulitzer.org}}</ref>

He is also known for his portrayal of [[Andrew Lockhart]] in seasons 3 and 4 of [[Showtime (TV network)|Showtime]]'s [[Homeland (TV series)|''Homeland'']], for which he has been nominated for two Screen Actors Guild Awards as a member of the ensemble. He portrayed the [[Bernie Madoff|pyramid-scheme con-artist]] Nick on the HBO comedy ''[[Divorce (TV series)|Divorce]]''. In 2017, Letts starred in three critically acclaimed films: ''[[The Lovers (2017 film)|The Lovers]]'', ''[[Lady Bird (film)|Lady Bird]]'', and ''[[The Post (film)|The Post]]''. The latter two films were nominated for the [[Academy Award for Best Picture]]. ''Lady Bird'' earned Letts a nomination for the [[Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by a Cast in a Motion Picture]] nomination. In 2019, he portrayed [[Henry Ford II]] in [[James Mangold]]'s ''[[Ford v Ferrari]]''.

Letts wrote the screenplays of three films adapted from his own plays: ''[[Bug (2006 film)|Bug]]'' and ''[[Killer Joe (film)|Killer Joe]]'', both directed by [[William Friedkin]], and ''[[August: Osage County (film)|August: Osage County]]'', directed by [[John Wells (filmmaker)|John Wells]]. His 2009 play ''[[Superior Donuts]]'' was adapted into a [[Superior Donuts (TV series)|television series of the same name]]. His first screenplay not to be adapted from his own work, ''[[The Woman in the Window (2021 film)|The Woman in the Window]]'', based on the [[The Woman in the Window (novel)|2018 novel of the same name]] by A. J. Finn, was released on May 14, 2021.

==Early life==
Letts was born in [[Tulsa, Oklahoma]], to author [[Billie Letts]] (née Gipson) and college professor and actor [[Dennis Letts]].<ref name="Billie Letts">{{cite web|url=http://www.readersread.com/features/billieletts2.htm|title=Interview with Billie Letts|work=readersread.org}}</ref><ref name=nyt>{{cite news | agency=Associated Press | title=Dennis Letts, 73, a Professor Who Became Broadway Actor, Dies | url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/02/25/theater/25letts.html?_r=1&em&ex=1204606800 | work=The New York Times | date=2008-02-25 | access-date=2008-07-03}}</ref> He has two brothers, Shawn, a musician, and Dana. Letts was raised in [[Durant, Oklahoma]] and graduated from [[Durant High School (Oklahoma)|Durant High School]] in the early 1980s. He moved to [[Dallas]], where he waited tables and worked in telemarketing while beginning his acting career. He appeared in Jerry Flemmons' ''O Dammit!'', which was part of a new playwrights' series sponsored by [[Southern Methodist University]].

Letts moved to [[Chicago]] at the age of 20, working for the next 11 years at [[Steppenwolf Theatre Company]] and Famous Door. He is still an active member of Steppenwolf. He was a founding member of Bang Bang Spontaneous Theatre, whose members included [[Greg Kotis]], [[Michael Shannon]], [[Paul Dillon]], and [[Amy Pietz]].<ref>{{cite news | title=Tracy Letts's Productions at Steppenwolf | url=http://www.steppenwolf.org/ensemble/members/productions.aspx?id=41 | work=Steppenwolf Theatre Company | year=2008 | access-date=2008-07-03}}</ref> In 1991, Letts wrote the play ''[[Killer Joe (play)|Killer Joe]]''. Two years later, the play premiered at the Next Lab Theater in [[Evanston, Illinois]], followed by the 29th Street Rep in [[New York City]]. Since then, ''Killer Joe'' has been performed in a number of countries in 12 languages.<ref name=observer>{{cite news | author=Carlton Stowers | title=Sweet Revenge | url=http://dallasobserver.com/2003-11-27/news/sweet-revenge | work=The Dallas Observer | date=27 November 2003| access-date=2008-07-03}}</ref>

His mother, Billie Letts, has said of his work, "I try to be upbeat and funny. Everybody in Tracy's stories gets naked or dead."<ref name=observer /> Letts's plays have depicted people struggling with moral and spiritual questions. He says he was inspired by the plays of [[Tennessee Williams]] and the novels of [[William Faulkner]] and [[Jim Thompson (writer)|Jim Thompson]]. Letts states that he considers sounds to be effective "storytelling tools" for theater.<ref>{{cite web | author=Aifen Wang | title=In-your-face Theatre with In Your Face Sound Design | url=http://www.stageresearch.com/showcases/indexArchived.aspx?0612_showcase.html | publisher=Stage Research | year=2008 | access-date=2008-07-03}}{{dead link|date=February 2020}}</ref>

==Career==

=== Theatre ===
==== Acting ====
During the late 1980s through the late 2000s, Letts acted in many of the [[Steppenwolf Theatre Company]]'s productions, starring in [[Steve Martin]]'s ''[[Picasso at the Lapin Agile]]'' (1994).

In 2012, Letts gained attention for his Broadway debut performance in the revival of [[Edward Albee]]'s ''[[Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?]]'' at the [[Booth Theatre]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.playbill.com/article/whos-afraid-of-virginia-woolf-with-tracy-letts-and-amy-morton-will-arrive-on-broadway-in-september-com-193689|title=Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?, with Tracy Letts and Amy Morton, Will Arrive On Broadway in September|last=Gans|first=Andrew|date=May 17, 2012|website=Playbill|language=en|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref> He received positive reviews and won the [[Tony Award for Best Actor in a Play]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/06/09/theater/theaterspecial/whos-afraid-of-virginia-woolf-at-the-tonys.html|title='Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?' at the Tonys|last=Schuessler|first=Jennifer|date=2013-06-06|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-03-17|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>

In 2019, Letts appeared in the Broadway revival of [[Arthur Miller]]'s ''[[All My Sons]]'' with [[Annette Bening]] at [[Roundabout Theatre Company]]'s [[American Airlines Theatre]]. The show officially opened on April 22, 2019 and closed on June 23, 2019.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.broadway.com/shows/all-my-sons/|title=All My Sons|website=Broadway.com|language=en|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref>

==== Writing ====

Letts has written over ten plays.{{citation needed|date=March 2020}} His most famous, ''[[August: Osage County]]'', premiered at the Steppenwolf Theatre in Chicago on June 28, 2007. It had its Broadway debut at the [[Imperial Theatre]] on December 4, 2007; the production transferred to [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]]'s [[Music Box Theatre]] on April 29, 2008. The Broadway show closed on June 28, 2009, after 648 performances and 18 previews. The show went on to receive seven [[Tony Award]] nominations, winning six, including [[Tony Award for Best Play|Best Play]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.vulture.com/2008/06/the_tony_awards_complete_list.html|title=Tony Winners: 'In the Heights,' 'August: Osage County'|website=www.vulture.com|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref> The play won Letts the [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]] in 2008. Letts has also been a finalist for the Pulitzer drama prize for his plays ''[[Man from Nebraska]]'' and ''[[The Minutes (play)|The Minutes]]''; the Pulitzer committee described ''The Minutes'' as a "shocking drama set in a seemingly mundane city council meeting that acidly articulates a uniquely American toxicity that feels both historic and contemporary."<ref>[http://www.pulitzer.org/finalists/tracy-letts-0 "Finalists Tracy Letts"] pulitzer.org, retrieved April 16, 2018</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2018/04/16/business/media/pulitzer-prize-winners.html|title=2018 Pulitzer Prize Winners: Full List|access-date=2018-04-16|language=en}}</ref>

=== Television ===
Early in his acting career, in the 1990s through the mid 2000s, Letts acted in TV shows including ''[[Prison Break]]'', ''[[The District]]'', ''[[Strong Medicine]]'', ''[[Profiler (TV series)|Profiler]]'', ''[[Judging Amy]]'', ''[[The Drew Carey Show]]'', ''[[Seinfeld]]'', ''[[Early Edition]]'', and ''[[Home Improvement (TV series)|Home Improvement]]''.

In 2013–14, Letts joined the cast of [[Showtime (TV network)|Showtime]]'s ''[[Homeland (TV series)|Homeland]]'' as US Senator Andrew Lockhart. He was nominated with the rest of the cast for the [[Screen Actors Guild Award for Outstanding Performance by an Ensemble in a Drama Series|Screen Actors Guild Award for Best Ensemble.]]

In 2016, Letts joined [[HBO]]'s marital comedy-drama ''[[Divorce (TV series)|Divorce]]''.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://variety.com/2018/tv/news/divorce-renewed-season-3-hbo-1203018852/|title='Divorce' Renewed for Season 3 at HBO With New Showrunner, Smaller Episode Count|last=Otterson|first=Joe|date=2018-11-02|website=Variety|language=en|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref>

In 2018, Letts was cast in the second season of [[USA Network]]'s anthology crime drama series ''[[The Sinner (TV series)|The Sinner]]'', opposite [[Bill Pullman]] and [[Carrie Coon]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.indiewire.com/2018/05/the-sinner-season-2-photos-cast-tracy-letts-emmys-1201966068/|title='The Sinner' Season 2 Reveals First-Look Photos and Who's Joining Carrie Coon in the Mystery Crime Series|last=Nguyen|first=Hanh|date=2018-05-18|website=IndieWire|language=en|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref>

=== Film ===
==== Acting ====

Letts then appeared in the 2017 films ''[[The Lovers (2017 film)|The Lovers]]'', ''[[The Post (film)|The Post]]'',<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/lists/post-16-films-stars-real-life-inspirations-1054331/item/fritz-beebe-portrayed-by-tracy-letts-1066929|title=Fritz Beebe, portrayed by Tracy Letts - 'The Post': 16 of the Film's Stars and Their Real-Life Inspirations|website=The Hollywood Reporter|language=en|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref> and ''[[Lady Bird (film)|Lady Bird]]''.<ref>{{Citation|title=Lady Bird (2017)|url=https://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/lady_bird|work=Rotten Tomatoes|language=en|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.theringer.com/movies/2017/12/13/16772210/sag-awards-nominations-lady-bird-three-billboards|title=SAG Awards Nominations: 'Lady Bird' Cleans Up, 'Phantom Thread' Flops|last=Surrey|first=Miles|date=2017-12-13|website=The Ringer|access-date=2019-03-17}}</ref>

In 2019, Letts portrayed [[Henry Ford II]] in [[James Mangold]]'s sports drama film ''[[Ford v Ferrari]]'', and played Mr. Dashwood in ''[[Little Women (2019 film)|Little Women]]'', a film adaptation of [[Louisa May Alcott]]'s [[Little Women|novel of the same name]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://variety.com/2020/film/news/2020-oscar-nominations-list-academy-awards-nominees-1203461985/|title=Oscar Nominations 2020: The Complete List|date=2020-01-13|website=Variety|language=en|access-date=2020-01-13|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200113151810/https://variety.com/2020/film/news/2020-oscar-nominations-list-academy-awards-nominees-1203461985/|archive-date=January 13, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref>

==== Writing ====

Letts has written screenplays for three feature films based on his plays: ''[[Bug (2006 film)|Bug]]'' (directed by [[William Friedkin]]), ''[[Killer Joe (film)|Killer Joe]]'' (also directed by Friedkin); and ''[[August: Osage County (film)|August: Osage County]]'' (directed by [[John Wells (TV producer)|John Wells]]). He also wrote the screenplay for the 2021 [[Netflix]] feature film ''[[The Woman in the Window (2021 film)|The Woman in the Window]]'', starring [[Amy Adams]], based on the [[The Woman in the Window (novel)|eponymous psychological thriller]] by [[A. J. Finn|A.J. Finn]].
