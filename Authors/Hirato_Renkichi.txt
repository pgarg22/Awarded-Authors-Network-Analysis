{{Nihongo|'''Hirato Renkichi'''|平戸 廉吉||9 December 1893 – 20 July 1922<ref name="kotobank">{{cite web|title=Hirato Renkichi|url=https://kotobank.jp/word/%E5%B9%B3%E6%88%B8%E5%BB%89%E5%90%89-1104076|website=Kotobank|publisher=Asahi Shinbun|accessdate=8 December 2016|language=Japanese}}</ref>}} was a Japanese avant-garde poet, art critic, and translator who was active during the [[Taishō period]] of [[Japan]]. He was associated with Japanese [[futurism]]. 

==Biography==

Hirato Renkichi is the pen name of Kawahata Seiichi. He was born in what is now [[Takatsuki, Osaka]], Japan, in 1893. His father was a military sniper who left the family, leaving Hirato and his mother in poverty.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://www.poetryfoundation.org/harriet/2018/01/In-Conversation-Sho-Sugita-on-Translating-Hirato-Renkichi-the-Japanese-Avant-Garde|title=In Conversation: Sho Sugita on Translating Hirato Renkichi & the Japanese Avant-Garde by Corina Copp|date=2018-04-14|website=Poetry Foundation|language=en-us|others=Poetry Foundation|access-date=2018-04-14}}</ref> He attended [[Sophia University]] in Tokyo for three years before dropping out of school. He later studied at Gyosei Gakkō, a Catholic language school.

His first publication was in ''Bansō'' (Accompaniment), a literary journal that was edited by the poet and literary critic [[Ryuko Kawaji]].<ref>{{cite book|author1=Hirato Renkichi|editor1-last=Momota|editor1-first=Sōji|title=Zenshishū taisei, gendai Nihon shijin zenshū|date=1955|publisher=Sōgensha|location=Tokyo|page=364}}</ref> Kawaji was his mentor, and occasionally also helped Hirato financially.<ref name=":0" /> Hirato wrote poems and art criticism for coterie journals, including ''Gendai Shiika'' (Modern Poetry), ''Taimatsu'' (Torchlight), and the [[proletarian literature|proletariat journal]] ''[[Tane maku hito]]'' (Sower).<ref name="Hattori">{{cite journal|last1=Hattori|first1=Tetsuya|title=Hirato Renkichi sakuhin mokuroku|journal=Seikei Kokubun|date=1990|volume=23|pages=87–102}}</ref> His translations of [[Paul Fort]], [[Arthur Symons]], and [[Jean Cocteau]] appeared in various literary magazines.<ref name="Hattori" /> In 1921, 10 years after [[Filippo Tommaso Marinetti|Marinetti's]] "[[Manifesto of Futurism]]", he created flyers of “Nihon miraiha undo dai ikkai no sengen” (First Manifesto of Japanese Futurism) and handed them out in several locations across Tokyo.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Solt|first1=John|title=Shredding the Tapestry of Meaning: The Poetry and Poetics of Kitasono Katue (1902-1978)|date=1999|publisher=Harvard University Asia Center|location=Cambridge, MA|isbn=9780674807334|page=23}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|last1=Masahiko|first1=Ōishi|title=Higa tōi : Nihon modanizumu, Roshia avangyarudo|date=2009|publisher=Suiseisha|location=Tōkyō|isbn=9784891767228|page=20|edition=Dai 1-han.}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last1=Sas|first1=Miryam|title=Scent from the Future|url=http://www.cabinetmagazine.org/issues/13/sas.php|website=Cabinet Magazine|accessdate=16 November 2016}}</ref>

Hirato died of complications from a pulmonary disease on July 20, 1922.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://hyperallergic.com/372104/hirato-renkichi-spiral-staircase-collected-poems-ugly-duckling-presse-2017/|title=Hirato Renkichi: The Black Shadow-Man Illuminated|date=2017-04-16|work=Hyperallergic|access-date=2018-04-14|language=en-US}}</ref> A posthumous collection of his poems was published in 1931 by Kawaji Ryūkō, [[Kanbara Tai]], [[Hagiwara Kyōjiro]], and [[Yamazaki Yasuo]].
