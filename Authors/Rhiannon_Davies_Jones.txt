'''Rhiannon Davies Jones''' (4 November 1921 – 22 October 2014) was a Welsh historical novelist, lecturer and [[Welsh nationalism|Welsh nationalist]] who wrote in Welsh. Educated at [[Bangor University|University College Bangor]], she won two prizes for short novels, two Prose Medals at the [[National Eisteddfod of Wales]] and the crown at the 1973 Anglesey Eisteddfod. Jones published ten novels with her works covering fictional diaries, her political beliefs and responses to political events, and Welsh kings and princes.

==Biography==
===Early life and education===
Jones was born at Meirion House in [[Llanbedr]], [[Merionethshire|Meirioneth]] on 4 November 1921,<ref name="ODNBEntry">{{Cite ODNB|doi=10.1093/odnb/9780198614128.013.108038|title=Jones, (Sarah) Rhiannon Davies}}</ref><ref name="TranscelticEntry">{{cite web|last=Williams|first=Mair|title=Rhiannon Davies Jones: Welsh-language author of historical novels with a nationalist message|url=https://www.transceltic.com/blog/rhiannon-davies-jones-welsh-language-author-of-historical-novels-nationalist-message|publisher=Transceltic|date=29 October 2014|access-date=25 May 2020|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160912153515/http://transceltic.com/blog/rhiannon-davies-jones-welsh-language-author-of-historical-novels-nationalist-message|archive-date=12 September 2016}}</ref> to the former Baptist minister of Salem chapel Hugh Davies Jones and his wife, the teacher Laura Jones (née Owen).<ref name="DWBBio" /><ref name="BBCDeath">{{cite news|title=Marwolaeth y nofelydd Rhiannon Davies Jones|trans-title=Death of novelist Rhiannon Davies Jones|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/cymrufyw/29725484|publisher=[[BBC Cymru Fyw]]|language=Welsh|date=22 October 2014|access-date=25 May 2020|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180223103846/http://www.bbc.co.uk/cymrufyw/29725484|archive-date=23 February 2018}}</ref> She was the second child in the family after her elder sister Annie Davies Evans (née Jones).<ref name="DWBBio" /> After the death of Jones' father when she was two years old, the family moved from their home in [[Oswestry]], [[Shropshire]] and went to live with her maternal grandmother in [[Penbontrhydyfoethau|Penbont]], [[Llanbedr]].<ref name="ODNBEntry" /><ref name="DWBBio" /> 

She was educated at Llanbedr Primary School, Llanfair Primary School and Barmouth Grammar School, where the headmaster introduced her to the history of [[Christendom]]. In 1940, Jones moved on to [[Bangor University|University College Bangor]], where she met [[Ifor Williams]], [[Thomas Parry (author)|Thomas Parry]], and [[Robert Thomas Jenkins]]. She graduated with a [[School leaving qualification|Certificate of Education]] in 1945.<ref name=DWBBio>{{cite encyclopedia|last=Llewelyn|first=Haf|title=Jones, Sarah Rhiannon Davies (1921 – 2014), author and lecturer|url=https://biography.wales/article/s12-JONE-DAV-1921#?c=0&m=0&s=0&cv=30&manifest=https%3A%2F%2Fdamsssl.llgc.org.uk%2Fiiif%2F2.0%2F4635494%2Fmanifest.json&xywh=-93%2C685%2C4352%2C3580|encyclopedia=[[Dictionary of Welsh Biography]]|date=19 June 2019|access-date=25 May 2020|url-status=live|archive-date=3 November 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201103205359/https://biography.wales/article/s12-JONE-DAV-1921#?c=0&m=0&s=0&cv=30&manifest=https%3A%2F%2Fdamsssl.llgc.org.uk%2Fiiif%2F2.0%2F4635494%2Fmanifest.json&xywh=-93%2C685%2C4352%2C3580}}</ref>

===Career===
That same year, Jones was employed as a teacher of Welsh at Brynhyfryd Grammar School, [[Ruthin|Rhuthin]].<ref name="DWBBio" /><ref name="IndyObit">{{cite news|last=Stephens|first=Meic|title=Rhiannon Davies Jones: Welsh-language author whose impassioned historical novels carried a nationalist message|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/rhiannon-davies-jones-celebrated-welsh-language-author-whose-impassioned-historical-novels-carried-a-9824182.html|work=[[The Independent]]|date=28 October 2014|access-date=25 May 2020|url-status=live|archive-date=3 November 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201103205310/https://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/rhiannon-davies-jones-celebrated-welsh-language-author-whose-impassioned-historical-novels-carried-nationalist-message-9824182.html}}</ref> There, she began writing with the support of acquaintances with literary interests in Rhuthin. Parry advised Jones to choose between poetry and prose without mixing the two.<ref name="DWBBio" /> In 1963,<ref name="DWBBio" /> Jones was appointed to a lectureship at College of Education in [[Monmouthshire]],<ref name="IndyObit" /> before moving on to teach the same subject at [[Bangor Normal College]] two years later, remaining there until she retired in 1983.<ref name="DWBBio" />

She earned prizes for short novels that eventually went unpublished at the 1952 and 1956 editions of the [[National Eisteddfod of Wales]].<ref name="DWBBio" /> In 1960, Jones authored ''Fy Hen Lyfr Cownt'' (''<nowiki/>'My old account book'''), which is centred on a fictional diary about the final ten years of the hymn writer [[Ann Griffiths]].<ref name="TranscelticEntry" /><ref name="IndyObit" /> This won her the 1960 Prose Medal at the National Eisteddfod,<ref name="DWBBio" /> the first of her career,<ref name="BBCDeath" /><ref name="Golwg360">{{cite news|date=22 October 2014|title=Rhiannon Davies Jones wedi marw yn 92 oed|language=Welsh|trans-title=Rhiannon Davies Jones passed away at the age of 92|publisher=[[Golwg360]]|url=https://golwg360.cymru/newyddion/cymru/165729-rhiannon-davies-jones-wedi-marw-yn-92-oed|url-status=live|access-date=25 May 2020}}</ref> and the novel is credited by Meic Stephens of ''[[The Independent]]'' as having "gathered a momentum that it has maintained to the present day."<ref name="IndyObit" /> Jones won a second Prose Medal at the 1964 National Eisteddfod for the novel ''Lleian Llan Llŷr (The Nun of Llan Llŷr),'' which is focused on the grief she felt over the death of her partner.<ref name="DWBBio" /> This was followed by ''Llys Aberffraw'' (''The Court of Aberffraw''), a novel about [[Owain Gwynedd]]'s illegitimate granddaughters. As a [[Welsh nationalism|Welsh nationalist]], Jones was inspired to write the novel after learning of the [[Investiture of the Prince of Wales#Investiture of Prince Charles|Investiture of Prince Charles as Prince of Wales]] and the deaths of two young nationalists who were killed by their own bomb in [[Abergele]] on the eve of the investiture.<ref name="IndyObit" /> The novel won the crown at the 1973 Anglesey Eisteddfod and was published four years later.<ref name="DWBBio" />

In 1981, ''Eryr Pengwern'' ('''The eagle of Pengwern''<nowiki/>'), set in 7th-century Powys and based on the Heledd Saga,<ref name="IndyObit" /> was written by Jones in response to [[Gwynfor Evans]]' threat to launch a hunger strike for [[S4C|a Welsh television channel]].<ref name="DWBBio" /> Four years later, she moved to journal form with the novel in ''Dyddiadur Mari Gwyn'' (''Mari Gwyn’s Diary'') dealing with the persecution of Catholics in [[Elizabeth I]]'s reign and focusing on the Elizabethan Welsh writer Robert Gwyn.<ref name="DWBBio" /><ref name="IndyObit" /> This was followed by Jones authoring a trilogy of novels between 1987 and 1993 which were set in the Age of the Princes: ''Cribau Eryri'' (''The Ridges of Snowdonia''), ''Barrug y Bore'' (''Morning Frost'') and ''Adar Drycin'' (''Storm Birds'').<ref name="IndyObit" /> She was inspired to write the trilogy after the failed [[1979 Welsh devolution referendum]],<ref name="DWBBio" /> and an increase of demographic pressure in the Welsh language central areas.<ref name="ODNBEntry" /> Jones' final novel ''Cydio Mewn Cwilsyn (Taking up a Quill''), a factious diary of [[Edmund Prys]]' daughter, was published in 2002.<ref name="TranscelticEntry" /><ref name="BBCDeath" /><ref name="IndyObit" /> She wrote ten novels over her lifetime and published a collection of original children's nursery rhymes.<ref name="BBCDeath" /><ref name="Golwg360" /> 

===Death===
Jones died on 22 October 2014, in Penrhos Stanley Hospital, [[Holyhead]], [[Anglesey]],<ref name="DWBBio" /><ref name="IndyObit" /> after a long illness from [[pneumonia]] and a broken [[femur]].<ref name="ODNBEntry" /><ref name="Golwg360" /> She was unmarried. Jones was given a funeral service at Penuel Baptist Chapel in Bangor and was buried at Salem Baptist Chapel in [[Llansilin]], Denbighshire on 30 October.<ref name="ODNBEntry" />

==Analysis and legacy==
In the book ''Rebirth of a Nation: Wales, 1880-1980'', author Kenneth O. Morgan wrote, "the historical novel flourished" with authors such as Jones.<ref name="Wales18801980">{{cite book|editor-last=Williams|editor-first=Glannor|last=O. Morgan|first=Kenneth|title=Rebirth of a Nation: Wales, 1880–1980|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=2DB2EHJ08aIC&q=Rhiannon+Davies+Jones&pg=PA363|publisher=[[Oxford University Press]]|year=1981|page=363|isbn=978-0-19-821736-7}}</ref> Haf Llewelyn, writing in her entry in the ''[[Dictionary of Welsh Biography]]'', noted Jones' "passionate interest in her subjects is evident from the detailed research"  was "the hallmark of her work" and "her beliefs and responses to political events of the period are clearly reflected in her work"<ref name="DWBBio" /> Mair Williams of Transceltic said her works featured historical accuracies to "impart powerful nationalist ideals."<ref name="TranscelticEntry" /> Jones was added to the [[Dictionary of National Biography|''Oxford Dictionary of National Biography'']] in 2018.<ref>{{cite news|title=Literary duo included in book of notable people|url=http://www.cambrian-news.co.uk/article.cfm?id=119699&headline=Literary%20duo%20included%20in%20book%20of%20notable%20people&sectionIs=news&searchyear=2018|work=[[Cambrian News]]|date=16 March 2018|access-date=25 May 2020|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180316134126/http://www.cambrian-news.co.uk/article.cfm?id=119699&headline=Literary%20duo%20included%20in%20book%20of%20notable%20people&sectionIs=news&searchyear=2018|archive-date=16 March 2018}}</ref>
