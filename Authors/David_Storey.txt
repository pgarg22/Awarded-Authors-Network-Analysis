'''David Malcolm Storey''' (13 July 1933 – 27 March 2017) was an English [[playwright]], [[screenwriter]], award-winning [[novelist]] and a professional [[rugby league]] player.  He won the [[Booker Prize]] in 1976 for his novel ''[[Saville (novel)|Saville]]''.  He also won the MacMillan Fiction Award for ''[[This Sporting Life (novel)|This Sporting Life]]'' in 1960.

==Early life and career==
Storey was born on 13 July 1933 in [[Wakefield]], [[West Riding of Yorkshire]],<ref name="theguardian"/> the son of a coal miner, Frank Richmond Story, and Lily (née Cartwright) Story.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://search.findmypast.co.uk/results/world-records/england-and-wales-births-1837-2006?lastname=story&eventyear=1933&eventyear_offset=0&mothersmaidenname=cartwright |publisher = Find My Past | title = Cartwright|access-date=29 March 2017}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |url = http://search.findmypast.com/results/world-records/england-and-wales-marriages-1837-2005?firstname=frank%20r&lastname=story&spouse1surname=cartwright|publisher = Find My Past | title = Cartwright|access-date=29 March 2017}}</ref><ref name="Encyclopedia of British Writers, 1800 to the Present">{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=T7pVTz46T3cC&pg=PA469|title=Encyclopedia of British Writers, 1800 to the Present|last1=Stade|first1=George|last2=Karbiener|first2=Karen|publisher=Infobase Publishing|year=2010|isbn=9781438116891|volume=2|page=469}}</ref><ref name="Guardian">{{cite news|first=Frank |last=Keating |url=https://www.theguardian.com/sport/blog/2010/dec/01/this-sporting-life-david-storey |title=David Storey's yarn hits 50 and is still top of the league|work=The Guardian|date=1 December 2010|access-date= 8 January 2016}}</ref> He was educated at [[Queen Elizabeth Grammar School, Wakefield|QEGS Wakefield]].<ref name="BCouncil">{{cite web|url=https://literature.britishcouncil.org/writer/david-storey |title=British Council:Literature|access-date= 8 January 2016}}</ref> He continued his education at London's [[Slade School of Fine Art]],<ref name="Guardian"/><ref name="BCouncil"/> and supported himself there by playing [[rugby league]] for [[Leeds Rhinos|Leeds RLFC]] as a {{rlp|hb}} for the "A" team, with occasional appearances in the first.<ref name="Guardian"/>

His plays include ''The Restoration of Arnold Middleton'', ''[[The Changing Room]]'', ''Cromwell'', ''[[Home (play)|Home]]'', and ''Stages''.<ref name="BCouncil"/> His novels include ''[[Flight into Camden]]'', which won the 1961 [[John Llewellyn Rhys Prize]] and the 1963 [[Somerset Maugham Award]]; and ''[[Saville (novel)|Saville]]'', which won the 1976 [[Booker Prize]].<ref name="theguardian"/>

He wrote the screenplay for ''[[This Sporting Life]]'' (1963), directed by [[Lindsay Anderson]], adapted from his first [[This Sporting Life (novel)|novel of the same name]], originally published in 1960,<ref name="David Storey dies at 83"/> which won the 1960 Macmillan Fiction Award.<ref name="The Plays of David Storey: A Thematic Study">{{Cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=8gBAoExNEUUC&pg=PA8 |title=The Plays of David Storey: A Thematic Study |last=Hutchings |first=William |publisher=SIU Press |year=1988 |isbn=9780809314614 |page=8}}</ref> The film was the beginning of a long professional association with Anderson, whose film version of Storey's play ''[[In Celebration (play)|In Celebration]]'' was released as part of the [[American Film Theatre]] series in 1975. ''[[Home (play)|Home]]'' and ''Early Days'' (both starred Sir [[Ralph Richardson]]; ''Home'' starred Sir [[John Gielgud]]) were made into television films.<ref name="bbc"/>

Storey's novel ''Pasmore'' was shortlisted for the [[Booker Prize]].<ref name="nytimes"/>

[[National Life Stories]] conducted an oral history interview (C464/67) with David Storey in 2008-2009 for its National Life Stories General collection held by the British Library.<ref name=oralhistory>[http://sounds.bl.uk/Oral-history/Art/021M-C0464X0067XX-0001V0 National Life Stories, Storey, David  (1 of 12). National Life Story Collection: General, The British Library Board, 2009].  Retrieved 1 February 2018</ref>

==Personal life and death==
[[File:Grave of David Storey in Highgate Cemetery.jpg|thumb|Grave of David Storey in [[Highgate Cemetery]]]]
In 1956, Storey married Barbara Rudd Hamilton, with whom he had four children.<ref name="A Reader's Guide To Modern British Drama">{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=LRNpGm7Ceg8C&pg=PA167 |title=A Reader's Guide To Modern British Drama|last=Sternlicht|first=Sanford V.|publisher=Syracuse University Press|year=2004|isbn=9780815630760|page=167}}</ref> Barbara Storey died in 2015.<ref name="theguardian">{{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2017/mar/27/david-storey-obituary|title=David Storey obituary|work=The Guardian|date=27 March 2017|access-date=27 March 2017}}</ref>

Storey died on 27 March 2017 in London at the age of 83 and was buried on the eastern side of [[Highgate Cemetery]].<ref name="bbc">{{cite news|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/entertainment-arts-39404214 |title=David Storey: Booker Prize-winning author dies at 83|work=[[BBC News]]|date= 27 March 2017|access-date= 27 March 2017}}</ref><ref name="David Storey dies at 83">{{cite news |url=https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2017/mar/27/david-storey-author-of-this-sporting-life-dies-aged-83|title=David Storey, author of This Sporting Life, dies at 83 |work=The Guardian|date=27 March 2017|access-date=27 March 2017}}</ref> The cause was [[Parkinson's disease]] and [[dementia]].<ref name="nytimes">{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/03/27/theater/david-storey-dead-british-novelest-playwright.html|title=David Storey, British Novelist and Playwright, Dies at 83|author=Benedict Nightingale|access-date=28 March 2017|date=27 March 2017|work=[[The New York Times]]}}</ref> Survivors include his two sons, Jake and Sean; two daughters, [[Helen Storey|Helen]] and [[Kate Storey|Kate]]; a brother, Anthony; and six grandchildren.<ref name="theguardian"/><ref name="nytimes"/>
