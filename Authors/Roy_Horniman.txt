'''Roy Horniman''' (1874&ndash;1930) was a British writer, best known for his novel ''Israel Rank: The Autobiography of a Criminal'', which inspired several adaptations.<ref>{{cite journal|title=Rank Outsider|journal=Portsmouth Grammar School Magazine|url=https://www.pgs.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2013/02/website-Roy-Horniman-by-J-Sadden.pdf|pages=21–23|access-date=3 August 2020}}</ref>

Horniman was born in [[Southsea]]. His father, William Horniman, was Paymaster-in-Chief of the British [[Royal Navy]], and his mother was Greek.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://archiveshub.jisc.ac.uk/search/archives/ee7200c5-df75-30bf-9dd6-d99986765340|title=Papers of Roy Horniman|website=Archives Hub|access-date=3 August 2020}}</ref>

He was the owner of ''The Ladies' Review'' for some years and was a member of the British Committee of The [[Indian National Congress]]. As well as acting he became tenant and manager of the [[Criterion Theatre]] and wrote many plays as well as adaptations of his own and others’ novels. In his later years he wrote and adapted for the screen. Amongst his notable works were ''Israel Rank: The Autobiography of a Criminal'' (1907), which was republished by [[Faber Finds]] in 2008 and again by Cavalier Classics in 2014, and by Dean Street Press in 2020. The 1949 film ''[[Kind Hearts and Coronets]]'' was based on ''Israel Rank: The Autobiography of a Criminal'' and the novel also inspired the 2013 Broadway musical ''[[A Gentleman's Guide to Love and Murder]]''.  Horniman also wrote ''The Sin of Atlantis'' in 1900 and ''Lord Cammarleigh’s Secret: A Fairy Story of To-Day'' in 1907.<ref>{{cite journal|title=Review of ''Lord Cammarleigh's Secret'' by Roy Horniman|journal=The Athenaeum|issue=4175|date=November 2, 1907|pages=546–547|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=__I_PcOFSw8C&pg=PA546}}</ref>

Roy Horniman served in the [[Artists Rifles]] during the [[First World War]].
