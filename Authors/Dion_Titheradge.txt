'''Dion Titheradge''' (30 March 1889 – 16 November 1934) was an Australian-born actor and writer of revues, plays and screenplays.

==Early life==
Dion Titheradge was born in [[Melbourne]], Australia in 1889, son of the actor [[George Sutton Titheradge]]. He was brother of the actress [[Madge Titheradge]].<ref name=nla>[http://trove.nla.gov.au/newspaper/article/10994534 Death of Mr. Dion Titheradge] [[The Argus (Melbourne)]], 19 November 1934. Via [[National Library of Australia]].</ref>
[[File:Dion Titheradge, Laurette Taylor, and Philip Merivale in &quot;The Harp of Life&quot;.jpg|thumb|left|Titheradge (left) with [[Laurette Taylor]] and [[Philip Merivale]] in ''The Harp of Life'']]

His early career was as an actor. His first appearance on stage was in 1908 at the [[Theatre Royal, Newcastle|Theatre Royal]], Newcastle-on-Tyne, in ''The Woman of Kronstadt'', and he then toured Australia and New Zealand playing various roles. In 1910 he was a member of [[Lewis Waller]]'s company, touring with him in the play ''Bardelys the Magnificent''.<ref name=nla/>

Titheradge appeared with Waller in September–October 1912 at Daly's Theatre, New York, in ''[[Henry V (play)|Henry V]]''. Also in New York, he played Harry Anson in ''[[The Whip (play)|The Whip]]'' at the [[Manhattan Opera House]] from November 1912 to April 1913; he appeared in ''Life'' by [[Thompson Buchanan]] at the same theatre from October 1914 to March 1915, and in ''The Harp of Life'' by [[J. Hartley Manners]] at the [[Globe Theatre (Broadway)|Globe Theatre]] from November 1916 to May 1917.<ref name=nla/><ref name=ibdb>[https://www.ibdb.com/broadway-cast-staff/dion-titheradge-5054 Dion Titheradge] at the [[Internet Broadway Database]]</ref>

==Writer==
Writing became important in his subsequent career. Titheradge and [[Kenneth Duffield]], of South Australia, created a successful [[West End theatre|West End]] [[revue]] in London in 1920 and, before Duffield returned to Australia in 1922, they produced several more revues including ''A to Z'', ''Puss Puss'', ''Snap'' and ''Pot Luck''.<ref name=nla/><ref name=ozvta>[https://ozvta.com/practitioners-other-m-z/ Dion Titheradge] Australian Variety Theatre Archive, accessed 11 September 2016.</ref>

The song "And Her Mother Came Too", of which he wrote the lyrics, became famous; the music was by [[Ivor Novello]], and it was first heard in ''A to Z'', which opened at the [[Prince of Wales Theatre]] on 11 October 1921. It was sung by [[Jack Buchanan]].<ref>[https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O1295270/and-her-mother-came-too-sheet-music-novello-ivor/ "And her Mother came too"] V & A Museum, accessed 13 September 2016.</ref><ref>[http://rpo.library.utoronto.ca/poems/and-her-mother-came-too And her Mother came too] Representative Poetry Online, accessed 22 September 2016.</ref> Also famous was his sketch "Dinner Napkins" (also known as "Double Damask"), heard in [[Noel Gay]]'s show ''Clowns in Clover'' (1927).<ref>[http://www.ovrtur.com/song/100000465 "Dinner Napkins" (sketch)] Ovrtur, accessed 13 September 2016.</ref>

Titheradge wrote many plays.<ref name=nla/> His play ''Loose Ends'' was first seen at the [[Duke of York's Theatre]] in London from 19 April 1926; it ran for 81 performances, transferring to the [[Original Shaftesbury Theatre|Shaftesbury Theatre]] from 7 June. He directed and appeared in the play in New York; it ran for 40 performances from 1 November 1926 at the [[Walter Kerr Theatre|Ritz Theatre]]. It was later (1930) made into a film of the same name.<ref name=ibdb/><ref>J. P. Waring, ''The London Stage 1920–1929''. Rowman & Littlefield 2014. Page 433.</ref><ref name=imdbdion>{{IMDB name|id=0864451|name=Dion Titheradge}}</ref> His play ''The Crooked Billet'', a murder mystery, ran for 168 performances from 13 October 1927 at the [[Royalty Theatre]] in London, and was made into a [[The Crooked Billet|film of the same name]] (1929).<ref name=imdbdion/><ref>J. P. Waring, ''The London Stage 1920–1929''. Rowman & Littlefield 2014. Page 542.</ref>

He wrote the [[screenplay]]s for the films ''[[Mr. Bill the Conqueror]]'' (1932) and ''[[Dangerous Ground (1934 film)|Dangerous Ground]]'' (1934).<ref name=imdbdion/>

==Family==
Titheradge married Margaret Ann Bolton in 1909 in New South Wales; their children were Meg and the writer Peter Dion Titheradge (1910–1989).<ref>[https://titheradgefamilyhistory.wordpress.com/dion-titheradge-famous-actor-and-playwright/ Dion Titheradge] Titheradge family history, accessed 22 September 2016.</ref><ref name=imdbpeter>{{IMDB name|id=1548867|name=Peter Dion Titheradge}}</ref> They divorced in 1927, and he married the actress [[Madge Stuart]] in 1928.<ref name=imdbdion/>

He died in London in 1934.<ref name=nla/>
