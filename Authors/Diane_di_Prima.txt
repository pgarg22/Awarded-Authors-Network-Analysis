'''Diane di Prima''' (August 6, 1934{{spnd}}October 25, 2020) was an American [[poet]], known for her association with the [[Beat Generation|Beat movement]]. She was also an artist, prose writer, and teacher. Her magnum opus is widely considered to be ''Loba'', a collection of poems first published in 1978 then extended in 1998.  

==Early life and education==
Di Prima was born in [[Brooklyn]], New York, on August 6, 1934.<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|last=Genzlinger|first=Neil|date=2020-10-28|title=Diane di Prima, Poet of the Beat Era and Beyond, Dies at 86|language=en-US|work=[[The New York Times]]|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2020/10/28/books/diane-di-prima-dead.html|url-status=live|access-date=2020-10-29|issn=0362-4331|archive-date=October 29, 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201029005820/https://www.nytimes.com/2020/10/28/books/diane-di-prima-dead.html}}</ref> She was a second generation [[Italian American|American of Italian descent]]. Her father Francis was a lawyer, and her mother Emma (née Mallozzi) was a teacher.<ref name=":0" /> Her maternal grandfather, Domenico Mallozzi, was an activist and associated with [[anarchy|anarchists]] [[Carlo Tresca]] and [[Emma Goldman]].<ref name=Bio>{{cite web|title=BIOGRAPHY OF DIANE DI PRIMA|url=http://dianediprima.com/bio.html|website=Diane di Prima|access-date=23 April 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120717001957/http://dianediprima.com/bio.html|archive-date=17 July 2012}}</ref> Di Prima changed her last name from DiPrima to di Prima because she believed it better reflected her Italian ancestry.<ref name=":0" /> 

She attended [[Hunter College High School]] and [[Swarthmore College]] before dropping out to be a poet in [[Manhattan]].<ref name=":0" /> Di Prima began writing as a child and by the age of 19 was corresponding with [[Ezra Pound]] and [[Kenneth Patchen]]. Her first book of poetry, ''This Kind of Bird Flies Backward'', was published in 1958 by [[Hettie Jones]] and [[LeRoi Jones]]' Totem Press.

== Career ==

===Involvement with the Beats===
Di Prima spent the late 1950s and early 1960s in [[Manhattan]], where she participated in the emerging [[Beat generation|Beat movement]].<ref name=":1" /> She spent some time in [[California]] at [[Stinson Beach]] and [[Topanga Canyon]], returned to [[New York City]], and eventually moved to [[San Francisco]] permanently.

She edited the newspaper ''The Floating Bear'' with [[Amiri Baraka|Amiri Baraka (LeRoi Jones)]]<ref>{{Cite book|last1=Hathaway|first1=Heather|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=vYZ5UddwfnwC&pg=PT259|title=Race and the Modern Artist|last2=Jarab|first2=Josef|last3=Melnick|first3=Jeffrey|date=2003-01-16|publisher=Oxford University Press|isbn=978-0-19-535262-7|pages=245|language=en}}</ref> and was co-founder of the [[New York Poets Theatre]] and founder of the Poets Press. On several occasions she faced charges of obscenity by the United States government due to her work with the New York Poets Theatre and ''The Floating Bear''. In 1961 she was arrested by the [[Federal Bureau of Investigation]] (FBI) for publishing two poems in ''The Floating Bear''.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Diane di Prima|url=https://poets.org/poet/diane-di-prima|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200506184613/https://poets.org/poet/diane-di-prima|archive-date=May 6, 2020|website=poets.org}}</ref><ref name=":2">{{Cite web|last=Limbong|first=Andrew|date=2020-10-27|title=Diane di Prima, Beat Poet And Activist, Dead At 86|url=https://www.npr.org/2020/10/27/927948704/diane-di-prima-beat-poet-and-activist-dead-at-86|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201028015532/https://www.npr.org/2020/10/27/927948704/diane-di-prima-beat-poet-and-activist-dead-at-86|archive-date=October 28, 2020|access-date=2020-10-29|website=[[NPR]]|language=en}}</ref> According to di Prima, police persistently harassed her due to the nature of her poetry.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://doddcenter.uconn.edu/asc/findaids/Diprima/MSS19910042.html|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121105031316/http://doddcenter.uconn.edu/asc/findaids/Diprima/MSS19910042.html|url-status=dead|title=Diane Di Prima Papers, Archives & Special Collections at the Thomas J. Dodd Research Center.|archive-date=Nov 5, 2012|access-date=Oct 28, 2020}}</ref> In 1966, she spent some time at [[Timothy Leary#Millbrook and psychedelic counterculture (1963–1967)|Millbrook]] with [[Timothy Leary]]'s [[psychedelia|psychedelic]] community.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Langer|first=Emily|date=2020-10-26|title=Diane di Prima, feminist poet of the Beat Generation, dies at 86|work=[[Seattle Times]]|url=https://www.seattletimes.com/nation-world/diane-di-prima-feminist-poet-of-the-beat-generation-dies-at-86/|access-date=2020-10-28}}</ref>

From 1974 to 1997, di Prima taught poetry at the [[Jack Kerouac School of Disembodied Poetics]],<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|last=Associated Press|author-link=Associated Press|date=2020-10-28|title=Diane di Prima, Beat poet and activist, dead at 86|url=https://abcnews.go.com/Entertainment/wireStory/diane-di-prima-beat-poet-activist-dead-86-73888949|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201029013248/https://abcnews.go.com/Entertainment/wireStory/diane-di-prima-beat-poet-activist-dead-86-73888949|archive-date=October 29, 2020|access-date=2020-10-29|website=[[ABC News]]|language=en}}</ref> of the [[Naropa Institute]] in [[Boulder, Colorado]], sharing the program with fellow Beats [[Allen Ginsberg]] and [[Anne Waldman]] (co-founders of the program), [[William Burroughs]], [[Gregory Corso]], and others.

===Later career===
In the late 1960s, di Prima moved permanently to California. There, she became involved with the [[Diggers (theater)|Diggers]] and studied [[Buddhism]], [[Sanskrit]], [[Gnosticism]], and [[alchemy]]. In 1966, she signed a vow of [[tax resistance]] to the [[Vietnam War]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://triptych.brynmawr.edu/cdm4/item_viewer.php |archive-url=https://archive.today/20120715150745/http://triptych.brynmawr.edu/cdm4/item_viewer.php?CISOROOT=%2FSC_Ephemera&CISOPTR=158 |url-status=dead |title=triptych &#124; tri-college digital library: Item Viewer |access-date=October 28, 2020 |archive-date=July 15, 2012}}</ref> In the 1970s, she published the collection ''Revolutionary Letters'', influenced by her time with the Diggers.<ref name=":2" /> At [[The Band|The Band's]] famous [[The Last Waltz|Last Waltz]] concert in 1976, she read aloud from Revolutionary Letters and the one-line poem "Get Yer Cut Throat Off My Knife".<ref name=":2" /> 

She published her major work, the long poem ''Loba'', in 1978, with an enlarged edition in 1998.  From the 1960s on she worked as a photographer and a [[collage]] artist, and in the last decade or so of her life she took up [[watercolor painting]].{{Citation needed|date=October 2021}}

From 1980 to 1987, di Prima taught [[Hermeticism|Hermetic]] and [[esotericism|esoteric]] traditions in poetry, in a short-lived but significant Masters-in-Poetics program at [[New College of California]],<ref>{{cite web |last=Barmann |first=Jay |date=October 27, 2020 |title=Diane di Prima, Noted Female Voice in the Beat Generation Boys' Club, Dies at 86 |url=https://sfist.com/2020/10/27/diane-di-prima-noted-female-voice-in-the-beat-generation-boys-club-dies-at/ |access-date=October 29, 2020 |website=SFist|language=en}}</ref> which she established together with poets [[Robert Duncan (poet)|Robert Duncan]] and [[David Meltzer (poet)|David Meltzer]]. She has also taught at the [[San Francisco Art Institute]]. She was one of the co-founders of San Francisco Institute of Magical and Healing Arts (SIMHA), where she taught Western spiritual traditions from 1983 to 1992.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Meltzer|first=David|date=2020|title=Di Prima, Diane|url=https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/culture-magazines/di-prima-diane|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201029013220/https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/culture-magazines/di-prima-diane|archive-date=October 29, 2020|access-date=2020-10-28|website=Contemporary Poets}}</ref>

In 2009, di Prima became San Francisco's poet laureate.<ref name=":0" />

==Activism==
Di Prima was known for her activism, having been exposed early on to political consciousness by her grandfather, Domenico, as detailed in her memoir ''Recollections of My Life as a Woman''; she also discusses this in a 2001 interview with David Hadbawnik.<ref name="auto">{{Cite web|url=http://jacketmagazine.com/18/diprima-iv.html|title=Jacket 18 - Diane di Prima in conversation with David Hadbawnik, August 2001|website=jacketmagazine.com|access-date=Oct 28, 2020|archive-date=July 21, 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200721115141/http://jacketmagazine.com/18/diprima-iv.html|url-status=live}}</ref> In her memoir, di Prima describes seeing her grandfather speak at a rally in the park, writing: "I am proud of him, and afraid, but mostly amazed. His words have awakened my full acknowledgment, consent. I hear what he says as truth, and it seems I have always known it. I feel old, self-contained, passionate with the pure passion of a child."<ref>''Recollections of My Life as a Woman'', pp. 13–14.</ref> Moments such as these sparked a dedication to social activism, especially as it concerned women's rights, that persisted throughout di Prima's life.

== Death and legacy ==
Di Prima died on October 25, 2020, at San Francisco General Hospital. She was 86 years old.<ref>{{cite news|last=Genzlinger|first=Neil|date=October 28, 2020|title=Diane di Prima, Poet of the Beat Era and Beyond, Dies at 86|work=[[The New York Times]]|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2020/10/28/books/diane-di-prima-dead.html|access-date=March 21, 2021}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=Diane di Prima Papers, University of Louisville Archives & Special Collections.|url=http://louisville.edu/library/archives/findingaids/diprima.html|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20091204233803/http://louisville.edu/library/archives/findingaids/diprima.html|archive-date=December 4, 2009|access-date=November 12, 2012}}</ref> She was battling several health issues such as [[Parkinson's disease]] and [[Sjogren's Syndrome]]. However, she did not suffer any cognitive impediment and was working on several books until two weeks prior to her death.<ref>di Prima, Dominique. “Instagram Post .” 

''Instagram'', 27 Oct. 2020, www.instagram.com/p/CG02jx4BrF2/?utm_medium=copy_link. </ref>

Di Prima's works are held at the [[University of Louisville]], [[Indiana University]], [[Southern Illinois University]], and the [[University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill]].<ref>{{Cite web|last=Foundation|first=Poetry|date=Oct 27, 2020|title=Diane di Prima|url=https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/diane-di-prima|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201005092005/https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/diane-di-prima|archive-date=October 5, 2020|access-date=Oct 28, 2020|website=Poetry Foundation}}</ref>
