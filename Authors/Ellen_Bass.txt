'''Ellen Bass''' (born June 16, 1947 in [[Philadelphia]]) is an [[United States|American]] [[poet]] and co-author of ''[[The Courage to Heal]]''.

==Life==
Bass grew up in [[Pleasantville, New Jersey]], where her parents owned a liquor store. Her family later moved to [[Ventnor City, New Jersey]].{{Citation needed|date=December 2017}} She attended [[Goucher College]], where she graduated magna cum laude in 1968 with a bachelor's degree. She pursued a master's degree in creative writing at [[Boston University]], where she studied with [[Anne Sexton]], and graduated in 1970. From 1970 to 1974, Bass worked at Project Place, a social service center in Boston.<ref name="pf">{{cite web |website=Poetry Foundation |title=Ellen Bass |date=25 May 2021 |url=https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/ellen-bass}}</ref><ref name="enotes">{{cite web |website=ENotes.com |title=Ellen Bass Biography |url=https://www.enotes.com/topics/ellen-bass}}</ref>

From 1983 to 2003, she worked in the field of healing from childhood sexual abuse: writing the best-selling ''[[The Courage to Heal]]'', developing training seminars for professionals, offering workshops for survivors, and lecturing to mental health professionals nationally and internationally. She is a co-founder of the Survivors Healing Center in Santa Cruz, a non-profit organization offering services to survivors of child sexual abuse.

Bass has taught poetry and creative writing for the [[Low-residency program|low-residency]] [[Master of Fine Arts]] program at [[Pacific University]] in Oregon since 2007.<ref name="enotes" /><ref name="aap" /> She has taught workshops in [[Santa Cruz, California]]<ref name="rumpus">{{cite web |website=The Rumpus |title=In Plain Sight: The Vanishing of Ellen Bass |date=5 January 2016 |url=http://therumpus.net/2016/01/in-plain-sight-the-vanishing-of-ellen-bass/}}</ref> since 1974 and also nationally.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.ellenbass.com/readings-workshops/|title=Events|last=admin|website=Ellen Bass|language=en-US|access-date=2020-01-14}}</ref>

Among Bass' previous books are ''Like a Beggar'' (2014), which was a finalist for the Paterson Poetry Prize, the Publishing Triangle Award, the Milt Kessler Poetry Award, the Lambda Literary Award, and the Northern California Book Award, ''The Human Line'' (2007), and ''[https://www.boaeditions.org/collections/poetry/products/mules-of-love?_pos=1&_sid=0dd5410ad&_ss=r Mules of Love]'' (2002), which won the Lambda Literary Award. Her poems have been published widely in journals and anthologies, including the ''[[The New Yorker|New Yorker]]'',<ref>{{cite web |website=The New Yorker |title=Contributors: Ellen Bass |url=https://www.newyorker.com/contributors/ellen-bass}}</ref> the ''[[American Poetry Review]]'', the ''[[Kenyon Review]]'', and ''[[Ploughshares]]''.<ref name="pshares">{{cite web |website=Ploughshares |title=Ellen Bass |url=https://www.pshares.org/authors/ellen-bass}}</ref>

Her nonfiction books include ''I Never Told Anyone: Writings by Women Survivors of Child Sexual Abuse'' (HarperCollins, 1983), ''Free Your Mind: The Book for Gay, Lesbian and Bisexual Youth and Their Allies'' (HarperCollins, 1996), and ''[[The Courage to Heal]]: A Guide for Women Survivors of Child Sexual Abuse'' (HarperCollins, 1988, 2008), which has been translated into twelve languages.<ref name="pshares" />

In 2013, she founded the Poetry Program at the Salinas Valley State Prison, which offers a weekly workshop to incarcerated men. In 2014, she also founded the Santa Cruz Poetry Project, which offers six weekly workshops to men and women incarcerated in the Santa Cruz County jails.

In 2017, Bass was elected as a Chancellor of the [[Academy of American Poets]].<ref name="aap">{{cite web |website=Academy of American Poets |title=Ellen Bass |url=https://www.poets.org/poetsorg/poet/ellen-bass}}</ref>

Bass was named the Santa Cruz County Artist of the Year in 2019.

Bass lives in [[Santa Cruz, California]] with her wife, Janet Bryer. She has two children, Saraswati Bryer-Bass and Max Bryer-Bass.

==Awards==
Bass was awarded the Elliston Book Award for Poetry from the [[University of Cincinnati]], ''Nimrod''/Hardman's Pablo Neruda Prize, ''The Missouri Review’s'' Larry Levis Award, the Greensboro Poetry Prize, the ''New Letters'' Poetry Prize, the Chautauqua Poetry Prize, three [[Pushcart Prize]]s (2003, 2015, 2017), Fellowships from The Guggenheim Foundation, The [[National Endowment for the Arts]], and The [[California Arts Council]].<ref name="pf" /><ref name="purdy">{{cite web |website=Eclectica |last1=Purdy |first1=Gilbert Wesley |title=Review of ''The Human Line'' by Ellen Bass |url=http://www.eclectica.org/v11n4/purdy_bass.html}}</ref>

''Like a Beggar'' (Copper Canyon Press, 2014) was a finalist for the Paterson Poetry Prize, the Publishing Triangle Award, the Milt Kessler Poetry Award, the Lambda Literary Award, and the Northern California Book Award. ''The Human Line'' ([[Copper Canyon Press]], 2007) was named among the notable books of 2007 in the poetry section by the ''San Francisco Chronicle'',<ref name="pshares" /> and ''Mules of Love'' (BOA Editions, 2002) won the [[15th Lambda Literary Awards|2002 Lambda Literary Award]].<ref name="purdy" />
