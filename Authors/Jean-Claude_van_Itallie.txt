'''Jean-Claude van Itallie''' (May 25, 1936 – September 9, 2021) was a Belgian-born American [[playwright]], performer, and theatre workshop teacher.<ref name="biography">{{Cite web|url=http://speccoll.library.kent.edu/theater/vanitallie/index.html|title=Jean-Claude van Itallie papers|website=Kent State University Libraries|language=en|access-date=23 July 2018}}</ref> He is best known for his 1966 [[Opposition to United States involvement in the Vietnam War|anti-Vietnam War]] play ''[[America Hurrah]];'' ''The Serpent'', an ensemble play he wrote with [[Joseph Chaikin]]'s [[The Open Theatre|Open Theatre]]; his [[theatrical adaptation]] of the ''[[Tibetan Book of the Dead]]''; and his translations of [[Anton Chekhov]]'s plays.<ref name="official">{{Cite web|url=http://www.vanitallie.com/|title=Jean-Claude van Itallie: Broadway Playwright, Author, Acting Technique|website=Jean-Claude van Itallie|language=en|access-date=23 July 2018}}</ref><ref name="Autobiography">{{cite journal|last=van Itallie|first=Jean-Claude|year=2013|title=Jean-Claude van Itallie: Autobiography|journal=Contemporary Authors Online, Literature Resource Center|publisher=Detroit: Gale}}</ref><ref>[[Gary Botting]], ''The Theatre of Protest in America'', Edmonton: Harden House, 1972.</ref>

==Early life and education==
Van Itallie was born in [[Brussels]], Belgium on May 25, 1936, to Hugo Ferdinand van Itallie (an [[Investment banking|investment banker]]) and Marthe Mathilde Caroline Levy van Itallie.<ref name="biography" /> His family was Jewish.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.vanitallie.com/performances|title=Jean-Claude van Itallie&#124; Broadway Playwright, Author, Acting Technique}}</ref> In 1940, when the [[Nazi Germany|Nazis]] invaded Brussels, he fled with his family to France, where they received visas to [[Portugal]] from the Portuguese [[consul (representative)|consul]] [[Aristides de Sousa Mendes]].<ref>[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NZOa8eg5_oY Jean-Claude Van Itallie, I Remember]. Exiles Memorial Centre</ref> They stayed in [[Estoril]], at the Pensão Royal, between 8 July and 28 September 1940.<ref>[[Exiles Memorial Center]].</ref> On the same day, they boarded the ''S.S. Hakozaki Maru'' headed for New York City, arriving on 10 October.<ref>Ellis Island Passenger Registration Records.</ref>
Van Itallie grew up in [[Great Neck, New York]], studied at Great Neck High School and [[Deerfield Academy]] in [[Deerfield, Massachusetts]], and graduated from [[Harvard College]] in 1958.<ref name="official" /> He has one sibling, his brother Michael van Itallie.<ref name="LiteraryBiography">{{cite journal|last=Bennett|first=Beate Hein|year=1981|editor1-last=MacNicholas|editor1-first=John|title=Jean-Claude van Itallie in 'Twentieth-Century American Dramatists'|journal=Dictionary of Literary Biography|publisher=Literature Resource Center|volume=7}}</ref>

==Career==
After graduating from Harvard, van Itallie moved to [[Greenwich Village]], studied acting at The Neighborhood Playhouse and film editing at [[New York University]], and wrote for the [[CBS]] [[television program]] ''[[Look Up and Live]]''. In 1963, van Itallie's short play, ''War'', was produced at the Barr Albee Wilder Playwrights Unit on [[Vandam Street (Manhattan)|Vandam Street]], featuring [[Gerome Ragni]] and Jane Lowry and directed by [[Michael Kahn (theatre director)|Michael Kahn]]. ''War'' was later produced alongside [[John Guare]]'s ''Muzeeka'' at the [[Dallas Theater Center]].<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/338 "Production: ''War'' (1968)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref> Van Itallie joined director-actor Joseph Chaikin's Open Theater as Playwright-of-the Ensemble. Van Itallie's early plays were also produced at [[Ellen Stewart]]'s [[La MaMa Experimental Theatre Club|Café La MaMa]], and at [[Joe Cino]]'s [[Caffe Cino]], "birthplace of gay theater."

His 1966 anti-war trilogy, ''America Hurrah'' (''Interview'', ''TV'', and ''Motel''), ran for almost two years at the Pocket Theater [[Off-Broadway]] and at the [[Royal Court Theatre|Royal Court Theater]] in [[London]].<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/164 "Production: ''America Hurrah: 3 Views of the U.S.A.'' (1966-1968)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref> Two of the one-acts were first presented at [[La MaMa Experimental Theatre Club]] in 1964 and 1965. ''Interview'' was directed by Peter Feldman, and ''Motel'' was directed by [[Michael Kahn (theatre director)|Michael Kahn]].<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/167 "Production: ''Two Short Plays by Jean-Claude van Itallie'' (1965)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref> ''Motel'' was revived at La MaMa in 1981, again directed by Kahn, for the theater's 20th anniversary.<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/2361 "Production: ''Motel'' (1981)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref>

In 1972, he wrote the script for a [[gay pornography|gay pornographic]] film ''America Creams'' under a pseudonym, one of the first well-known writers to write a [[hardcore pornography|hardcore pornographic]] film.<ref>{{cite magazine|magazine=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|title=Is Tom Eyen First 'Name' Porno Scribe?|date=13 November 1974|page=26}}</ref>

Van Itallie has written over thirty plays. He wrote the ensemble play ''The Serpent'' with Chaikin's Open Theater. ''The Serpent'' premiered at [[Rome]]'s Teatro dell'Arte in 1968. Van Itallie's ''Tibetan Book of the Dead, or How Not to Do It Again'', based on the [[Bardo Thodol]] and with music by [[Steve Gorn|Steven Gorn]], premiered at La MaMa in 1983.<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/2480 "Production: ''The'' ''Tibetan Book of the Dead or How Not To Do It Again'' (1983)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref>

Other van Itallie plays include:

*''Dream'' (produced at La MaMa in 1965)<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/288 "Production: ''Dream'' (1965)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref>
*''King of the United States'' (a [[Musical theatre|musical]] written with composer [[Richard Peaslee]]; premiered at [[Theater for the New City]], 1972)
* ''Bag Lady'' (premiered at Theater for the New City, 1979)
*''Naropa'' (produced at La MaMa in 1982 with music by Steven Gorn)<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/2453 "Production: ''Naropa'' (1982)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref> 
* ''The Traveler'' (about a composer struck with [[aphasia]]; premiered at [[Mark Taper Forum]] in [[Los Angeles]], 1987 produced at [[Almeida Theatre|Almeida Theater]] in London with [[David Threlfall]], 1988)
* ''Struck Dumb'' (written for and with [[Joseph Chaikin]], premiered at Taper Too in Los Angeles, 1989; anthologized in ''Best American Short Plays, 1991–92'')
* ''Light'' (about [[Voltaire]], [[Émilie du Châtelet|Emilie du Chatelet]], and [[Frederick the Great]]; premiered at Boston Court Theater in [[Pasadena, California|Pasadena]], 2003; received several Los Angeles critics awards)
* ''Fear Itself, Secrets of the White House'' (premiered at Theater for the New City, 2006)

In January 1967, van Itallie's ''Pavane'' was broadcast along with [[Sam Shepard]]'s ''Fourteen Hundred Thousand'' and [[Paul Foster (playwright)|Paul Foster]]'s ''The Recluse'' as "La MaMa Playwrights" on [[NET Playhouse]].<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/1423 "Special Event: La Mama Playwrights on NET Playhouse". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref> The program was re-broadcast on [[National Educational Television|NET]] in 1969 along with footage from the opening of La MaMa's new theater space.<ref>La MaMa Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/1505 "Special Event: La Mama Playwrights on NET Playhouse (re-broadcast)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref>

Van Itallie's translation of Chekhov's ''[[The Seagull]]'' was first produced at the MacArthur Theater in [[Princeton, New Jersey|Princeton]] in 1973. It then premiered at the [[Manhattan Theatre Club|Manhattan Theater Club]] and the [[American Repertory Theater|American Repertory Theatre]] in [[Cambridge, Massachusetts]]. His translation of [[The Cherry Orchard|''The'' ''Cherry Orchard'']] premiered at [[Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts|Lincoln Center]], featuring [[Irene Worth]] and [[Meryl Streep]] and directed by [[Andrei Serban]], in 1977. His translation of ''[[Three Sisters (play)|Three Sisters]]'' premiered both at the American Repertory Theatre and at the Manhattan Theatre Club, featuring [[Sam Waterston]] and [[Dianne Wiest]], in 1979. His translation of ''[[Uncle Vanya]]'' premiered at La MaMa, featuring [[F. Murray Abraham]] and Chaikin and directed by Serban, in 1983.<ref>La Mama Archives Digital Collections. [http://catalog.lamama.org/index.php/Detail/Occurrence/Show/occurrence_id/2542 "Production: ''Uncle Vanya'' (1983)". Accessed July 24, 2018.]</ref>

In 1997, van Itallie performed with co-creators Kermit Dunkelberg and Court Dorsey in ''Guys Dreamin''', as directed by Kim Mancuso and Joel Gluck. In 1999 and 2000, van Itallie performed his [[one-man show]] ''War, Sex, and Dreams'' at Highways in [[Santa Monica, California|Santa Monica]] and at La MaMa. In 2012, he performed his one-man show ''Confessions and Conversation'' at La MaMa, as directed by Rosemary Quinn.

His 2016 book, ''Tea with Demons - Games of Transformation'', includes [[memoir]] and forty-nine [[self-development]] games for the reader to play.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.teawithdemons.com/|title=Tea with Demons|access-date=23 July 2018}}</ref> He taught writing and performance workshops, and has taught at [[Princeton University]], [[New York University]], [[Harvard University]], [[Yale University]], [[Amherst College]], [[Columbia University]], [[Middlebury College]], the [[University of Colorado]], [[Smith College]], the [[The New School|New School for Social Research]], [[Naropa University]], the [[Esalen Institute]], the [[Omega Institute for Holistic Studies]], the [https://www.opencenter.org/ New York Open Center], [https://rowecenter.org/ Rowe Conference Center], and [http://www.eastonmountain.org/ Easton Mountain], among other universities and retreat centers.<ref name="official" />

He lived on a farm in [[western Massachusetts]], where he taught and directed the [http://www.shantigar.org/ Shantigar Foundation] for theatre and [[meditation]], and in Greenwich Village.<ref name="biography" /> His [[Archive|papers]] are held in [http://www.library.kent.edu/special-collections-and-archives/jean-claude-van-itallie-papers Kent State University] [[Special collections|Special Collections]]. The papers cover van Itallie's full career, and he would regularly deposit additional items to the collection.
