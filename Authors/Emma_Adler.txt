'''Emma Adler''' (née, Braun; pen names, '''Marion Lorm''' and '''Helene Erdmann'''; 20 May 1858 – 23 February 1935) was an Austrian ''[[fin de siècle]]''<ref name="FeiereisenHill2012">{{cite book|last1=Feiereisen|first1=Florence|last2=Hill|first2=Alexandra Merley|title=Germany in the Loud Twentieth Century: An Introduction|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=-T22vJHaIKIC&pg=PP13|year=2012|publisher=Oxford University Press|isbn=978-0-19-975939-2|pages=13–}}</ref> journalist and writer.

==Biography Overview==
She is known for works of fiction, [[Historical fiction|historical novels]], translations, as well as her correspondence with [[Karl Kautsky]].<ref name="Blum2015">{{cite book|last=Blum|first=Mark E.|title=The Austro-Marxists 1890—1918: A Psychobiographical Study|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=oakgBgAAQBAJ&pg=PA234|date=13 January 2015|publisher=University Press of Kentucky|isbn=978-0-8131-6216-4|pages=234–}}</ref> She was a socialist who, with other [[Jewish]] writers of the time, such as [[Hedwig Dohm]], [[Bertha Pappenheim]], and [[Hedwig Lachmann]], "combined political activity with artistic creativity".<ref name="EiglerKord1997">{{cite book|last1=Eigler|first1=Friederike Ursula|last2=Kord|first2=Susanne|title=The Feminist Encyclopedia of German Literature|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=8taL9KiA0ykC&pg=PA207|date=1 January 1997|publisher=Greenwood Publishing Group|isbn=978-0-313-29313-9|pages=207–}}</ref> Adler was the publisher of the ''Arbeiterinnen-Zeitung''.<ref name="Jacobs1992">{{cite book|last=Jacobs|first=Jack|title=On Socialists and "the Jewish Question" After Marx|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=XffJLtagfF4C&pg=PA211|date=1 January 1992|publisher=NYU Press|isbn=978-0-8147-4213-6|pages=211, 214–}}</ref>

Adler was born in [[Debrecen]], [[Kingdom of Hungary|Hungary]] in 1858. She was the sister of [[Heinrich Braun (writer)|Heinrich Braun]]; and the wife of [[Victor Adler]], a physician and politician who founded the [[Social Democratic Party of Austria]] in Austria. They married in 1878, and had three children, [[Friedrich Adler (assassin)|Friedrich]] (born 1878), Marie (born 1881), Karl (born 1885). She dealt with severe depressive episodes during periods of her life,<ref name="Loewenberg1996">{{cite book|last=Loewenberg|first=Peter|title=Decoding the Past: The Psychohistorical Approach|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=eTn61f9M-EYC&pg=PA138|date=1 January 1996|publisher=Transaction Publishers|isbn=978-1-4128-2139-1|pages=138, 141–}}</ref> and died in [[Zurich]], Switzerland in 1935.
