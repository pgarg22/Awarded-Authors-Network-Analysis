'''Bennie Lee Sinclair''' (April 15, 1939 – May 22, 2000) was an American poet, novelist, and short story writer. She was named by [[Governor of South Carolina|Governor]] [[Richard Wilson Riley]] as the fifth [[South Carolina Poet Laureate]] from 1986 to 2000.

==Biography==

===Early life and education===
Sinclair was born on April 15, 1939, in [[Greenville, South Carolina]].<ref name=SCCftB>{{cite web|title=Bennie Lee Sinclair|url=http://faculty.libsci.sc.edu/literarymap/authors/sincl.htm|publisher=South Carolina Center for the Book|accessdate=December 21, 2012|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140127113458/http://faculty.libsci.sc.edu/literarymap/authors/sincl.htm|archivedate=January 27, 2014}}</ref> She was born to William Graham Sinclair, Sr., and the former Bennie Lee Ward. Her parents separated when she was five years old and she stayed with her mother.<ref name=Bloodroot>{{cite book|last=Dyer|first=Joyce|title=Bloodroot: Reflections on Place by Appalachian Women Writers|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=jIFsZinedfoC&pg=PA261|accessdate=December 23, 2012|date=March 2, 2000|publisher=University Press of Kentucky|isbn=978-0-8131-0983-1|pages=261–271}}</ref> Sinclair graduated from Greenville High School in 1956<ref name="SC Encyclopedia">{{cite book|title=The South Carolina Encyclopedia|year=2006|publisher=University of South Carolina Press|location=Columbia, South Carolina|isbn=978-1-57003-598-2|chapter=Sinclair, Bennie Lee|page=871}}</ref> and then graduated from [[Furman University]] in 1961. She was elected as an alumna member of [[Phi Beta Kappa Society|Phi Beta Kappa]] in 1989.<ref name=Bloodroot /><ref>{{cite news|title=96 Press Symposium|url=http://www2.furman.edu/academics/english/NewsandEvents/Documents/English%20Newsletter%20Fall12.pdf|accessdate=December 24, 2012|newspaper=English @ Furman|date=Fall 2012|format=PDF}}{{dead link|date=July 2017 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref> Her brother Walt (Waldo Graham Sinclair, Jr., nicknamed "Buster")<ref name=Bloodroot /> was a 1967 graduate of [[The Citadel, The Military College of South Carolina|The Citadel]] and was the inspiration for her poetry collection ''The Arrowhead Scholar''.<ref name=Huff />

===Career===
Sinclair's talents exhibited early as one of her poems was published in a national teacher's journal, submitted by her first-grade teacher.<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" /><ref name="The State">{{cite news|title=Bennie Lee Sinclair Writes Poetry for South Carolinians|newspaper=The State|page=A14|date=March 25, 1999}}</ref> Later, she returned to her alma mater and becoming a creative writing instructor at Furman University for many years. Her first poem as an adult was published in the journal ''[[Foxfire (magazine)|Foxfire]]'' in 1968.<ref name=Huff>{{cite news|last=Huff|first=Bobbin|title=Creative Spirits Share Arts|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=zqpJAAAAIBAJ&sjid=EgsNAAAAIBAJ&dq=bennie-lee-sinclair&pg=1222%2C3123152|accessdate=December 23, 2012|newspaper=The Charleston News and Courier|date=March 15, 1979}}</ref>

===Poet laureateship===
Sinclair was named to be South Carolina's fifth [[poet laureate]] by Governor Dick Riley in 1986. At the time, she was the youngest poet laureate the state had appointed, at age 47.<ref name=appointed>{{cite news|title=Greenville native tapped as state's poet laureate|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=rCwtAAAAIBAJ&sjid=Cr0EAAAAIBAJ&dq=bennie%20lee%20sinclair&pg=1162%2C2837927|accessdate=December 21, 2012|newspaper=The Rock Hill Herald|date=September 18, 1986|agency=AP}}</ref> The poet laureate often reads and/or writes a poem for the South Carolina Governor's inauguration. In 1999, at the inauguration of Governor [[Jim Hodges]], high winds blew her papers away, but she proceeded to recite the poem from memory.<ref name="The State" />

===Personal life===
Sinclair married sculptor Don Lewis in 1958.<ref name=Huff /> Lewis was a former [[United States Marine Corps|Marine]] whom she met while they were both freshmen at Furman.<ref name=Bloodroot /> They lived most of their life on a 135-acre wildlife and plant sanctuary in the Cleveland community of [[Greenville County, South Carolina]],<ref name=Huff /><ref>{{cite news|title=South Carolina poet to be highlighted on 'Writers' Circle'|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=k-lCAAAAIBAJ&sjid=06sMAAAAIBAJ&dq=bennie-lee-sinclair&pg=3022%2C3609090|accessdate=December 23, 2012|newspaper=The Herald-Independent|date=September 15, 1994}}</ref> which they moved into in 1976.<ref name=Bloodroot />

Sinclair had suffered from [[diabetes]] and suffered many ailments over the last several years of her life. In 1993, she underwent a kidney transplant.<ref>{{cite news|title=State's poet laureate undergoes transplant|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=PIgeAAAAIBAJ&sjid=TM8EAAAAIBAJ&dq=bennie-lee-sinclair&pg=2296%2C4540645|accessdate=December 23, 2012|newspaper=The Spartanburg Herald-Journal|date=May 15, 1993}}</ref> She died of an apparent heart attack on May 22, 2000, in Greenville, South Carolina.<ref name=SCCftB /><ref name="SHJ obit" />

==Awards and honors==
Sinclair was one of the featured writers of the Southern Appalachian Writers Collection exhibition in the 1980s at the [[University of North Carolina at Asheville]]. The university still maintains the materials in that collection.<ref>{{cite web|title=Bennie Lee Sinclair|url=http://toto.lib.unca.edu/findingaids/mss/southern_appalachian_writers/sinclair_bennie_lee/sinclair_bennie_lee.htm|work=Southern Appalachian Writers Collection|publisher=University of North Carolina at Asheville|accessdate=December 21, 2012|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110927005641/http://toto.lib.unca.edu/findingaids/mss/southern_appalachian_writers/sinclair_bennie_lee/sinclair_bennie_lee.htm|archive-date=September 27, 2011|url-status=dead}}</ref> Her 1990 book of poetry, ''Lord of Spring'', was nominated for a [[Pulitzer Prize for Poetry]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Sinclair at Furman|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=IlgaAAAAIBAJ&sjid=yyQEAAAAIBAJ&dq=bennie%20lee%20sinclair&pg=3341%2C5001080|accessdate=December 21, 2012|newspaper=The Hendersonville Times-News|date=May 17, 1990}}</ref>

Other awards include:<ref name=SCCftB />
* Stephen Vincent Benet Award for narrative poem – 1970
* ''[[The Best American Short Stories]]'' – 1972
* ''[[The South Carolina Review]]'' poetry award – 1972
* Excellence in Writing Award in poetry, [[Winthrop College]] – 1978
* [[South Carolina Poet Laureate]] – 1986–2000
* Book of the Year, Appalachian Writers Association (for ''Lords of Spring'') – 1991<ref>{{cite news|title=People|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=pOseAAAAIBAJ&sjid=684EAAAAIBAJ&dq=bennie-lee-sinclair&pg=6702%2C1573106|accessdate=December 23, 2012|newspaper=The Spartanburg Herald-Journal|date=October 6, 1991}}</ref>

==Works==
Sinclair published works included a novel, short stories, and poetry:<ref name=SCCftB /><ref name="SHJ obit">{{cite news|title=S.C.'s poet laureate Bennie Lee Sinclair dies|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=3boeAAAAIBAJ&sjid=-88EAAAAIBAJ&dq=bennie%20lee%20sinclair&pg=4819%2C9583506|accessdate=December 21, 2012|newspaper=The Spartanburg Herald-Journal|date=May 24, 2000|agency=AP}}</ref>
* ''Little Chicago Suite'' (poetry, 1971)
* ''Taproots: A Study in Cultural Exploration, (as editor 1975)
* ''The Arrowhead Scholar (poetry, 1978)
* ''The Fine Arts Center Story: A Living History'' (as editor, 1980)
* ''South Carolina's International Greenville: A Guide'' (with Linda Shirley Robertson and Lori Storie-Pahlitzsch, 1982)
* ''Lord of Spring'' (poetry, 1990)
* ''The Endangered: New and Selected Poems'' (poetry, 1992)
* ''The Lynching'' (novel, 1992)
* ''Appalachian Trilogy'' (short stories)

In addition to her collections, some of her writings have been anthologized and have appeared in magazines and journals such as ''Foxfire'', ''[[Ms. (magazine)|Ms.]]'', ''[[North American Review]]'', and ''[[The South Carolina Review]]''.<ref name=appointed /> In 1994, Sinclair composed the [[Alma Mater (song)|alma mater]] for [[Coastal Carolina University]].<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" />
