'''Evelyn Bridgett Patricia Ward-Thomas''', née ''Stephens'' (3 July 1926 &ndash; 25 September 2018),<ref name="dk">{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2018/oct/10/evelyn-anthony-obituary|title=Evelyn Anthony obituary|first=Danuta|last=Kean|date=10 October 2018|website=The Guardian|accessdate=24 October 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.thetimes.co.uk/article/evelyn-anthony-obituary-9hzwmxrms|title=Evelyn Anthony obituary|date=5 October 2018|publisher=|accessdate=24 October 2018|via=www.thetimes.co.uk}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=http://announcements.telegraph.co.uk/deaths/227639/ward-thomas.-evelyn|title=Ward-Thomas.- Evelyn|last=Ward-Thomas|first=Christian|date=28 September 2018|website=The Telegraph}}</ref> better known by the pen name '''Evelyn Anthony''', was a British writer. Anthony was born in the Lambeth district of [[London]].

She had a very prolific writing career, translated into at least 19 languages and her 1971 ''[[The Tamarind Seed]]'' was adapted for a [[film]] in 1974, starring [[Julie Andrews]] as Judith Farrow.

==Early life and education==
She was born Evelyn Bridget Patricia Stephens to Henry and Elizabeth (née Sharkey).<ref name="dk"/> Her father had a fortune which came from the [[indelible ink]] invented by his grandfather [[Henry Stephens (doctor)|Dr. Henry Stephens]].<ref name="dk"/> She was a keen reader as a child and attended the Convent of the Sacred Heart school in [[Roehampton]].<ref name="dk"/> She was evacuated to the [[West Country]] during World War II.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=http://www.mysterypeople.co.uk/evelynanthony.html|title=Evelyn Anthony|last=Hayes|first=Lizzies|date=|website=www.mysterypeople.co.uk|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2020-02-29}}</ref>

Her parents‘ marriage was not happy and by the time she was 12 they had divorced.<ref name="dk" /> She was close to her father and when his health deteriorated within two years of the divorce she cared for him at home.<ref name="dk" />

==Personal life==
She met Michael Ward-Thomas on a double date in [[The Dorchester]] and both were attracted to each other.<ref name="dk"/> He worked for the [[Consolidated African Selection Trust]].<ref name="dk"/> They switched partners and were married a few months later.<ref name="dk"/>

They bought [[Horham Hall]] in 1968 but found that it was costly and sold it in 1976 and moved to [[Naas]], [[County Kildare]] where she had relatives.<ref name="dk"/> Increased income from her writing allowed her to buy Horham Hall back in 1982.<ref name="dk"/>

In 1994 she became the first female [[High Sheriff of Essex]].<ref name="dk"/><ref name=":0" />

In 1995 her daughter Kitty died of a heroin overdose, leading Evelyn to not write for another seven years.<ref name="dk"/>

In 2004 her husband died of a stroke.<ref name="dk"/>

She died at 92, on 25 September 2018, she was survived by her children Susan, Anthony, Ewan, Christian and Luke as well as 16 grandchildren.<ref name="dk"/>

==Writing==
Her pen name is derived from the name of St. [[Anthony of Padua]], patron saint of lost things.<ref name="dk"/>

Her writing career began with short stories that she submitted to women's magazines after the second world war.<ref name="dk"/><ref name=":0" />

Anthony's first historical novel, ''Rebel Princess'' (1953), focused on [[Catherine the Great]]. Other Anthony historical novels depicted [[Anne Boleyn]] (''Anne Boleyn'' 1957) and [[Cultural depictions of Queen Victoria|Queen Victoria]] (''Victoria and Albert'' 1958).<ref name="dk" /> Her historical novels ''Far Fly The Eagles'' and ''Valentina'' were both set during [[Napoleon]]'s invasion of Russia. The later novels had a theme of [[espionage]].<ref>[http://www.trademe.co.nz/Browse/PrintListing.aspx?id=92069296 Trademe.co.nz] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070927235149/http://www.trademe.co.nz/Browse/PrintListing.aspx?id=92069296 |date=27 September 2007 }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.jrank.org/literature/pages/3191/Evelyn-Anthony.html|title=Evelyn Anthony Biography - (1928– ), Imperial Highness, Victoria, Elizabeth, Clandara, Assassin, The Poellenberg Inheritance, The Occupying Power|website=www.jrank.org|accessdate=24 October 2018}}</ref>

Her 1971 ''[[The Tamarind Seed]]'' was adapted for a [[film]] in 1974, starring [[Julie Andrews]] as Judith Farrow, a British Home Office functionary and [[Omar Sharif]] as Feodor, a Soviet air attaché-lovers involved in [[Cold War]] intrigue.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.imdb.com/name/nm0030875/|title=Evelyn Anthony|website=IMDb|accessdate=24 October 2018}}</ref>

Her books were translated into at least nineteen languages.<ref name=":0" />
