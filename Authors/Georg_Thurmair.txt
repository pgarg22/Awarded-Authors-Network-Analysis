'''Georg Thurmair''' (7 February 1909 – 20 January 1984) was a German poet who wrote around 300 hymns, a writer, journalist and author of documentary films.

==Career==
Born in [[Munich]], he took commercial training and worked from 1926 as a secretary at the {{ill|Jugendhaus Düsseldorf|de}}. He became an assistant to {{ill|Ludwig Wolker|de}} who had worked in Munich from 1923, but moved to [[Düsseldorf]] when he was elected president of the ''Katholischer Jungmännerverband Deutschlands''.<ref name="John" /> Thurmair studied at the Düsseldorf [[Abendgymnasium]].<ref name="Labonté" />

In 1932 Thurmair edited at a national meeting of the {{ill|Sturmschar|de}} several editions of the weekly ''Junge Front'', which was directed against the emerging [[National Socialism]]. The Nazis claimed the title, and it had to be renamed ''Michael'' in 1935, and was banned in 1936.<ref name="Jugend" /> Thurmair worked on two songbooks of the ''Jungmännerverband'', ''{{ill|Singeschiff|de|Singeschiff|lt=Das graue Singeschiff}}'' and ''Das gelbe Singeschiff''.<ref name="John" /><ref name="Linner" /> From 1934, Thurmair was an editor of the youth journal ''Die Wacht'', which first published in 1935 his hymns "[[Nun, Brüder, sind wir frohgemut]]" (known as the [[Altenberg (Bergisches Land)|Altenberg]] pilgrimage song) and "Wir sind nur Gast auf Erden", which was first called a ''Reiselied'' (travel song).<ref name="Jugend" />

He was interrogated by the [[Gestapo]] and included in a ''Liste der verdächtigen Personen'' (list of suspicious persons).<ref name="Linner" /> He therefore wrote under various pseudonyms, such as Thomas Klausner, Stefan Stahl, Richard Waldmann, Simpel Krone, and Schikki.<ref name="Jugend" /> In 1936, Thurmair and [[Adolf Lohmann]] published a school songbook for the Rhineland. As it juxtaposed Catholic songs and Nazi songs, it was banned.<ref name="Linner" />

[[File:Kirchenlied (Text) - 2. Aufl. 1938.jpg|thumb|upright|Cover of ''[[Kirchenlied]]'', second edition, 1938]]
Together with {{ill|Josef Diewald|de}} and Lohmann, in 1938 Thurmair published the hymnal ''[[Kirchenlied]]'', intended to be a common [[hymnal]] for German-speaking Catholics. Called a Standard Songbook,<ref name="Sachs" /> this collection of 140 old and new songs, beginning with the 16th century and including several Protestant songs, as well as ten of Thurmair's songs, was significant for ecumenical church singing in German and became the germ cell for the ''[[Gotteslob (1975)|Gotteslob]]'' of 1975, which incorporated 75 of the ''Kirchenlied'' songs.<ref name="Linner" /> This hymnal was not immediately banned, because of its many Protestant songs.<ref name="Labonté" />

When the Jugendhaus Düsseldorf was closed on 6 February 1939, Thurmair became a freelance writer in [[Recklinghausen]] and, a year later, in Munich. He was drafted from 1940 to 1945.<ref name="Jugend" />

He married [[Maria Luise Thurmair]] in 1941, and they worked together. He worked mainly for the ''Christophorus-Verlag'' in [[Freiburg im Breisgau|Freiburg]], which belongs to the Catholic [[Verlag Herder]], and as chief editor of several Catholic papers.<ref name="Jugend" /> He died in Munich and was buried in the [[Munich Waldfriedhof]].{{sfn|Martin Persch|1996}}

=== Hymns in ''Gotteslob'' ===
Several of Thurmair's hymn were part of the Catholic hymnal [[Gotteslob (1975)|''Gotteslob'' of 1975]],<ref name="Labonté" /> and are part of the 2013 ''[[Gotteslob]]'', including (with the older GL number in brackets):
* 551 (262) [[Nun singt ein neues Lied dem Herren]] (1969/1972, after [[Psalm 98]])
* 271 (169) O Herr, aus tiefer Klage (1935)
* 334 (208) [[O Licht der wunderbaren Nacht]] (1963)
* 377 (472), 2 O Jesu, all mein Leben bist du (1938)
* 455 (615), 2+3 Alles meinem Gott zu Ehren (1963)
* 489 (637) Laßt uns loben, Brüder, loben (1948), now: [[Lasst uns loben, freudig loben]] (2013)
* 487 (638) [[Nun singe Lob, du Christenheit]] (1964)
* 500 (660) [[Nun lässest du, o Herr]] (1966)
* 505 (656) {{ill|Wir sind nur Gast auf Erden|de}} (1935)
