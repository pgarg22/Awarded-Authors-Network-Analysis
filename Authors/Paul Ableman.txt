'''Paul Victor Ableman''' (13 June 1927 – 25 October 2006) was an English playwright and novelist. He was the writer of much erotic fiction and novelisations, and a freelance writer who turned his hand to non-fiction.

==Life and career==
Ableman was born in [[Leeds]], Yorkshire to a Jewish family. He was the son of Jack Ableman, a trouser cutter at a tailoring factory, and Gertrude (née Gould), an actress and writer.
<ref name="auto">Science Fiction and Fantasy Literature, vol. 2, R. Reginald, 1979, pg 789</ref> Following his parents' divorce, he lived with his mother and stepfather, Thurston B. Macauley, a journalist (sometime London correspondent for ''[[The New York Times]]'')<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1997/05/28/world/thurston-b-macauley-a-former-times-correspondent-95.html|title=Thurston B. Macauley, A Former Times Correspondent, 95|date=28 May 1997|accessdate=20 August 2020|via=NYTimes.com}}</ref> in New York. After National Service in the Education Corps based in [[Gibraltar]], he read English at [[King's College, London]], but did not take a degree.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.independent.co.uk/news/obituaries/paul-ableman-422308.html|title=Paul Ableman|date=31 October 2006|website=The Independent|accessdate=20 August 2020}}</ref><ref name="auto"/>

His experimental novel, ''I Hear Voices'', was published in 1958 by the Olympia Press, and his plays include ''Green Julia'' (1966), a witty two-hander in which two young men discuss an absent mistress, and ''Tests'' (1966), which collects surreal playlets written for Peter Brook's Theatre of Cruelty.<ref>The Oxford Companion to English Literature, 6th Edition. Edited by Margaret Drabble, Oxford University Press, 2000 p.2</ref>

Ableman lived in [[Hampstead]], London in the United Kingdom. He was married twice: first to Tina Carrs-Brown in 1958- they had one son, then divorced; then to Sheila Hutton-Fox in 1978 until his death in 2006- they had one son.

Ableman was of Russian ancestry on his father's side and German on his mother's side.
