'''Anna Harriett Drury''' (also '''Harriet''', 1824–1912)<ref name="Circulating" >At the Circulating Library [http://www.victorianresearch.org/atcl/show_author.php?aid=89 Retrieved 22 April 2018.]</ref> was an English novelist who wrote "conventional romances, with a few sharp observations on the role of unattached women in their relatives' houses".<ref name="Feminist">Virginia Blain, Patricia Clements and Isobel Grundy: ''The Feminist Companion to Literature in English. Women Writers from the Middle Ages to the Present Day'' (London: Batsford, 1990), p. 310.</ref> She also published poetry and other novels, some of them marked as being intended "for boys".

==Life==
Anna Drury's birth is recorded in the 1901 census return as in 1824 in [[Harrow, Middlesex]].<ref name="Wikisource">Wikisource page [https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Author_talk:Anna_Harriett_Drury Retrieved 22 April 2018.]{{unreliable source?|date=April 2018}}</ref> Her father, Rev. William James Joseph Drury, and her grandfather, Rev. Mark Drury, had both taught at [[Harrow School]], but they left sizeable debts when they withdrew in 1826.<ref name="Feminist"/> Through this Harrow connection, Drury became friends with [[Anthony Trollope]], who later helped her in her literary work.<ref name="Circulating"/>

Drury's father then became chaplain to King [[Leopold I of Belgium]]. While in [[Brussels]], Anna Drury first met [[Frances Trollope]], with whose family the Drurys were friendly. By then Anna Drury knew Greek and some Hebrew. She had begun creative writing as a child.<ref name="Feminist"/>

After a period in London, Anna Drury moved to [[Torquay]] in 1866, where she became a close friend of [[Frances Mary Peard]].<ref name="Feminist"/> The 1901 census return gives as her occupation "living by her own means (author)". She and two probable sisters of hers, Jane Emily and Sarah Frances, were boarders in a house that may have belonged to Thomas and Jane Tothill, who are also listed as resident there.<ref name="Wikisource"/>

Anna Harriett Drury never married. She died in [[Newton Abbot]] in June 1912.<ref name="Circulating"/>

==Writings==
Drury's "conventional romances" and other works included ''Annesley, and other poems'' (1847), ''Friends and Fortune, a moral tale'' (1849), ''Light and Shade: or, the Young Artist. A tale'' (1853), ''Misrepresentation'' (1859), ''The Story of a Shower'' (1872), ''Gabriel's Appointment'' (1877), ''Called to the Rescue'' (1879) and ''In the Enemy's Country; or, the Raven of Steinbrück. A story of 1813, etc.'' (1891) Some are marked as "for boys".<ref>British Library catalogue [http://explore.bl.uk/primo_library/libweb/action/search.do?ct=Next+Page&pag=nxt&indx=1&pageNumberComingFrom=1&frbg=&&vl(488279563UI0)=any&fn=search&indx=1&dscnt=0&scp.scps=scope%3A(BLCONTENT)&tb=t&vid=BLVU1&mode=Basic&ct=search&srt=rank&tab=local_tab&dum=true&vl(freeText0)=Anna%20Harriet%20Drury&dstmp=1524423780637 Retrieved 22 April 2018.]</ref>

Numerous books of Drury's are available in facsimile reprints, secondhand original editions, and as free downloads.<ref>E. g. ''Eastbury'' (1851) [https://books.google.com/books/about/Eastbury.html?id=gL0BAAAAQAAJ&redir_esc=y Retrieved 22 April 2018.]</ref>

In ''The Normans: or, Kith and Kin'' (1870), the heroine pointedly asks her father, "What becomes of clergymen's daughters when their fathers die, and their homes are broken up?"<ref>Vol. I, p. 29, quoted in ''Feminist...''.</ref>
