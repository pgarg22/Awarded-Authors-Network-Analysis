'''William Gibson''' (November 13, 1914 – November 25, 2008) was an [[United States|American]] [[playwright]] and [[novelist]]. He won the [[Tony Award for Best Play]] for ''[[The Miracle Worker (play)|The Miracle Worker]]'' in 1959, which he later adapted for the [[The Miracle Worker (1962 film)|film version]] in 1962.

==Early life and education==
Gibson graduated from the [[City College of New York]] in 1938, and was of [[Irish people|Irish]], [[French people|French]], [[Germans|German]], [[Dutch people|Dutch]] and [[Russians|Russian]] and [[Greeks|Greek]] ancestry.<ref>{{cite news|author=Christopher Hawtree |url=https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2009/feb/11/obituary-william-gibson-playwright/ |title=ObituaryWilliam GibsonLate-blooming writer best known for his play The Miracle Worker |newspaper=Guardian |access-date=2014-06-15}}</ref>

==Work as playwright==
Gibson's Broadway debut had been with ''[[Two for the Seesaw (play)|Two for the Seesaw]]'' in 1958, a critically acclaimed two-character play which starred Henry Fonda and, in her own Broadway debut, Anne Bancroft. It was directed by [[Arthur Penn]]. Gibson published a chronicle of the vicissitudes of rewriting for the sake of this production with a nonfiction book in the following year, ''The Seesaw Log''. His most famous play is ''[[The Miracle Worker (play)|The Miracle Worker]]'' (1959), the story of [[Helen Keller]]'s childhood education, which won him the [[Tony Award for Best Play]] after he adapted it from his original 1957 [[telefilm]] script.<ref name="nyt-obit">{{cite news |first=David |last=Carr |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/11/28/theater/28gibs.html?_r=1&scp=5&sq=William%20Gibson&st=cse |title=William Gibson, playwright, dies at 94 |newspaper= The New York Times |date= November 27, 2008 |page= A34}}</ref><ref name="reuters">[https://www.reuters.com/article/peopleNews/idUSTRE4AR66520081128 "'Miracle Worker' playwright William Gibson dies,"] November 28, 2008.</ref> He adapted the work again for the [[1962 in film|1962]] [[The Miracle Worker (1962 film)|film version]], receiving an [[Academy Award]] nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay; the same actresses who previously had won [[Tony Award]]s for their performances in the stage version, [[Anne Bancroft]] and [[Patty Duke]], received Academy Awards for the film version as well.<ref name="nyt-obit"/>  [[Arthur Penn]] directed both the stage and film versions.

His other works include ''[[Dinny and the Witches]]'' (1948, revised 1961), in which a jazz musician incurs the wrath of three [[Macbeth|Shakespearean]] witches by blowing a riff which stops time;  the book for the musical version of [[Clifford Odets]]' ''[[Golden Boy (musical)|Golden Boy]]'' (1964), which earned him yet another Tony nomination; ''A Mass for the Dead'' (1968), an autobiographical family chronicle; ''A Cry of Players'' (1968), a speculative account of the life of young [[William Shakespeare]] (with Anne Bancroft starring for Gibson, this time as Shakespeare's wife, [[Anne Hathaway (Shakespeare)|Anne Hathaway]]); "American Primitive" (1969) a verse play adapted from the letters of John and Abigail Adams, premiered at Williamstown Theatre Festival, directed by Frank Langella and starring Anne Bancroft; ''Goodly Creatures'' (1980), about Puritan dissident [[Anne Hutchinson]]; and ''Monday After the Miracle'' (1982), a continuation of the Helen Keller story. His ill-received<ref name="reuters"/> ''Golda'' (1977), a work about the late [[Israel]]i [[Prime Minister]] [[Golda Meir]] became so popular in its revised version, ''[[Golda's Balcony]]'' (2003), that it set a record as the longest-running one-woman play in Broadway history on January 2, 2005.<ref>[[Simonson, Robert]] (September 23, 2004). [http://www.playbill.com/article/goldas-balcony-becomes-longest-running-one-woman-show-in-bway-history-oct-3-com-122091 “Golda's Balcony Becomes Longest-Running One-Woman Show in Bway History Oct. 3”]. ''Playbill''. Retrieved March 22, 2013.</ref> 

1984 marked the debut of ''[[Raggedy Ann (musical)|Raggedy Ann: The Musical Adventure]]'', a dark fantasy about a sickly little girl who's whisked away on a quest to evade death, featuring the [[Raggedy Ann|titular doll]] from popular children's stories, and songs by ''[[Sesame Street]]'''s [[Joe Raposo]]. The show traveled to Russia, where it was a smash-hit the following year under the title ''Rag Dolly'',<ref>{{cite magazine |last=Bumpers |first=Jasmine |date=2020 |title=Dolly Diplomacy |url=https://www.nysarchivestrust.org/new-york-archives-magazine/magazine-highlights/spring-2020-volume-19-number-4 |magazine=New York Archives |location= |publisher= |access-date=2021-08-18}}</ref> and then it closed on Broadway in 1986 with only 15 previews and 5 performances.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.guidetomusicaltheatre.com/shows_r/raggedyann.html |title=Raggedy Ann |author=<!--Not stated--> |date= |website=Guide to Musical Theater |publisher= |access-date=2021-08-18 |quote=Opened 16th October, 1986; closed 19th October 1986 (15 previews; 5 performances)}}</ref> Thanks to bootleg recordings, the show went on to garner a cult reputation on the internet.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://orangecow.org/board/viewtopic.php?f=4&t=5&start=80 |title=Re: Raggedy Ann & Andy Thread |last=Gilchrist |first=Garrett |date=2021-04-16 |website=Orange Cow |publisher= |access-date=2021-08-18 |quote=Young people on Tumblr and Twitter have discovered the Raggedy Ann 1977 film and later Rag Dolly musical (by Joe Raposo). There's a new fandom.}}</ref>

==Other published works==
In 1973, Gibson published ''A Season in Heaven'', an account of his studies with Maharishi Mahesh Yogi in Punta Umbria and La Antilla, Spain. In 1954, Gibson published a novel, ''[[The Cobweb (1955 film)|The Cobweb]]'', set in a psychiatric hospital resembling the [[Menninger Clinic]];<ref name="nyt-obit"/> in 1955, the novel was adapted as a movie by [[Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer]].

==Family and later life==
Gibson married [[Margaret Brenman-Gibson]], a [[psychotherapist]] and biographer of Odets, in 1940. After 1954, the couple would later move from [[Topeka, Kansas]] to [[Stockbridge, Massachusetts]], where Margaret took a position as a psychoanalyst. Brenman-Gibson died in 2004, leaving behind her husband and their two sons, Daniel and Thomas.<ref name="nyt-obit"/>
