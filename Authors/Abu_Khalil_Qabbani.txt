'''Abu Khalil Qabbani''' ({{lang-ar|أبو خليل القباني}} / [[ALA-LC]]: ''Abū Khalīl al-Qabbānī''; 1835–1902) was a [[Syria]]n playwright and composer of [[Turkish people|Turkish]] origin.<ref name="Sadgrove 2010 loc=267">{{citation |last=Sadgrove|first=Philip|year=2010|chapter=Ahmad Abu Khalil al-Qabbani (1833-1902)|title=Essays in Arabic Literary Biography: 1850-1950|editor1-last=Allen|editor1-first=Roger M. A.|editor2-last=Lowry|editor2-first=Joseph Edmund|editor3-last=Stewart |editor3-first=Devin J.|publisher=Otto Harrassowitz Verlag|page=267|isbn=978-3447061414}}</ref> He is considered the founder of the short musical play (the [[operetta]])<ref name="las">{{cite book
  | last = Hamdan
  | first = Masud
  | author-link = Masud Hamdan
  | title = Poetics, Politics and Protest in Arab Theatre
  | publisher = [[Sussex Academic Press]]
  | isbn=1-84519-106-4
  | year = 2006 }}
</ref> in Arabic theatre. His play ''Abu al-Hassan al-Mughaffal'' caused a wave of protest as a result of his mockery of [[Caliph]] [[Harun al-Rashid]], and ended with a decree by the [[Ottoman Empire|Ottoman]] government to close his theatre and prevent theatrical performances in Syria.<ref name="las"/> Qabbani left for [[Egypt]] and produced his plays there until 1900. He returned to Syria and died two years later in 1902.<ref name="las"/> He is considered the "father of Syrian theatre", and has influenced later generations including the famous [[Duraid Lahham]].<ref name="las"/> His brother is the grandfather of [[Nizar Qabbani]].
He also performed in Turkey and America.
