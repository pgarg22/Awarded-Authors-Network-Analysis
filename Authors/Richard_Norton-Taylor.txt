'''Richard Norton-Taylor''' (born 6 June 1944)<ref name="D-day baby">{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2019/jun/02/i-was-a-d-day-baby-at-least-i-wasnt-called-dwight|title=I was a D-day baby, but at least I wasn't called Dwight|first=Richard|last=Norton-Taylor|newspaper=The Guardian|date=2 June 2019}}</ref> is a [[British people|British]] [[editing|editor]], [[journalist]], and [[playwright]]. He wrote for ''[[The Guardian]]'' on defence and security matters from 1975 to 2016, and was the newspaper's security editor. He now works for the investigative journalism site ''[[Declassified UK]]''.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://declassifieduk.org/how-british-journalists-are-seduced-by-the-ministry-of-defence-and-spooks/|title=How British journalists are seduced by the Ministry of Defence and spooks|first=Richard|last=Norton-Taylor|date=28 September 2021|website=Declassified UK}}</ref>

==Early life and education==
He was born '''Richard Seymour Norton-Taylor''' to Lt. Seymour Norton-Taylor, R.A. and Gweneth Joan Powell (died 9 January 1978).

Norton-Taylor was educated at [[The King's School, Canterbury|The King's School]] in [[Canterbury]], [[Kent]], going on to study at [[Hertford College, Oxford|Hertford College]], a [[Collegiate university|constituent college]] of the [[University of Oxford]], and the [[College of Europe]] in [[Bruges]].<ref name="D-day baby" />

==Career==
He was the [[European Economic Community|European Community]] and [[Brussels]], [[Belgium]], correspondent for both ''[[The Washington Post]]'' and ''[[Newsweek]]'' between 1967 and 1975, while also contributing to ''[[The Economist]]'' and the ''[[Financial Times]]''.

Norton-Taylor joined ''The Guardian'' in 1975, concentrating on [[Government of the United Kingdom|Whitehall]], official secrecy, and behind-the-scenes decision-making. He became an expert on British and Soviet intelligence activities during the Second World War. In 1988, he made an extended appearance on the TV discussion programme ''[[After Dark (TV series)|After Dark]]'', alongside (among others) [[Harold Musgrove]], [[Hilary Wainwright]] and [[George Brumwell]], discussing his book ''Blacklist: The Inside Story of Political Vetting'', co-written with Mark Hollingsworth.

He has written several plays based on transcripts of public inquiries, including ''The Colour of Justice'' (1999), based on the hearing of the [[Death of Stephen Lawrence#Public inquiries into the police investigation|MacPherson Inquiry]] into the police conduct of the investigation into the [[Death of Stephen Lawrence|murder of Stephen Lawrence]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/uk/1999/jan/17/race.world|title=Here is racism in all of its subtle shades|first=Susannah|last=Clapp|newspaper=[[The Observer]]|date=17 January 1999}}</ref> Another was ''Justifying War: Scenes from the Hutton Inquiry'' (2003), both of which premiered at the [[Tricycle Theatre]].

Norton-Taylor left ''The Guardian'' in July 2016<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/media/2016/jul/17/brussels-spies-paradise-europe|last=Norton-Taylor|first=Richard|title='Brussels was paradise for journalists ... and full of spies'|newspaper=The Guardian|date=18 July 2016|page=31}}</ref> and currently writes for ''[[Declassified UK]]''.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://declassifieduk.org/author/richard-norton-taylor/|title=RICHARD NORTON-TAYLOR, Author at Declassified UK|website=Declassified UK}}</ref>

==Awards==
In 1986 Norton-Taylor won the [[Campaign for Freedom of Information|Freedom of Information Campaign]] award. That same year he was prevented initially by a court injunction from reporting the contents of ''[[Spycatcher]]'' (1987), the memoirs of [[Peter Wright (MI5 officer)|Peter Wright]], a former [[MI5]] agent. The government's injunction was dismissed in the High Court by [[Richard Scott, Baron Scott of Foscote|Lord Justice Scott]].

Norton-Taylor was one of the few journalists to cover the [[Scott Report|Scott inquiry]] from start to finish. His play, ''Half the Picture'', based on the inquiry, received a 1994 ''Time Out'' Drama, Comedy and Dance award for its "brave initiative".

In 2010, with fellow ''Guardian'' journalist [[Ian Cobain]], he was awarded a Human Rights Campaign of the Year Award from [[Liberty (advocacy group)|Liberty]] for their "investigation into Britain's complicity in the use of torture" by the United States against detainees at their facility at [[Guantanamo Bay]] and at [[black sites]].<ref>Liberty, [https://www.liberty-human-rights.org.uk/previous-award-winners List of previous winners]</ref>

==Personal life==
In 1967, he married Anna C. Rendle, eldest daughter of Mr. and Mrs. J. E. Rendle, of [[Kemerton]], near [[Tewkesbury]], [[Gloucestershire]].<ref>{{cite journal|title=Forthcoming marriages|journal=[[The Times]]|date=16 June 1967|page=12}}</ref>

Norton-Taylor is a Member of Council of the [[Royal United Services Institute]] and a trustee of the Civil Liberties Trust and the London Action Trust.
