'''Michael Cristofer''' (born January 22, 1945) is an American actor, playwright and filmmaker. He received the [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]] and the [[Tony Award for Best Play]] for ''[[The Shadow Box]]'' in 1977. From 2015 to 2019, he played the role of Phillip Price in the [[USA Network]] television series ''[[Mr. Robot]]''.

==Life and career==
{{more citations needed|section|date=March 2018}}
Cristofer was born '''Michael Procaccino''' in [[Trenton, New Jersey]], the son of Mary and Joseph Procaccino.<ref name="TVGuide"/> He started his theatrical career as an actor, primarily on stage. He also started writing plays. He has also written numerous screenplays for film.

Cristofer was awarded a [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]] and a [[Tony Award]] for the Broadway production of his play, ''[[The Shadow Box]]'' (1977). After [[New York City]], the play was produced in every major American city and worldwide from Europe to the Far East. Other plays include ''Breaking Up'' at [[Primary Stages]]; ''Ice'' at [[Manhattan Theatre Club]]; ''Black Angel'' at [[Circle Repertory Company]]; ''The Lady and the Clarinet'' (starring [[Stockard Channing]]), produced by the Mark Taper Forum, [[Long Wharf Theater]], Off-Broadway and on the London Fringe; and ''Amazing Grace'' (1996; starring [[Marsha Mason]]), which received the [[American Theatre Critics Association|American Theater Critics Award]] as the best play produced in the United States during the 1996–97 season.

Cristofer's film work includes the screenplays for ''The Shadow Box'', directed by [[Paul Newman]] ([[Golden Globe Award]], [[Emmy Award|Emmy]] nomination); ''[[Falling in Love (1984 film)|Falling in Love]]''; ''[[The Witches of Eastwick (film)|The Witches of Eastwick]]'', adapted from the novel by [[John Updike]]; ''[[The Bonfire of the Vanities (film)|The Bonfire of the Vanities]],'' adapted from the novel by [[Tom Wolfe]] and directed by [[Brian De Palma]]; ''[[Breaking Up (1997 film)|Breaking Up]]'', and ''[[Casanova (2005 film)|Casanova]]''.{{Citation needed|date=August 2021}}

His directing credits include ''[[Gia]]'', for [[Home Box Office|HBO Pictures]] (starring [[Angelina Jolie]], [[Mercedes Ruehl]] and [[Faye Dunaway]]), which was nominated for five Emmy Awards and for which he won a [[Directors Guild of America|Directors Guild Award]]. He next directed ''[[Body Shots (film)|Body Shots]]''; and ''[[Original Sin (2001 film)|Original Sin]]'', which was released in 2001.{{Citation needed|date=August 2021}}

For eight years he worked as artistic advisor and finally co-artistic director of River Arts Repertory in [[Woodstock, New York]], a company which produced new plays by writers such as [[Richard Nelson (playwright)|Richard Nelson]], [[Mac Wellman]], [[Eric Overmeyer]], and others, including the American premiere of [[Edward Albee]]'s ''[[Three Tall Women]]'', a production which later moved to Off-Broadway.

Also at River Arts, he wrote stage adaptations of the films ''[[Love Me or Leave Me (film)|Love Me or Leave Me]]'' and the legendary ''[[Casablanca (film)|Casablanca]]''. He directed [[Joanne Woodward]] in his own adaptation of [[Henrik Ibsen]]'s ''[[Ghosts (play)|Ghosts]]''. His most recent work for the theater, ''The Whore and Mr. Moore'', premiered at [[Dorset, Vermont|Dorset Theatre Festival]]'s 2012 summer season. He collaborated with trumpeter [[Terence Blanchard]], writing the libretto for ''[[Champion (opera)|Champion]]'', a boxing opera in jazz music based on the life of prize fighter [[Emile Griffith]]. It premiered in June 2013 at [[Opera Theater of St. Louis]]. His latest work, ''Execution of the Caregiver'', is based on the true story of a woman in South Carolina who killed her mother, fiancé and several people for whom she was purportedly caring.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1992-07-08-ca-1650-story.html|title=The Lighter Side of Michael Cristofer : Stage: The playwright's latest, opening tonight at the Old Globe, is about breaking up. But in an upbeat kind of way.|date=July 8, 1992|website=Los Angeles Times}}</ref>

After a 15-year hiatus, Cristofer returned to his acting career, appearing in ''[[Romeo and Juliet]]'' ([[New York Shakespeare Festival]]), ''Trumpery'' by [[Peter Parnell]], ''[[Three Sisters (play)|Three Sisters]]'' (Williamstown Theater), ''Body of Water'' (with [[Christine Lahti]]), and the [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]] revival of ''[[A View from the Bridge]]'' (starring [[Liev Schreiber]] and [[Scarlett Johansson]]). He recently appeared in ''The Other Woman'' (with [[Natalie Portman]]), and created the role of Gus in [[Tony Kushner]]'s ''[[The Intelligent Homosexual's Guide to Capitalism and Socialism with a Key to the Scriptures]]'' at the [[Public Theater]].

In 2010, Cristofer was a cast member on [[AMC (TV channel)|AMC]]'s ''[[Rubicon (TV series)|Rubicon]]'', in which he played Truxton Spangler.{{Citation needed|date=August 2021}} In 2012, he played Jerry Rand on the [[NBC]] series, ''[[Smash (U.S. TV series)|Smash]]'', husband to [[Anjelica Huston]]'s character, Eileen Rand.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://articles.boston.com/2012-02-07/theater-art/31028780_1_smash-theater-arts-major-revival|archive-url=https://archive.today/20120701133328/http://articles.boston.com/2012-02-07/theater-art/31028780_1_smash-theater-arts-major-revival|url-status=dead|archive-date=July 1, 2012|title=A familiar face in 'Smash'|work=Boston Globe|author=Aucoin, Don|date=February 6, 2012}}</ref>

In 2013–14, he played millionaire witch-hunter Harrison Renard in ''[[American Horror Story: Coven]]''. In 2015, Cristofer made guest appearances in four episodes of season one of ''[[Mr. Robot]]'' as Phillip Price, the shadowy CEO of the sinister E Corp, and went on to be promoted as a cast member in season two, three, and four.{{Citation needed|date=August 2021}}
