'''Peter Härtling''' ({{IPA-de|ˈpeːtɐ ˈhɛʁtlɪŋ|lang|De-Peter Härtling.ogg}}; 13 November 1933 – 10 July 2017) was a German writer, [[poet]], publisher and journalist. He received the [[Bundesverdienstkreuz|Order of Merit of the Federal Republic of Germany]] for his major contribution to [[German literature]].<ref name=Hartling>{{cite web|url=http://www.haertling.de|title=Lebensdaten von Peter Härtling|publisher=Peter Härtling|language=German|access-date=7 February 2010}}</ref>

==Biography==
Härtling was born in [[Chemnitz]],<ref name=Goethe>{{cite web|url=http://www.goethe.de/kue/lit/prj/kju/aut/hl/hae/enindex.htm|title=Goethe-Institut Children and Young Adult Literature Portal – Peter Härtling|publisher=[[Goethe-Institut]]|access-date=7 February 2010}}</ref> and spent the early part of his childhood living in [[Hartmannsdorf, Mittweida|Hartmannsdorf]], [[Mittweida (district)|Mittweida]], where his father maintained a law firm.<ref name=Hartling/> Following the outbreak of [[World War II]], the family moved to the German-occupied town of [[Olomouc]] in [[Moravia]].<ref name=Hartling/><ref name=Goethe/> Like many of the town's German residents, Härtling's family fled before the [[Red Army]]'s advance on the city during the final months of the war; the family briefly settled in [[Zwettl]], Austria.<ref name=Hartling/> Härtling's father was captured by the Russians, and died in June 1945 at the [[prisoner-of-war camp]] in [[Dollersheim]].<ref name=Hartling/> Following the conclusion of World War II, Härtling finally settled in [[Nürtingen]], [[Baden-Württemberg]].<ref name=Hartling/><ref name=Goethe/> His mother committed suicide in October 1946.<ref name=Hartling/> He studied under [[HAP Grieshaber]] at the Bernsteinschule art school, before starting work as a journalist.<ref name=Goethe/>

Härtling had his first collection of poetry published in 1953.<ref name=Hartling/><ref name=Goethe/> From 1967 to 1973, Härtling was the managing director of the German publishing house [[S. Fischer Verlag]], located in [[Frankfurt]].<ref name=Goethe/> Härtling became a full-time writer after leaving S. Fischer Verlag.<ref name=Goethe/> In the winter semester of 1983/84, he hosted the annual Frankfurter Poetik-Vorlesungen, a lecture series, in which a prominent writer discourses on topics pertaining to their work.<ref name=PoetikVorlesungen>{{cite web|url=http://www.poetikvorlesung.uni-frankfurt.de/Geschichte_der_Poetikdozentur1.html|title=Zur Geschichte der Gastdozentur Poetik|publisher=[[Goethe University Frankfurt]]|date=19 October 2009|language=German|access-date=7 February 2010|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20100715021827/http://www.poetikvorlesung.uni-frankfurt.de/Geschichte_der_Poetikdozentur1.html|archivedate=15 July 2010}}</ref> Härtling used his lectureship to demonstrate the process of using a [[found object]] as the inspiration for a literary work. During the series of lectures, he wrote ''Der spanische Soldat'', a [[short story]] based on a photograph by [[Robert Capa]].<ref name=Soldat>{{cite journal|jstor=40141538|title=Review of Der spanische Soldat|publisher=[[University of Oklahoma Press]]|year=1985|author=Reinhold Grimm|volume=59}}</ref>

Härtling worked as the editor of the magazine ''Der Monat'', and as the president of the Hölderlin society.<ref name=Hartling/> In 1973 he moved to [[Mörfelden-Walldorf]] where he lived until his death on 10 July 2017.<ref name=hr>{{cite web|url=http://hessenschau.de/kultur/schriftsteller-peter-haertling-gestorben,haertling-gestorben-100.html|title=Schriftsteller Peter Härtling gestorben|publisher=hessenschau ([[Hessischer Rundfunk]])|language=German|access-date=10 July 2017}}</ref>

==Literary themes==
Härtling devoted a large proportion of his literary output – both in poetry, and in prose – to the reclamation of history, and his own past.<ref name=Wasbleibt>{{cite web|url=http://www.goethe.de/kue/lit/prj/was/hae/enindex.htm|title=Was bleibt? – Peter Härtling|publisher=[[Goethe-Institut]]|access-date=7 February 2010}}</ref><ref name=Oxford>{{cite web|url=http://www.mywire.com/a/Oxford-Companion-German-Literature/Hrtling-Peter/9543248/|title=Oxford Companion to German Literature: Peter Härtling|publisher=[[Oxford University Press]]|access-date=7 February 2010}}</ref> His autobiographical novel, ''Zwettl'' (1973), deals with the period he spent living in Lower Austria, after his family fled from the Red Army.<ref name=Zwettl>{{cite journal|jstor=40128555|title=Review of Zwettl|publisher=[[University of Oklahoma Press]]|author=Egbert Krisypn|year=1974|volume=48}}</ref> ''Nachgetragene Liebe'' (1980) recounts Härtling's earliest memories of his deceased father.<ref name=Nachgetragene>{{cite journal|jstor=40136080|title=Review of Nachgetragene Liebe|publisher=[[University of Oklahoma Press]]|author=Ernestine Schlant|year=1981|volume=55}}</ref>

Another major influence on Härtling's works has been the literature and music of [[Romanticism]].<ref name=Romanticism>{{cite book|title=America in the eyes of the Germans: An essay on anti-Americanism|last=Diner|first=Dan|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=JcbLSEQQY04C|year=1996|publisher=Markus Wiener|page=34|access-date=7 February 2010 | isbn=978-1-55876-105-6}}</ref> Amongst other works, Härtling has written fictionalised biographical works on the writers [[Friedrich Hölderlin]],<ref name=Oxford/> [[Wilhelm Waiblinger]]<ref name=Oxford/> and [[E. T. A. Hoffmann]],<ref name=Perlentaucher>{{cite magazine|url=http://www.perlentaucher.de/buch/5396.html|title=Peter Härtling – Hoffmann oder Die vielfältige Liebe|magazine=[[Perlentaucher]]|language=German|accessdate=7 February 2010}}</ref> and the composers [[Franz Schubert]],<ref name=Oxford/> and [[Robert Schumann]].<ref name=Schumann>{{cite journal|jstor=40152883|title=Review of Schumanns Schatten|publisher=[[University of Oklahoma Press]]|author=Marjorie L. Hoover|year=1997|volume=71}}</ref>

==Children's literature==
In 1969, after writing a eulogy for the Czech [[Children's literature|children's writer]] [[Jan Procházka (writer)|Jan Procházka]], Härtling began writing books for children. His first children's book, ''Und das ist die ganze Familie'', was published the following year.<ref name=Goethe/> His children's literature has often focused on social problems involving children.<ref name=Goethe/> In ''Das war der Hirbel'' (1973), he wrote about the home of a maladjusted child, and ''Oma'' (1975) talks about aging and death, whilst ''Theo haut ab'' (1977) deals with being uprooted from home and family. There are English translations of several of his children's books, including ''Granny'' (''Oma''), ''Crutches'' (''Krücke''), ''Ben Loves Anna'' (''Ben liebt Anna''), ''Old John'' (''Alter John''),<ref>[https://openlibrary.org/search?q=Peter+H%C3%A4rtling&language=eng English books by Peter Härtling at the Open Library]</ref> and ''Herbie's World'' (''Das war der Hirbel'').<ref>[http://www.worldcat.org/title/herbies-world/oclc/793674446 ''Herbie's World'' at WorldCat]</ref>

==Radio==
Härtling moderated ''Literatur im Kreuzverhör'', a radio show on the cultural radio station of [[Hessischer Rundfunk]].
