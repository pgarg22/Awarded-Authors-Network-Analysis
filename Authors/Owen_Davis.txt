'''Owen Gould Davis''' (January 29, 1874 – October 14, 1956) was an American dramatist known for writing more than 200 plays and having most produced. In 1919, he became the first elected president of the [[Dramatists Guild of America]]. He received the 1923 [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]] for his play ''[[Icebound (play)|Icebound]]'',<ref>{{cite web|title=1923 Pulitzer Prizes|url=http://www.pulitzer.org/prize-winners-by-year/1923|website=The Pulitzer Prizes|access-date=January 20, 2018|archive-url=https://archive.today/20160619044358/http://www.pulitzer.org/prize-winners-by-year/1923|archive-date=June 19, 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref> His plays and scripts included works for radio and film.

Before the [[First World War]], he wrote racy sketches of New York high jinks and low life for the ''[[National Police Gazette|Police Gazette]]'' under the name of '''Ike Swift'''. Many of these were set in the [[Tenderloin, Manhattan]]. Davis also wrote under several other [[pseudonyms]], including '''Martin Hurley''', '''Arthur J. Lamb''', '''Walter Lawrence''', '''John Oliver''', and '''Robert Wayne'''.<ref>{{cite book |title=The Facts on File Companion to American Drama |editor1-first=Jackson R. |editor1-last=Bryer |editor2-first=Mary C. |editor2-last=Hartig |publisher=[[Facts on File]] |location=New York |edition=2nd |year=2010 |isbn=978-0-8160-7748-9 |pages=119–120}}</ref>

== Personal life ==
Davis was born into a large family in [[Portland, Maine]].<ref name="gap">{{cite book|last1=Roberts|first1=Jerry|title=The Great American Playwrights on the Screen: A Critical Guide to Film, Video, and DVD|date=2003|publisher=Hal Leonard Corporation|isbn=978-1-55783-512-3|page=129|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ciwdL9jp0OoC&q=%22Owen+Gould+Davis%22&pg=PA129|access-date=January 20, 2018}}</ref> They moved to [[Bangor, Maine|Bangor]], where he lived until he was 15. As a boy, Davis wrote plays for his eight siblings, who performed them for the town. His parents were Owen Warren Davis, an iron manufacturer, and his wife Abigail Augusta Gould.<ref name="anb">{{cite web|author-link=Brooks McNamara|last1=McNamara|first1=Brooks|title=Davis, Owen Gould|url=http://www.anb.org/view/10.1093/anb/9780198606697.001.0001/anb-9780198606697-e-1600427|website=American National Biography|year=2000|publisher=Oxford University Press|doi=10.1093/anb/9780198606697.article.1600427|access-date=January 20, 2018|archive-url=https://archive.today/20180120033500/http://www.anb.org/view/10.1093/anb/9780198606697.001.0001/anb-9780198606697-e-1600427|archive-date=January 20, 2018|url-status=dead}}</ref>

His brother [[William Hammatt Davis]] later served as chairman of the [[National War Labor Board (1942–1945)|National War Labor Board]] in President [[Franklin D. Roosevelt]]'s administration.

Davis attended the [[University of Tennessee]] in 1888–1889 and transferred to [[Harvard University]] in 1890, completing his degree there in three years. At Harvard, he was active with the Society of Arts drama organization. For a time, he coached a New York preparatory school's football team.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Fischer|first1=Heinz Dietrich|last2=Fischer|first2=Erika J.|title=Complete Biographical Encyclopedia of Pulitzer Prize Winners, 1917–2000: Journalists, Writers and Composers on Their Ways to the Coveted Awards|date=2002|publisher=Walter de Gruyter|isbn=978-3-598-30186-5|pages=53–54|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=-2o4Ywn4LJwC&q=%22Owen+Davis%22+writer&pg=PA54|access-date=January 20, 2018}}</ref>

He married Elizabeth Drury Breyer, an actress, in 1901 or 1902, and they had two sons. Both entered the theater world; [[Owen Davis Jr.]] became an actor, and [[Donald Davis (writer)|Donald Davis]] a playwright.<ref name="anb" />

Davis lived in New York City for much of his life, and died there.

== Career ==
For the first two decades of his writing career, Davis produced melodramas that followed a formula. His entry in the ''Encyclopedia of American Drama''  notes, "The plays all contain life-threatening, visually exciting predicaments out of which the good emerge at the ultimate expense of the villains who put them there."<ref name="ead">{{cite book|last1=Bryer|first1=Jackson R.|last2=Hartig|first2=Mary C.|title=Encyclopedia of American Drama|date=2015|publisher=Infobase Learning|isbn=978-1-4381-4076-6|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=UJ5bAgAAQBAJ&q=%22Owen+Davis%22+writer&pg=PT366|access-date=January 20, 2018}}</ref>

=== Stage ===
In 1897, ''Through the Breakers'', Davis's first play, opened in Bridgeport, Connecticut. It ran for three years.<ref name=gap /> His first Broadway play was ''Reaping the Whirlwind'', which opened on September 17, 1900. He wrote or was otherwise involved in 75 additional Broadway productions, either under his own name or as John Oliver.<ref>{{cite web|title=Owen Davis|url=https://www.ibdb.com/broadway-cast-staff/owen-davis-7980|website=Internet Broadway Database|publisher=The Broadway League|access-date=January 20, 2018|archive-url=https://archive.today/20180120021634/https://www.ibdb.com/broadway-cast-staff/owen-davis-7980|archive-date=January 20, 2018|url-status=dead}}</ref>

=== Film ===
Davis was on the staff of [[Paramount Pictures]] as a screenwriter from 1927 to 1930. His work during that time included ''[[They Had to See Paris]]'' (1929) and ''[[So This Is London (1930 film)|So This Is London]]'' (1930), both of which starred humorist [[Will Rogers]].<ref name=gap />

=== Radio ===
Davis wrote scripts for the radio program ''[[The Gibson Family]]'', which presented each episode in the form of a Broadway musical.
<ref>{{cite book|last1=Ellett|first1=Ryan|title=Radio Drama and Comedy Writers, 1928–1962|date=2017|publisher=McFarland|isbn=978-1-4766-6593-1|page=59|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=SME8DwAAQBAJ&q=%22Owen+Davis%22+writer&pg=PA59|access-date=January 20, 2018}}</ref>

=== Books ===
Davis wrote two autobiographies, ''I'd Like to Do It Again'', which was published in 1931,<ref>{{cite book|last1=Bryer|first1=Jackson R.|last2=Hartig|first2=Mary C.|title=The Facts on File Companion to American Drama|date=2010|publisher=Infobase Publishing|isbn=978-1-4381-2966-2|page=120|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=A4grSoWwXSwC&q=%22Owen+Davis%22+writer&pg=PA120|access-date=January 20, 2018}}</ref> and ''My First Fifty Years in the Theatre'', which focused on the years 1897–1947.<ref name=gap />

== Death ==
On October 13, 1956, Davis died in New York City at age 82. He had been suffering from a long illness and had recently been released from a hospital after three years. He was survived by his wife, their second son Donald, one of his brothers, [[William Hammatt Davis]]<!-- Middle name of brother is corrected from mistake in Chicago Tribune -->, and a sister, Perley Davis.<ref name="ct">{{cite news|title=Owen Davis, Playwright, Dies in N.Y.|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/16692947/owen_davis/|work=Chicago Tribune|date=October 15, 1956|location=Illinois, Chicago|page=77|via = [[Newspapers.com]]|access-date = January 19, 2018}} {{Open access}}</ref>

* ''[[Nellie, the Beautiful Cloak Model]]'' (1906)
* ''Deadwood Dick's Last Shot'' (1907)
* ''[[What Happened to Mary]]'' (1913)
* ''The Scrap of Paper'' (1917)
* ''The Detour'' (1921)
* ''[[Icebound (play)|Icebound]]'' (1923), for which he was awarded the [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]]<br />Adapted as a 1924 silent film ''[[Icebound (1924 film)|Icebound]],'' directed by [[William C. deMille]]
* ''The Nervous Wreck'' (1923), play, later adapted as a 1926 motion picture of the same name. Another adaptation was released in 1944 as ''Up in Arms].'' A different adaptation was as a musical titled ''[[Whoopee!]]'', staged on [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]] in 1928, produced as a film ''[[Whoopee! (film)|Whoopee!]],'' and revived again as a musical in 1979.{{citation needed|date=March 2020}}
* ''The Haunted House'' (1924)
* ''Lazybones'' (1924), made into [[Lazybones (1925 film)|the 1925 motion picture of the same name]]
* ''Beware of Widows'' (1925)
* ''Easy Come, Easy Go'' (1926); play later adapted as a musical, ''Lady Fingers'' (1929)
* ''The Great Gatsby'' (1926), play based on the [[F. Scott Fitzgerald]] [[The Great Gatsby|novel]] of the same name. The play was adapted for the 1949 motion picture of the same name.
* ''Just to Remind You'' (1931), play which premiered at Lakewood Theatre in Maine, with [[Humphrey Bogart]] in lead
* ''The Good Earth'' (1932), dramatization of the [[Pearl S. Buck]] [[The Good Earth|novel]] of the same name. A later adaptation was released as a film in [[The Good Earth (film)|1937]], also with the same name.

* ''The Convict's Sweetheart''
* ''Ethan Frome'' (1935), play based on the [[Edith Wharton]] [[Ethan Frome|novel]], produced on Broadway in 1936
* ''Mr. and Mrs. North'' (1941), dramatization from short stories by [[Richard Lockridge|Richard]] and [[Frances Lockridge]]. This was adapted as a 1942 [https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0033926/ motion picture]
