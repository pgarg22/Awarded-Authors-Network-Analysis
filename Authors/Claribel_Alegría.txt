'''Clara Isabel Alegría Vides'''<ref>{{cite news| title =Claribel Alegría - 1924| url =http://redescolar.ilce.edu.mx/redescolar/memorias/escritoras_hispano01/pbclaribel.htm| work =Escritoras de Hispanoamerica| accessdate =2007-10-24| archive-url =https://web.archive.org/web/20071012021824/http://redescolar.ilce.edu.mx/redescolar/memorias/escritoras_hispano01/pbclaribel.htm| archive-date =2007-10-12| url-status =dead}}</ref> (May 12, 1924 – January 25, 2018), also known by her pseudonym '''Claribel Alegría''',<ref>{{cite news| title=Claribel Alegría| url =http://www.britannica.com/eb/article-9002775/Claribel-Alegria| work =Encyclopædia Britannica| accessdate = 2007-10-24}}</ref> was a Nicaraguan-Salvadoran poet, essayist, novelist, and journalist who was a major voice in the literature of contemporary [[Central America]]. She was awarded the 2006 [[Neustadt International Prize for Literature]].

==Biography==
Alegría was born in [[Estelí]], Nicaragua, to a Nicaraguan father, Daniel Alegría, and a Salvadoran mother, Ana María Vides. Her cousin was activist [[Leonel Gómez Vides]]. When Claribel was nine months old, her father was sent into exile for protesting human rights violations occurring during the [[United States occupation of Nicaragua]]; as a result, Claribel grew up in [[Santa Ana, El Salvador|Santa Ana]], a city in western [[El Salvador]], where her mother came from. Claribel Alegría considered herself to be Nicaraguan-Salvadorean.<ref>{{cite magazine |last=Flores y Ascencio |first=Daniel |date=Winter 2000 |title=Claribel Alegría |url= http://bombmagazine.org/article/2290/claribel-alegr-a |magazine= Bomb |location=New York |publisher= BOMB Magazine|access-date=April 1, 2017 }}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine |last= Moreno|first= Víctor|date=1999 |title=Claribel Alegría |url= https://www.buscabiografias.com/biografia/verDetalle/6461/Claribel%20Alegria |magazine= Busca biografías |publisher= Busca Biografías|access-date=November 14, 2017 }}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine |last= Úbeda|first= Luis Miguel|date=November 14, 2017 |title=El ojo crítico entrevista a Claribel Alegría |url= http://www.rtve.es/alacarta/audios/el-ojo-critico/ojo-critico-claribel-alegria-rosa-montero-secretos-14-11-17/4305246/|magazine= Programa El Ojo Crítico RTVE |location=Madrid|publisher=Radio Televisión Española|access-date=November 14, 2017 }}</ref> Although she was too young to read or write, she began composing poetry at the age of six and dictated them to her mother, who would write them down. Alegría consistently cited [[Rainer Maria Rilke]]'s "[[Letters to a Young Poet]]" as the impetus for becoming a poet.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Alegría, Claribel Joy: 1924—|url = http://www.encyclopedia.com/doc/1G2-3433900009.html|website = www.encyclopedia.com|accessdate = October 2, 2015}}</ref> At the age of seventeen, she published her first poems in [[Repertorio Americano]], a Central American cultural supplement. Soon after, Mexican educator [[José Vasconcelos]] arranged for Alegría to attend finishing school in [[Hammond, Louisiana]].<ref>{{Cite journal|url = http://ic.galegroup.com/ic/bic1/BiographiesDetailsPage/BiographiesDetailsWindow?failOverType=&query=&prodId=BIC1&windowstate=normal&contentModules=&display-query=&mode=view&displayGroupName=Biographies&limiter=&currPage=&disableHighlighting=false&displayGroups=&sortBy=&amp;search_within_results=&p=BIC1&action=e&catId=&activityType=&amp;scanId=&documentId=GALE%7CK2429100132&source=Bookmark&u=wash84213&amp;jsid=c5450a7975fa86cd74a18b41760ffa45|title = Claribel Joy Alegría|last = Gale|date = 2003|journal = Contemporary Hispanic Biography|access-date = September 30, 2015}}</ref> In 1943, she moved to the United States and in 1948 received a [[Bachelor of Arts|B.A.]] in Philosophy and Letters from [[George Washington University]].<ref name="CA">{{cite news| title =Claribel Alegría bio| publisher =University of Oklahoma| url =http://www.ou.edu/worldlit/1Claribel%20Bio.pdf| archive-url =https://web.archive.org/web/20080227090921/http://www.ou.edu/worldlit/1Claribel%20Bio.pdf| url-status =dead| archive-date =February 27, 2008| work =World Literature Today| accessdate =October 24, 2007}}</ref> Alegría was committed to nonviolent resistance. She had a close association with the [[Sandinista National Liberation Front]] (FSLN), which overthrew [[Anastasio Somoza Debayle]] and took control of the Nicaraguan government in 1979. Alegría returned to Nicaragua in 1985 to aid in the reconstruction of Nicaragua.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.poets.org/poet.php/prmPID/275|title=Claribel Alegría|accessdate=October 5, 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140106060717/http://www.poets.org/poet.php/prmPID/275|archive-date=January 6, 2014|url-status=dead}}</ref>

Alegría later lived in [[Managua]], Nicaragua.<ref name="CA"/> She died on 25 January 2018, aged 93.<ref>[https://elpais.com/cultura/2018/01/25/actualidad/1516903304_337221.html Muere la poeta nicaragüense Sofía Claribel Alegría] {{in lang|es}}</ref>

==Career==
Alegría's literary work reflects the style of the popular literary current in Central America during the 1950s and 1960s, "la generacion comprometida" (the committed generation). Like many other poets of her generation who are critical of their societies, she made claims for rights using a language which is often counter-literary.

Alegría published many books of poetry: ''Casting Off'' (2003), ''Sorrow'' (1999), ''Umbrales'' (1996), and ''La Mujer del Río'' (1989). She also published novels and children's stories, as well as testimonies (often in collaboration with her husband, DJ "Bud" Flakoll), such as ''They Won't Take Me Alive''.
