'''David Wolf Budbill''' (June 13, 1940 â€“ September 25, 2016) was an American [[poet]] and [[playwright]]. He was the author of eight books of poems, eight plays, two novels, a collection of short stories, a children's picture book, and dozens of essays.

==Early life==
David Wolf Budbill was born on June 13, 1940, in [[Cleveland, Ohio]]. He studied philosophy and art history at [[Muskingum College]] in New Concord, Ohio. In 1967, he graduated from college with a degree in theology, and from the [[Union Theological Seminary (New York City)|Union Theological Seminary]] in New York City, where he was influenced by the writings of [[Thomas Merton]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.poets.org/poetsorg/poet/david-budbill|title=David Budbill|last=|first=|website=poets.org|publisher=Academy of American Poets|access-date=}}</ref>

==Career==
His three most recent books of poems are "Tumbling toward the End" (Copper Canyon Press, 2017), ''Happy Life'' ([[Copper Canyon Press]], 2011), and ''While We've Still Got Feet'' (Copper Canyon Press, 2005). His collection of narrative poems, ''Judevine'', was republished in an expanded edition by [[Chelsea Green Publishing]] Company in 1999.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.chelseagreen.com/bookstore/item/judevine:paperback|title=Judevine|publisher=Chelseagreen.com|accessdate=5 October 2014}}</ref>

His play ''Judevine'', a stage version of his narrative poems, had 65 productions in 22 states since the early 1980s. Among Budbill's other plays are ''Little Acts of Kindness'', ''Thingy World!'', ''Two for Christmas'', and his latest, first produced in 2010, ''A Song for My Father''.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.davidbudbill.com/play.html |title=Plays by David Budbill |website=davidbudbill.com |publisher=David Budbill |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110720160236/http://www.davidbudbill.com/play.html  |archive-date=July 20, 2011 |access-date=March 2, 2017}}</ref> ''Zen Mountains/Zen Streets'' and ''Songs for a Suffering World'', both, audio CDs of his poetry, with the music of jazz bassist and composer [[William Parker (musician)|William Parker]] and drummer [[Hamid Drake]] were released on the Boxholder Records label in 1999 and 2003. Inspired by ancient Chinese and Japanese reclusive poets, Budbill kept alive a discourse about his struggles living a simple life in a complex modern time. [[Garrison Keillor]] read frequently from David's poems on ''[[The Writer's Almanac]]'' on [[NPR|National Public Radio]] (NPR).<ref>{{Cite news |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/10/02/books/david-budbill-dead.html?_r=0 |title=David Budbill, a Poet of Small-Town Vermont, Dies at 76 |last=Grimes |first=William |date=September 30, 2016 |newspaper=[[The New York Times]] |access-date=March 2, 2017}}</ref>

Among his honors and prizes were his first Honorary Doctor of Humane Letters from [[New England College]], in Henniker, New Hampshire, in January 2009. His other prizes and honors include: a Guggenheim Fellowship in Poetry in 1981, a National Endowment for the Arts Play Writing Fellowship in 1991, The [[Dorothy Canfield Fisher Children's Book Award]] in 1980, and The Vermont Arts Council's Walter Cerf Award for Lifetime Achievement in the Arts in 2002. In November 2009, Budbill was inducted as a Fellow into the Vermont Academy of Arts and Letters. In 2011, he received the Kjell Meling Memorial Award for Distinction in the Arts & Humanities, presented by Pennsylvania State University/Altoona.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.davidbudbill.com/biog.html#long |title=Biography of David Budbill |website=davidbudbill.com |publisher=David Budbill |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120312155528/http://www.davidbudbill.com/biog.html#long |archive-date=March 12, 2012 |access-date=March 3, 2017}}</ref> He was also the recipient of a [[National Endowment for the Arts]] fellowship in playwriting, a 1982 [[Guggenheim Fellowship]] in poetry, and a Dorothy Canfield Fisher Award for fiction.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.nec.edu/people-profiles/poet-david-budbill/?searchterm=None |title=Poet David Budbill Honored |website=nec.edu |publisher=New England College |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120305164830/http://www.nec.edu/people-profiles/poet-david-budbill/?searchterm=None |archive-date=March 5, 2012 |access-date=March 3, 2017}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.poets.org/poetsorg/poet/david-budbill|title=David Budbill|last=|first=|date=|website=Poets.org|publisher=[[Academy of American Poets]]|access-date=}}</ref>

==Personal life and death==
Budbill lived in the mountains of northern Vermont with his wife, painter Lois Eby; their daughter is the poet Nadine Wolf Budbill.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rherald.com/news/2003-08-14/Arts/a05.html|title=Three Generations of Vermont Poets at Strafford Town House - Randolph Herald|publisher=Rherald.com|accessdate=5 October 2014}}</ref>
His papers are held at [[University of Vermont]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://cdi.uvm.edu/findingaids/collection/budbilld.ead.xml|title=David Budbill Papers|publisher=Cdi.uvm.edu|accessdate=5 October 2014}}</ref>

In 1968, Budbill signed the "[[Writers and Editors War Tax Protest]]" pledge, vowing to refuse tax payments in protest against the Vietnam War.<ref>"Writers and Editors War Tax Protest" January 30, 1968 ''New York Post''</ref>

Budbill died at the age of 76 with his family by his side at his home in Montpelier at 12:30am September 25, 2016 from [[Parkinson's disease]].<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://vtdigger.org/2016/09/26/vermont-writer-david-budbill-dies-age-76/ |title=Vermont Writer David Budbill Dies at Age 76 |accessdate=26 September 2016 |work=vtdigger.org|date=26 September 2016 }}</ref> He was posthumously named The People's Poet of Vermont by the Vermont legislature.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Frothingham|first1=Nat|title=Late Poet David Budbill To Be Honored|url=http://www.montpelierbridge.com/2017/02/late-poet-david-budbill-to-be-honored/|accessdate=3 March 2018|work=The Montpelier Bridge|date=February 17, 2017}}</ref>
