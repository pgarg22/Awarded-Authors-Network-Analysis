{{short description|American baseball player, soldier, civil rights campaigner, and poet}}
'''Simpson Charles Younger''' (May 17, 1850 – May 14, 1943)<ref name="Obit">"Civil War Veteran Dies", ''[[The Sedalia Democrat]]'' (May 16, 1943), p. 11.</ref> was a baseball player, soldier in the [[Union Army]] during the [[American Civil War]], Civil Rights campaigner, and poet. An African American, he was one of the youngest soldiers to serve in the Union Army and one of the last to remain living.<ref name="Obit"/><ref name="SUVCW">{{cite web|url=http://www.suvcwmo.org/uploads/3/0/9/6/30960503/pettis_co_-_simpson_charles_younger_-_27th_usct.pdf|title=Younger, Simpson Charles|publisher=Sons of Union Veterans of the Civil War|access-date=November 7, 2019}}</ref>

His father was Charles Lee Younger (grandfather of the Younger brothers of the [[James–Younger Gang]]) and his mother, Elizabeth, was one of his father's slaves. He died in 1854.<ref name="MoSOS"/> Younger was manumitted by his father's will and with his sister sent to study at Oberlin.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://martincitytelegraph.com/2019/02/26/an-ode-to-the-darker-youngers/|title=An Ode to the Darker Youngers|first=Martin City|last=Telegraph|date=February 26, 2019}}</ref><ref name="MoSOS">{{cite web|url=https://www.sos.mo.gov/default.aspx?PageId=4746|title=Charles Lee Younger, His Life and Legacy, Focus of Missouri State Archives Program|publisher=Missouri Department of State|date=November 5, 2001}}</ref>

Younger played baseball for [[Oberlin College]] and was a pitcher.<ref name="SUVCW"/> He also pitched for various teams including the Penfields, Resolutes, and all-black professional team the Zulus.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://ourgame.mlblogs.com/when-cleveland-and-baseball-were-young-65efc76121ff|title=When Cleveland and Baseball Were Young|first=John|last=Thorn|date=November 18, 2018|website=Medium}}</ref> He was interviewed about his experiences. On the Resolutes, a team "formed from the best players" from two other clubs in 1868, Younger was "the only black man on the only integrated club in the Western Reserve".<ref name="Egan">James M. Egan, Jr., ''Base Ball on the Western Reserve'' (2008), p. 27.</ref> He was categorized at the time as a [[quadroon]].

Younger sued a theater for denying he and a companion access to theater seats because they were black.<ref>"A Theater is Private Property", ''Greenleaf Journal'' (February 6, 1890), p. 2.</ref> The case went to the [[Missouri Supreme Court]] where, in 1892, Justice [[Francis Marion Black]] with all other justices in concurrence ruled for a standard of "[[separate but equal]]" as was custom even after passage of the [[Fourteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://newsantafetrailer.blogspot.com/2019/02/an-ode-to-darker-youngers.html|title=The New Santa Fe Trailer: An Ode to the Darker Youngers|first=Diane|last=Euston|date=February 25, 2019}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=_Q48AAAAIAAJ&q=simpson+c.+younger+missouri+supreme+court+black&pg=PA1109|title=The South Western Reporter|date=November 7, 1892|publisher=West Publishing Company|via=Google Books}}</ref>

Younger died days before his 93rd birthday, and had eight surviving children at the time of his death.<ref name="Obit"/>
