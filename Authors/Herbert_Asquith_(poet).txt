'''Herbert Dixon Asquith''' (11 March 1881 &ndash; 5 August 1947) was an English poet, novelist, and lawyer.<ref name="times">{{cite news |title=Mr. Herbert Asquith – Poet and Novelist|work=[[The Times]] |page= 7|date=8 August 1947 }}</ref> Nicknamed "Beb" by his family, he was the second son of [[H. H. Asquith]], British [[Prime Minister]], with whom he is frequently confused{{by whom?|date=May 2021}}, and the younger brother of [[Raymond Asquith]].<ref name="times"/>

Asquith was greatly affected by his service with the [[Royal Artillery]] in [[World War I]].<ref>{{cite journal |last1=Guest |first1=Philip |last2=Guest |first2=Wendy |year= 2012|title=A Prime Minister and his Family at War: Part II |journal= Siegfried's Journal|volume= 22|issue= Summer 2012|pages= 17–23|publisher= Siegfried Sassoon Fellowship}}</ref> His poems included "The Volunteer" and "The Fallen Subaltern", the latter being a tribute to fallen soldiers. His poem "Soldiers at Peace" was set to music by [[Ina Boyle]]. His novels include the best-selling ''Young Orland'' (set during and after the First World War), ''Wind's End'', ''Mary Dallon'', and ''Roon''.<ref name="times"/>

In 1910, he married [[Lady Cynthia Asquith|Lady Cynthia Charteris]], who was also a writer. She was the eldest daughter of [[Hugo Charteris, 11th Earl of Wemyss]], and his wife, [[Mary Constance Wyndham]].<ref name="times"/> They had three sons.{{cn|date=September 2021}}
