'''Martin Greif''', born '''Friedrich Hermann Frey''' (18 June 1839 – 1 April 1911) was a German freelance writer of poems and of dramas which were performed at the [[Burgtheater]] in Vienna and the [[National Theatre Munich|Bavarian Court Theatre]] in Munich. His songs inspired compositions by [[Max Reger]] and [[Alban Berg]], among others.

== Career ==
Friedrich Hermann Frey was born in [[Speyer]], the son of {{ill|Max Frey (Beamter)|de|Max Frey (Regierungsdirektor)|lt=Max Frey}},<ref name="Bartleby" /> who had served as ''Kabinettsrat'' of [[Otto of Greece]], and his wife Adelheid Friederike, née Ehrmann. The family moved to Munich, where he made the [[Abitur]]. He joined the Bavarian military and was promoted to ''Offizier'' in 1859. He retired from the military in 1867 to live as a freelance writer.<ref name="Britting" /> His first publications, enabled by [[Eduard Mörike]] were poems, published in 1868 by [[Cotta (publishing house)|''Cotta’sche Verlagsbuchhandlung'']] unter the pen name Martin Greif, which he used as his official name from 1882 on.<ref name="Britting" /> In 1869 he moved to Vienna, where many of his plays were successfully performed at the [[Burgtheater]], thank to its artistic director [[Heinrich Laube]]. When Laube left the post, Greif returned to Munich and wrote patriotic dramas performed at the [[National Theatre Munich|Bavarian Court Theatre]].

He died in [[Kufstein]].<ref name="Britting" />

== Work ==
Greif's drama on [[Agnes Bernauer]] has remained in the repertory of Bavarian lay theatre. Several of Greif's poems appeared in anthologies such as ''Hausbuch Deutscher Lyrik''.<ref name="Bailey" /> His poem "Das klagende Lied" possibly inspired Gustav Mahler's [[Das klagende Lied|cantata of the same name]], written from 1878 on with a libretto by the composer.<ref name="Mitchell" /> Several of Greif's poems were set to music,<ref name="Lesekost" /> for example "Der zerrißne Grabkranz" by [[Max Reger]] as part of his [[Sechs Lieder, Op. 4|''Sechs Lieder'', Op. 4]].<ref name="Reger" /> Alban Berg set his poem "Schattenleben" ("Phantom Life") in 1903 among his early songs,<ref name="Adams" /> Anton Webern set his poem "Hochsommernacht".<ref name="Bailey" />
