'''Juan del Encina''' (July 12, 1468 – 1529 or 1530)<ref name="Slonimsky">Slonimsky, Nicolas, ed. Baker's Biographical Dictionary of Musicians seventh edition. London, England: Collier Macmillan Publishers, 1984: 662</ref> was a composer, poet, and playwright,<ref name="Magill">Magill, Frank N., ed. Critical Survey of Drama: Foreign Language Series  v2. Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey: Salem Press, 1986.</ref>{{rp|535}} often called the founder, along with [[Gil Vicente]], of Iberian drama.<ref name="Slonimsky"/> His birth name was '''Juan de Fermoselle'''.<ref name="Slonimsky"/> He spelled his name '''Enzina''', but this is not a significant difference; it is two spellings of the same sound, in a time when "correct spelling" as we know it barely existed.

==Life==
He was born in 1468 near [[Salamanca]],<ref name="Slonimsky"/> probably at [[Encina de San Silvestre]], one of at least 7 known children of Juan de Fermoselle, a shoemaker, and his wife.<ref name="Pope"/> He was of [[Jewish]] [[converso]] descent.<ref name="Slonimsky"/><ref>See, Norman Roth, "Conversos, Inquisition, and the Expulsion of the Jews from Spain", Madison, WI: The University of Wisconsin Press, 1995, pp. 157, 176–178.</ref> After leaving [[Salamanca University]] sometime in 1492<ref name="Slonimsky"/> he became a member of the household of Don Fadrique de Toledo, the second [[Duke of Alba]], although some sources believe that he did not work for the Duke of Alba until 1495.<ref name="Pope">Pope, Isabel; Tess Knighton. ''Encina, Juan del''. Sadie, Stanley, ed. The New Grove Dictionary of Music and Musicians 2nd edition. V8. New York City: Grove’s Dictionaries Inc., 2001: 194</ref> A plausible argument is that his first post was as a Corregidor in northern Spain.<ref name="Pope"/>

Fermoselle was a Chaplain at the Salamanca Cathedral in the early 1490s.<ref name="Pope"/> It was here that he changed his name from Juan de Fermoselle to Juan del Enzina, or Encina (meaning [[Quercus rotundifolia|holm oak]]) during his stay as Chaplain.<ref name="Pope"/> He was later forced to resign as Chaplain because he was not ordained.<ref name="Pope"/>

==Works==
[[File:Enzina.gif|250px|thumb|Book cover of his [[chansonnier]]'s first edition (1496): ''Cancionero de todas las obras de Juan del Enzina con otras cosas nueuamente añadidas'']]
In 1492 the poet entertained his patron with a dramatic piece, the ''Triunfo de la fama'', written to commemorate the fall of [[Granada]].<ref name="Pope"/> In 1496 he published his ''Cancionero'',<ref name="Pope"/> a collection of dramatic and lyrical poems. He then applied for the cantor post at [[Salamanca Cathedral]], but the position was divided among three singers, including his rival Lucas Fernandez.<ref name="Pope"/>

While working for the Duke of Alba, Encina was the program director, along with Lucas Fernandez.<ref name="Magill"/>{{rp|537}} Here Encina wrote pastoral eclogues, the foundation of Spanish secular drama.<ref name="Slonimsky"/> Encina's plays are predominantly based on shepherds and unrequited love.<ref name="Magill"/>{{rp|540}}

Encina was ambitious, looking to be promoted based on preferment,<ref name="Pope"/> so around 1500 he relocated to Rome, where he apparently served in the musical establishments of several cardinals or noblemen.<ref name="Slonimsky"/> Encina was appointed to the Archdiaconate of [[Malaga Cathedral]] by Julius II in 1508.<ref name="Pope"/>

In 1518 he resigned from position at Malaga for a simple benefice at Moron, and the following year he went to [[Jerusalem]],<ref name="Pope"/> where he sang his first [[Mass (liturgy)|mass]].<ref name="Pope"/> He also wrote about the events during his pilgrimage to Jerusalem in ''Tribagia o Via Sacra de Hierusalem''.<ref name="Slonimsky"/> In 1509 he had held a lay [[Canon (priest)|canonry]] at [[Málaga]];<ref name="Pope"/> in 1519 he was appointed by Leon for the priorship of [[Leon Cathedral]].<ref name="Slonimsky"/> His last job was recorded as being in Leon, where he is thought to have died towards the end of 1529.<ref name="Magill"/>{{rp|438}}

His ''Cancionero'' is preceded by a prose treatise (''Arte de trobar'') on the condition of the poetic art in Spain. His fourteen dramatic pieces mark the transition from the purely [[ecclesiastical]] to the secular stage.<ref name="Magill"/>{{rp|539}} The ''Aucto del Repelón'' and the ''Égloga de Fileno'' dramatize the adventures of [[shepherd]]s;<ref name="Magill"/>{{rp|540}} the latter, like ''Plácida y Vitoriano'', is strongly influenced by the ''[[La Celestina|Celestina]]''. The intrinsic interest of Encina's plays is slight, but they are important from the historical point of view, for the lay pieces form a new departure, and the devout [[eclogue]]s prepare the way for the ''[[auto (Spanish literature)|autos]]'' of the 17th century. Moreover, Encina's lyrical poems are remarkable for their intense sincerity and devout grace.

Even though his works were dedicated to royal families, he never served as a member of a royal chapel.<ref name="Pope"/> And even though Encina worked in many Cathedrals and was ordained as a priest, no religious musical works are known to still exist.<ref name="Slonimsky"/> Most of his works were done by his mid-30s,<ref name="Pope"/> some 60 or more songs attributed to Encina, and another 9 settings of texts on top of that, to which the music could also be added, but not for certain.<ref name="Pope"/> Many of the surviving pieces are villancicos, of which he was a leading composer.<ref name="Pope"/> The Spanish villancico is the equivalent of the Italian Frottola.<ref name="Pope"/> There are three and four voice settings that offer a variety of styles depending on the kind of text, with very limited movements in the voices in preparation for the cadence points.<ref name="Pope"/> To make the text heard clearly, Encina used varied and flexible rhythms that are patterned on the accents of the verse, and used simple yet strong harmonic progressions.<ref name="Pope"/>

==Leonese times==
Encina held the priorship of Leon Cathedral from November 1523 until his final illness in December 1529.<ref name="Slonimsky"/> Juan del Encina's will was presented on January 14, 1530, so the exact date of his death is not known, but it is thought to be in late 1529 or early 1530.<ref name="Pope"/> In his will he noted that he wanted to be buried beneath the choir of Salamanca Cathedral,<ref name="Pope"/> and in 1534 his remains were taken to the Cathedral.<ref name="Magill"/>{{rp|538}}

===Leonese language influence===
Juan del Encina wrote in Castillian with [[Leonese language]] influences<ref>López Morales, "Elementos leoneses en la lengua del teatro pastoril de los siglos XV y XVI". 1967</ref>{{full citation needed|date=November 2018}} in his pastoral [[eclogue]]s. He was from Salamanca, a Leonese-speaking region, and eventually arrived at the capital of the long-vanished [[Kingdom of León]], where he died.

He was also a songwriter for the church.{{fact|date=October 2018}}
