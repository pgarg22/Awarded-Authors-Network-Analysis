'''Emma Donoghue''' (born 24 October 1969) is an [[Irish people|Irish]]-[[Canadians|Canadian]] playwright, literary historian, novelist, and screenwriter. Her 2010 novel ''[[Room (novel)|Room]]'' was a finalist for the [[Man Booker Prize]]<ref name="themanbookerprize.com">{{cite web |url=http://www.themanbookerprize.com/news/stories/1451 |title=News &#124; The Man Booker Prizes |website=Themanbookerprize.com |access-date=2016-01-14 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160202191241/http://themanbookerprize.com/news/stories/1451 |archive-date=2 February 2016 |df=dmy-all }}</ref> and an international best-seller. Donoghue's 1995 novel ''[[Hood (novel)|Hood]]'' won the [[Stonewall Book Award]]<ref>{{cite web|title=Stonewall Book Awards List|url=http://www.ala.org/glbtrt/award/honored#1997|publisher=American Library Association}}</ref> and ''Slammerkin'' (2000) won the [[Ferro-Grumley Award]] for [[Lesbian literature|Lesbian Fiction]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.publishingtriangle.org/awards.asp#Ferro|website=publishingtriangle.org|publisher=Publishing Triangle|access-date=2 October 2014|title=Awards}}</ref> She is a 2011 recipient of the [[Alex Awards]]. ''[[Room (novel)|Room]]'' was adapted by Donoghue into a [[Room (2015 film)|film of the same name]]. For this, she was nominated for the [[Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay]].

==Background==
Donoghue was born in [[Dublin]], Ireland, in 1969.<ref name="donoghue">{{cite web|url=http://www.emmadonoghue.com/emmadonoghue.htm|title=Emma Donoghue&nbsp;— Bio |access-date=2 September 2008|publisher=Official site |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100324232059/http://www.emmadonoghue.com/emmadonoghue.htm |archive-date=24 March 2010}}</ref> The youngest of eight children, she is the daughter of Frances (born Rutledge) and academic and literary critic [[Denis Donoghue (academic)|Denis Donoghue]].<ref name="js">{{Cite news|last=Stoffman|first=Judy|title=Writer has a deft touch with sexual identities|newspaper=[[Toronto Star]]|date=13 January 2007|url=https://www.thestar.com/article/170177|access-date=5 October 2009|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100821103433/http://www.thestar.com/article/170177|archive-date=21 August 2010|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref name="donoghue"/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.picador.com/blog/july-2010/the-a-z-of-emma-donoghue |title=A to Z of Emma Donoghue, author |website=Picador.com |date=2010-07-20 |access-date=2016-01-14}}</ref> She has a first-class honours Bachelor of Arts degree from [[University College Dublin]] (in English and French) and a PhD in English from [[Girton College, Cambridge]]. While at Cambridge she lived in a women's co-operative, an experience which inspired her short story ''The Welcome''.<ref name="official writings">{{cite web|url=http://www.emmadonoghue.com/writings.htm|title=Emma Donoghue&nbsp;— Writings|access-date=14 January 2016|publisher=emmadonoghue.com|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160304215933/http://www.emmadonoghue.com/writings.htm|archive-date=4 March 2016|url-status=dead|df=dmy-all}}</ref> Her thesis was on friendship between men and women in 18th-century fiction.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Richards|first=Linda|author-link=Linda L. Richards|title=Interview|newspaper=[[January Magazine]]|date=November 2000|url=http://januarymagazine.com/profiles/donoghue.html|access-date=5 October 2009}}</ref>

At Cambridge, she met her future wife Christine Roulston, a Canadian who is now professor of French and Women's Studies at the [[University of Western Ontario]]. They moved permanently to Canada in 1998 and Donoghue became a Canadian citizen in 2004.<ref name="js"/> She lives in [[London, Ontario]], with Roulston and their two children.<ref name="donoghue"/><ref>{{cite web|last1=McEvoy|first1=Marc|title=Interview: Emma Donoghue|date=4 April 2014|url=http://www.smh.com.au/entertainment/books/interview-emma-donoghue-20140403-35zqr.html}}</ref>

===''Stir Fry''===
Donoghue's first novel was 1994's ''Stir Fry'', a contemporary [[Bildungsroman|coming of age novel]] about a young Irish woman discovering her sexuality.<ref name="glbtq">{{cite web |last=Keehnen |first=Owen |title=Future Perfect: Talking With Irish Lesbian Author Emma Donoghue |publisher=glbtq.com |year=1994 |url=http://www.glbtq.com/sfeatures/interviewedonoghue.html |access-date=5 October 2009 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101107033016/http://www.glbtq.com/sfeatures/interviewedonoghue.html |archive-date=7 November 2010 |df=dmy }}</ref> It was a finalist for the [[Lambda Literary Award]] in 1994.<ref name="official writings"/> This was followed in 1995 by ''[[Hood (novel)|Hood]]'', another contemporary story, this time about an Irish woman coming to terms with the death of her girlfriend.<ref name="glbtq"/> ''Hood'' won the 1997 [[American Library Association]]'s Gay, Lesbian and Bisexual Book Award for Literature (now known as the [[Stonewall Book Award]] for Literature).<ref name="official writings"/>

===''Slammerkin''===
{{main|Slammerkin}}
''Slammerkin'' (2000) is a historical novel set in London and Wales. Inspired by an 18th-century newspaper story about a young servant who killed her employer and was executed, the protagonist is a prostitute who longs for fine clothes.<ref name="official writings"/><ref>{{Cite news|last1=Hagestadt|first1=Emma|last2=Hirst|first2=Christopher|title=Slammerkin by Emma Donoghue|newspaper=[[The Independent]]|date=8 May 2001|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/arts-entertainment/books/reviews/islammerkini-by-emma-donoghue-virago-acircpound799-422pp-685201.html|access-date=5 October 2009}}{{dead link|date=August 2021|bot=medic}}{{cbignore|bot=medic}}</ref> It was a finalist in the 2001 ''[[Irish Times]]'' Irish Literature Prize for Fiction and was awarded the 2002 [[Ferro-Grumley Award]] for Lesbian Fiction (despite a lack of lesbian content).<ref name="official writings"/><ref>{{cite book|last=Gonzalez|first=Alexander G.|title=Irish women writers: an A-to-Z guide|publisher=[[Greenwood Publishing Group]]|year=2006|pages=98–101|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=quocyNYLbLcC|isbn=0-313-32883-8}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last=O'Neill |first=Heather Aimee |title=Interview With Emma Donoghue |newspaper=AfterEllen.com |date=12 January 2008 |url=http://www.afterellen.com/people/2008/1/emmadonoghue |access-date=5 October 2009 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090515161522/http://www.afterellen.com/people/2008/1/emmadonoghue |archive-date=15 May 2009 |df=dmy }}</ref> Her 2007 novel, ''Landing'', portrays a long-distance relationship between a Canadian curator and an Irish flight attendant.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Brownrigg|first=Sylvia|author-link=Sylvia Brownrigg|title=In-Flight Moves|newspaper=[[The New York Times]]|date=22 July 2007|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2007/07/22/books/review/Brownrigg-t.html|access-date=5 October 2009}}</ref>

===''The Sealed Letter''===
''The Sealed Letter'' (2008), another work of historical fiction, is based on the Codrington Affair, a scandalous divorce case that gripped Britain in 1864. The protagonist is [[Emily Faithfull]].<ref>{{cite web|last1=Donoghue|first1=Emma|title=The Sealed Letter: Author's Note|url=http://www.picador.com/blog/october-2011/the-sealed-letter-author-s-note|website=Picador|publisher=Picador|access-date=21 November 2014}}</ref> ''The Sealed Letter'' was longlisted for the [[Giller Prize]],<ref>{{cite web|title=Past Winners|url=http://www.scotiabankgillerprize.ca/about/past-winners/|website=Scotiabank Giller Prize|access-date=3 May 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160303191755/http://www.scotiabankgillerprize.ca/about/past-winners/|archive-date=3 March 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref> and was joint winner, with [[Chandra Mayor]]'s ''All the Pretty Girls'', of the [[2009 Lambda Literary Awards|2009 Lambda Literary Award]] for Lesbian Fiction.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Cerna|first1=Antonio Gonzalez|title=21st Annual Lambda Literary Awards|url=http://www.lambdaliterary.org/winners-finalists/02/18/lambda-literary-awards-2008-2/|website=Lambda Literary|date=18 February 2010}}</ref>

===''Room''===
{{main|Room (novel)|l1=Room}} 
On 27 July 2010, Donoghue's novel ''[[Room (novel)|Room]]'' was longlisted for the [[Man Booker Prize]] and on 7 September 2010 it made the [[List of winners and shortlisted authors of the Booker Prize for Fiction|shortlist]].<ref name="themanbookerprize.com"/> On 2 November 2010, it was announced that ''Room'' had been awarded the [[Rogers Writers' Trust Fiction Prize]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.writerstrust.com/Awards/Rogers-Writers--Trust-Fiction-Prize/PrizeHistoryandGuidelines/Prize-History.aspx |title=The Writers' Trust of Canada - Prize History |website=Writerstrust.com |access-date=2016-01-14}}</ref> ''Room'' was also shortlisted for the [[2010 Governor General's Awards]] in Canada,<ref>[https://www.theglobeandmail.com/books/emma-donoghue-kathleen-winter-make-gg-short-list/article1754896 "Emma Donoghue, Kathleen Winter make GG short list"]. ''[[The Globe and Mail]]'', 13 October 2010.</ref> and was the winner of the [[Irish Book Awards|Irish Book Award]] 2010. It was short-listed for the [[Orange Prize for Fiction]] 2011,<ref>{{cite web |first=Mark |last=Brown |date=8 June 2011 |url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2011/jun/08/orange-prize-2011-tea-obreht |title=Orange prize 2011 goes to Téa Obreht |website=[[The Guardian]] |quote=Her victory meant defeat for Emma Donoghue – bookies' favourite for the bestselling Room |access-date=2016-09-26}}</ref> but lost out to [[Tea Obreht]]. She later wrote the screenplay for a film version of the book, ''[[Room (2015 film)|Room]]'' (2015), for which she was nominated for an Academy Award, Golden Globe and Bafta Award,<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt3170832/awards |title=Room - Awards - IMDb  |website=www.imdb.com |access-date=2016-01-31}}</ref> and in 2017 adapted it into a play performed at the [[Abbey Theatre]] in Dublin.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.abbeytheatre.ie/whats_on/event/room|title=Abbey Theatre - Whats on - Room  |website=www.abbeytheatre.com |access-date=2017-07-05}}</ref>

===''Frog Music''===
Her novel ''Frog Music'', a historical fiction based on the true story of a murdered 19th-century cross-dressing frog catcher, was published in 2014.

===''The Wonder''===
Donoghue's 2016 novel ''The Wonder'' was shortlisted for the [[Scotiabank Giller Prize]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://scotiabankgillerprize.ca/the-scotiabank-giller-prize-presents-its-2016-shortlist/|title=The Scotiabank Giller Prize Presents Its 2016 Shortlist - Scotiabank Giller Prize|website=scotiabankgillerprize.ca|language=en-US|access-date=2018-10-23}}</ref>

Shooting of a film of the novel is due to start in September 2021. Directed by [[Sebastián Lelio]], the screenplay is by Donoghue and [[Alice Birch]], with [[Florence Pugh]] in the leading role.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.thetimes.co.uk/article/netflix-film-based-on-dublin-writer-emma-donoghues-novel-to-be-made-in-ireland-7swjzvnn9|title=Netflix film based on Dublin writer Emma Donoghue's novel to be made in Ireland|last1=Corr|first1=Julieanne}}</ref><ref>https://entertainment.ie/movies/movie-news/florence-pugh-ireland-the-wonder-490978/</ref>

===''The Pull of the Stars''===
{{main|The Pull of the Stars}}
Her novel ''The Pull of the Stars'' (2020), written in 2018-2019, was published earlier than originally planned because it was set in the [[Spanish flu|1918 influenza pandemic]] in [[Dublin]], Ireland. All the characters were fictional except Dr [[Kathleen Lynn]].<ref name="Woman'sHour21July2020">{{cite web |title=Woman's Hour |url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/sounds/play/m000l0r5 |website=BBC |access-date=26 July 2020}}</ref> The novel received strongly positive reviews from critics<ref>{{Cite web|title=The Pull of the Stars {{!}} Book Marks|url=https://bookmarks.reviews/reviews/the-pull-of-the-stars/|access-date=2020-12-26|language=en-US}}</ref> and was longlisted for the Giller Prize in 2020.<ref>Deborah Dundas, [https://www.thestar.com/entertainment/books/2020/09/08/thomas-king-emma-donoghue-make-the-2020-giller-longlist-in-a-year-marked-by-firsts.html "Thomas King, Emma Donoghue make the 2020 Giller Longlist in a year marked by firsts"]. ''[[Toronto Star]]'', September 8, 2020.</ref>
