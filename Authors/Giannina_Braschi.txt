'''Giannina Braschi''' (born February 5, 1953) is a [[Puerto Rico|Puerto Rican]] poet, novelist, [[dramatist]], and scholar.  Her notable works include ''[[Empire of Dreams (poetry collection)|Empire of Dreams]]'' (1988), ''[[Yo-Yo Boing!]]'' (1998) ''and [[United States of Banana]]'' (2011).

Braschi writes [[Cross-genre|cross-genre literature]] and political philosophy in Spanish, [[Spanglish]], and English.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://pen.org/user/giannina-braschi/|title=PEN World Voices: Giannina Braschi|date=2012-08-09|website=PEN America|language=en|access-date=2020-04-30}}</ref> Her work is a hybrid of poetry, [[Metafiction|fiction]], [[Postdramatic theatre|theatre]], memoire, manifesto, and [[political philosophy|philosophy]].<ref name=":5">{{Cite web|url=https://upittpress.org/books/9780822946182/|title=Poets, Philosophers, Lovers: On the Writings of Giannina Braschi|last=Aldama|first=Frederick Luis|website=upittpress.org|access-date=2020-04-30}}</ref>  Her writings explore the enculturation journey of Hispanic immigrants, and dramatize the three main political options of Puerto Rico: independence, colony, and state.<ref>Aldama, Frederick, and Christopher González. Latinx Studies: The Key Concepts. Routledge, 2018.</ref><ref name=":6">{{cite book |last1=Perisic |first1=Alexandra |title=Precarious Crossings: Immigration, Neoliberalism, and the Atlantic |date=November 2020 |publisher=The Ohio State University Press |location=Ohio |isbn=978-0-8142-5552-0 |pages=152–173 |hdl=1811/88397 }}</ref>

==Early life==

Giannina Braschi was born to an upper-class family in San Juan, Puerto Rico.<ref>{{cite book |last1=Gonzalez |first1=Christopher |title=Permissible Narratives: The Promise of Latino/a Literature |date=2017 |publisher=The Ohio State University Press |location=Ohio |isbn=978-0-8142-5441-7 |pages=93–95 |url=https://ohiostatepress.org/books/titles/9780814213506.html}}</ref><ref name=":0">{{Cite journal|last=Rivera|first=Carmen H.|date=2011|title=El poder de la palabra y la experiencia transnacional: una entrevista con Giannina Braschi|url=https://revistas.upr.edu/index.php/opcit/article/view/7892|journal=Op. Cit. Revista del Centro de Investigaciones Históricas|language=es|issue=20|pages=181–201|issn=2578-5443}}</ref> In her teen years, she was a founding member of the [[San Juan Children's Choir]], a fashion model, and a tennis champion.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|last=Remeseira|first=Claudio Iván|date=August 25, 2013|title=Summer reads: brilliant takes on Nuyoricans, random murder and narco-literatura|url=http://nbclatino.com/2013/08/25/summer-reads-brilliant-takes-on-nuyoricans-random-murder-and-narco-literatura/|website=NBC Latino}}</ref> Her father Euripides ("Pilo") Braschi was also a tennis champion.<ref name=":0" /><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.s9.com/Biography/giannina-braschi/|title=Braschi Family Biographies|date=2015-08-08|website=Biographical Dictionary - s9.com|language=en-US|access-date=2020-04-30}}</ref>

In the 1970s, Braschi studied literature and philosophy in Madrid, Rome, Rouen, and London, before she settled in [[New York City]].<ref name=":1" /> She credited her start in poetry to the older Spanish poets who mentored her when she lived in Madrid: Claudio Rodríguez, [[Carlos Bousoño]], [[Vicente Aleixandre|Vicente Alexandre]], and Blas de Otero.<ref name=":0" /><ref name="auto" />

==Academic career==

With a PhD in Hispanic Literatures from [[State University of New York, Stony Brook]] (1980), Braschi was a professor at [[Rutgers University]], [[City University of New York]], and [[Colgate University]].<ref name=":2" /> Braschi has received awards and fellowships from institutions including [[Ford Foundation]], Danforth Scholarship, [[National Endowment for the Arts]], [[New York Foundation for the Arts]], Rutgers University, and PEN, among others.<ref>{{Cite book|last=Sommer|first=Doris|title=Introduction to Yo-Yo Boing!|publisher=Latin American Literary Review Press|year=1998|isbn=978-0-935480-97-9|location=Pittsburgh}}</ref>  She published a book on the poetry of [[Gustavo Adolfo Becquer]] and essays on [[Miguel de Cervantes|Cervantes]], [[Garcilaso de la Vega (poet)|Garcilaso]], [[César Vallejo]], [[Juan Ramon Jimenez]] and [[Federico García Lorca]].<ref name=":6" />

==Literary career==

Braschi's work is situated in the Latino [[avant-garde]], a "burgeoning body of work that testifies to [[Latino literature|Latino writers]]’ abiding interest in the avant-garde as a means for engaging ideas of material, social relevance".<ref name=":4">{{cite journal |last1=Riofrio |first1=John |title=Falling for debt: Giannina Braschi, the Latinx avant-garde, and financial terrorism in the United fields of [[Postcolonial literature|Postcolonial]], [[Postmodern literature|Postmodern]], and [[Nuyorican movement|Nuyorican]] literatures, as well as Latino political philosophy.<ref name=":5" /> Braschi is considered a "revolutionary voice" in contemporary [[Latin American literature]]".<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.loc.gov/bookfest/author/giannina_braschi|title=Giannina === Spanish: ''El Imperio de los sueños'' ===
In the 1980s, Braschi wrote dramatic poetry in Spanish prose in New York City.<ref name=":3">{{cite journal|last1=Cruz-Malave|first1=Arnaldo|date=September 1, 2014|title=Under the Skirt of Liberty: Giannina Braschi Rewrites Empire|url=https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283628679|journal=American Quarterly|volume=66|issue=3|pages=801–818|doi=10.1353/aq.2014.0042|s2cid=144702640}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|last=Loustau|first=Laura|title=Cuerpos errantes: Literatura latina y latinoamericana en Estados Unidos (Luisa Valenzuela and Giannina Braschi)|year=2002|location=Buenos Aires}}</ref> Her postmodern poetry titles were published in Barcelona, Spain, including: ''Asalto al tiempo'' (Assault on Time, 1980), ''La Comedia profana'' (Profane Comedy, 1985), and ''El Imperio de los sueños'' (''[[Empire of Dreams (poetry collection)|Empire of Dreams]]'', 1988).<ref name=":3" /> She was part of the [[Nuyorican]] movement.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Marting|first=Diane E.|date=2010|title=New/Nueva York in Giannina Braschi's "Poetic Egg": Fragile Identity, Postmodernism, and Globalization|journal=The Global South|volume=4|issue=1|pages=167–182|doi=10.2979/gso.2010.4.1.167|jstor=10.2979/gso.2010.4.1.167|s2cid=144342078|issn=1932-8648}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://nbclatino.com/2013/04/27/8-poets-disclose-their-favorite-lines-of-poetry/|title=8 Poets Disclose their Favorite Lines of Poetry (Nuyorican poet Giannina Braschi on Vallejo)|last=Puga|first=Kristina|date=2013-04-27|website=NBC Latino|language=en|access-date=2020-04-30}}</ref>  New York City is the site and subject of much of her poetry. In a climactic episode of Braschi's ''Empire of Dreams'', "[[Pastoral]] or the Inquisition of Memories", shepherds invade [[Fifth Avenue|5th Avenue]] during the [[Puerto Rican Day Parade]] and take over the City of New York; the shepherds ring the bells of [[St. Patrick's Cathedral, New York|St. Patrick's Cathedral]], and seize the observation deck of the [[Empire State Building]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Carrión|first=María|date=1996-01-01|title=Geography, (M)Other Tongues and the Role of Translation in Giannina Braschi's El imperio de los sueños|url=https://newprairiepress.org/sttcl/vol20/iss1/9|journal=Studies in 20th & 21st Century Literature|volume=20|issue=1|doi=10.4148/2334-4415.1385|issn=2334-4415}}</ref> Immigrant characters play the role of other characters, swapping names, genders, personal histories, and identities.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Kuebler|first=Carolyn|title=Review of Empire of Dreams|journal=Review of Contemporary Fiction|volume=15|issue=1}}</ref> [[Alicia Ostriker]] situates her gender-bending and genre-blending poetry as having a "sheer erotic energy that defies definition and dogma."<ref>Introduction to Giannina Braschi's ''Empire of Dreams'', Alicia Ostriker, New Haven: Yale University Press, 1994</ref>

=== Spanglish: ''Yo-Yo Boing! ''===
She published the first full-length [[Spanglish]] novel ''[[Yo-Yo Boing!]]''  in 1998. ''Yo-Yo Boing!'' explores "the lived experiences of urban life for Hispanics, as in the case with New York City, and her principal interest is in representing how individuals move in and out of different cultural coordinates, including one so crucial as language."<ref>''Lengua Fresca'', co-edited by [[Ilan Stavans]] and [[Harold Augenbraum]]</ref><ref>{{cite book |last1=Foster |first1=David William |title=Review of Yo-Yo Boing! |date=1999 |publisher=Review of Contemporary Fiction |location=McLean, Illinois |pages=202–203}}</ref> The book was written in an era of renewed calls for English-only laws, [[ethnic cleansing]] campaigns, and [[corporate censorship]].<ref>Introduction to Giannina Braschi's ''Yo-Yo Boing!'', Doris Sommer, Harvard University, 1998.</ref> "For decades, [[Dominican Republic|Dominican]] and [[Puerto Rican people|Puerto Rican]] authors have carried out a linguistic revolution", noted ''[[The Boston Globe]]'', "and Giannina Braschi, especially in her novel ''Yo-Yo Boing!'', testify to it".<ref>''The Boston Globe'', "Spanglish is everywhere now, which is no problema for some, but a pain in the cuello for purists", by [[Ilan Stavans]], 14 September 2003.</ref>

=== English: ''United States of Banana'' ===
Braschi published the geopolitical comic-tragedy ''[[United States of Banana]]'', her first book written entirely in English, in 2011.<ref name="auto">Sheeran, Amy, and Amanda M. Smith. "A Graphic Revolution: Talking Poetry & Politics with Giannina Braschi." ''Chiricú Journal: Latina/o Literatures, Arts, and Cultures''. 2.2 (2018): United States of 3-4.</ref><ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=https://poets.org/poet/giannina-braschi|title=About Giannina Braschi &#124; Academy of American Poets|first=Academy of American|last=Poets|website=poets.org}}</ref> It is a [[postmodern]] cross-genre work that opens with the collapse of the World Trade Center on [[9/11]].<ref name=":4" /> The work is a scathing critique of 21st-century [[capitalism]] and the [[War on terror|global war on terror]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Gonzalez|first=Madelena|title=United States of Banana (2011), Elizabeth Costello (2003) and Fury (2001): Portrait of the Writer as the 'Bad Subject' of Globalisation.|url=https://journals.openedition.org/ebc/1279|journal=Études britanniques contemporaines|year=2014|issue=46|doi=10.4000/ebc.1279|doi-access=free}}</ref> Subjects include [[Immigration to the United States|immigration]], [[Incarceration in the United States|mass incarceration]], [[Economic terrorism|financial terrorism]], colonial debt structures, and "power imbalances within the Americas."<ref name=":4" /> The work is celebratory of foreign influences.<ref>Braschi, Giannina, "What to Read Now: Mixed-Genre Literature", ''World Literature Today,'' September–October 2012 [http://www.worldliteraturetoday.com/2012/september/what-read-now-mixed-genre-literature-giannina-braschi] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120914075725/http://www.worldliteraturetoday.com/2012/september/what-read-now-mixed-genre-literature-giannina-braschi|date=2012-09-14}}</ref> Braschi stated in ''[[Evergreen Review]]'' that she considered herself "more French than [[Samuel Beckett|Beckett]], [[Pablo Picasso|Picasso]] and [[Gertrude Stein]]", and identifies as  the "granddaughter of [[Alfred Jarry]] and [[Antonin Artaud]], bastard child of [[Samuel Beckett]] and [[James Joyce]], half-sister to [[Heiner Müller]], kissing cousin of [[Tadeusz Kantor]], and lover of Witkiewicz".<ref>''The Evergreen Review's 50th Anniversary Edition'', (www.evergreenreview.com), Giannina Braschi, 2007.</ref>

==Adaptations and translations==

Giannina Braschi's texts have been adapted and applied to popular culture and fields such as television comedy, chamber music, comic books, industrial design, and ecological urban planning.<ref>O'Dwyer, Tess. ''Popping Up in Pop Culture and Other Unlikely Spaces: Latinx Author Giannina Braschi Crosses Over'' World Literature Today, Spring 2021.</ref> [[Michael Zansky]] has used Braschi's texts in his paintings and [[Michael Somoroff]] has created short films with her works. There is a theater play by Juan Pablo Felix and a graphic novel by [[Joakim Lindengren]] of [[United States of Banana]].<ref>{{cite book |last1=Somoroff |first1=Michael |title=Two Crowns of the Egg |url=https://www.lensculture.com/books/14630-michael-somoroff-two-crowns-of-the-egg |website=Lens Culture Magazine |publisher=Damiani |language=en}}</ref><ref>United States of Banana: A Postcolonial Dramatic Fiction, Juan Pablo Felix, Columbia University Stages, New York, 2015</ref><ref>{{cite journal |last1=Lindengren |first1=Joakim |title=Image from United States of Banana: A Comic Book, Cobolt, 2017 |journal=Chiricú Journal: Latina/o Literatures, Arts, and Cultures |date=2018 |volume= 2| issue =  2, 2018 |pages=3–4 |doi=10.2979/chiricu.2.2.02 |url=https://muse.jhu.edu/article/697732/summary}}</ref> Puerto Rican composer Gabriel Bouche Caro has composed chamber music works with her poems.<ref>Bouche Caro, Gabriel.''Latinx Spaces: On Turning Giannina Braschi's Poetry into a Song Cycle," Spring 2021)</ref> There is a namesake Giannina chair designed by American industrial designer Ian Stell.<ref>New York State Writers Institute, The Conversation. "Stell designed this kinetic device to exist as a functional hybrid between typologies and named it after Giannina Braschi.''December 7,2020.</ref> Her books have been translated into English by Tess O'Dwyer, into Spanish by Manuel Broncano, and into Swedish by [[Helena Eriksson]] and Hannah Nordenhok.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://poets.org/poet/tess-odwyer|title=About Tess O'Dwyer &#124; Academy of American Poets|first=Academy of American|last=Poets|website=poets.org}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://tranan.nu/bocker/drommarnas-imperium/|title=Drömmarnas imperium}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |last1=Smith |first1=Amanda M.|last2=Sheeran |first2=Amy |title=A Graphic Revolution: Talking Poetry & Politics with Giannina Braschi |journal=Chiricú Journal: Latina/o Literatures, Arts, and Cultures |date=Spring 2018 |volume=2|issue=2|pages=130–142 |doi=10.2979/chiricu.2.2.10 |jstor=10.2979/chiricu.2.2.10}}</ref>

==Political activism==

Braschi is an advocate for Puerto Rican independence.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://worldpolicy.org/2017/10/17/puerto-rico-i-took-liberties/|title=Puerto Rico: 'I Took Liberties' – World Policy|language=en-US|access-date=2020-04-30}}</ref> She declared the independence of Puerto Rico in [[United States of Banana]]<ref>{{Cite book|last=Stanchich|first=Maritza|title="Bilingual Big Bang: Giannina Braschi's Trilogy Levels the Spanish-English Playing Field". Poets, Philosophers, Lovers: On the Writings of Giannina Braschi. Frederick Luis Aldama, Ed.|publisher=University of Pittsburgh Press|year=2020|isbn=9780822946182|location=Pittsburgh}}</ref> and stated in the press that "[[Liberty]] is not an option — it is a human right."<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.elnuevodia.com/noticias/locales/nota/lalibertadnoesunaopcionesunderecho-1349719/|title=La libertad no es una opción, es un derecho|date=September 24, 2012|website=El Nuevo Dia}}</ref> In the 1990s, she protested the United States Navy's bombing exercises in [[Vieques]], along with politicians Rubén Berríos and Robert F. Kennedy, Jr., singers Danny Rivera and Willie Colón, and fellow authors [[Ana Lydia Vega]] and Rigoberta Menchú.<ref>{{Cite web|title=The Puerto Rican Struggle and the Plight of Political Prisoner Oscar Lopez Rivera|url=https://www.thepensivequill.com/2014/10/the-puerto-rican-struggle-and-plight-of.html|access-date=2020-08-25|website=TPQ}}</ref> Braschi spoke on a panel on "The New Censorship" at the [[PEN International|PEN]] 2012 World Voices Festival where she offered "a critique of 21st century capitalism in which [she] condemned corporate censorship and control."<ref>{{cite news|author=Roth, Larry|date=May 7, 2012|title=Rushdie Brings PEN Festival to Close|work=[[The New York Times]]|url=http://artsbeat.blogs.nytimes.com/2012/05/07/rushdie-brings-pen-festival-to-close/}}</ref> In July 2019, Braschi led early marches outside [[La Fortaleza]] in Old San Juan to demand the resignation of Puerto Rican Governor [[Ricardo Rossello]], and joined massive protests, with singers [[Bad Bunny]], [[Residente]], and [[Ricky Martin]], that led to the Governor's resignation.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Figueroa |first1=Jan Javier |title=Diferentes opiniones sobre manifestaciones en Fortaleza Las manifestaciones exigiendo la renuncia al gobernador, Ricardo Rosselló, continuarán mañana a las cinco de la tarde. |url=https://www.elvocero.com/gobierno/diferentes-opiniones-sobre-manifestaciones-en-fortaleza/article_8b643cc4-a821-11e9-b7d8-eb4abf0f4277.html |access-date=July 16, 2019 |agency=El Vocero |publisher=El Vocero, Puerto Rican News |date=July 16, 2019}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |last1=Cortez Chico |first1=Ricardo |last2=Lopez Alicea |first2=Keila |title=Mobilized by Indignation: Demanding the Resignation of Governor Rossello |work=Front Page News Image |agency=El Nuevo Dia |publisher=The Daily News of Puerto Rico |date=July 18, 2019}}</ref>
