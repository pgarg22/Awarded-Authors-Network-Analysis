'''Ryū Mitsuse''' ({{lang|ja|光瀬龍}} ''Mitsuse Ryū'', March 18, 1928 - July 7, 1999) was a [[Japan]]ese novelist, [[science fiction]] writer, alternate history writer, historical novelist, and essayist. Mitsuse is the author of ''Hyakuoku no Hiru to Sen'oku no Yoru''. Among his various works, this SF novel is considered as his representative work.<ref name="ngsj">''Nihon Gensō Sakka Jiten'' pp. 667-668</ref> Mitsuse is a founder member and was a member of the SFWJ ([[Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers of Japan]]). In the West he might be best known for [[manga]]-related works and the story ''The Sunset, 2217 A.D.'' which appeared in [[Frederik Pohl]]'s "''Best Science Fiction for 1972''".

== Biography ==
=== Birth and Age of student ===
Mitsuse was born at [[Arakawa, Tokyo|Minami-Senju]], Kita-Toshima District, [[Tokyo-Fu|Tokyo Prefecture]] in 1928. His birth name was '''Kimio Chiba''' ({{lang|ja|千葉 喜美雄}}). The eldest son of Kizō Chiba and Kiyo. There were three elder sisters.<ref name="gtl">''Yume wo nomi'' pp. 594-606</ref>

In around June, 1945, he evacuated to [[Iwate prefecture]], which was his parents' homeland, from Tokyo. He transferred to ''kyūsei'' Ichinose middle school.<ref>Ichinose pre-World War II middle school ({{lang|ja|旧制一関中学校}}). Now ''Iwate prefecrure Ichinose Dai-ichi Kōtōgakkō'' ({{lang|ja|岩手県立一関第一高等学校}} [[:ja:岩手県立一関第一高等学校・附属中学校|JA]])</ref> In 1948, he graduated from this middle school and entered the [[Toyo University]] in Tokyo. But he dropped out, and entered the [[Meiji University]]. But he again dropped out in short period. He transferred to Kawamura high school and graduated from this school. In 1949, Mitsuse entered the department of Agriculture of the Tokyo University of Education. In 1950, he transferred to the department of Science, zoology course, and graduated from this university in 1953.<ref name="ngsj" /><ref name="gtl" />

In 1954, Mitsuse again entered the department of literature, philosophy course, in Tokyo University of Education, which he did not graduate from. During this period in Tokyo, he was engaging in literary coterie activities.<ref name="gtl" />

=== Marriage ===
In 1955, Mitsuse proposed marriage to Chitose Iizuka, but her father opposed this proposal and rejected their marriage. Mitsuse could not marry.<ref name="gt-propose">''Yume wo nomi'' pp.180-182</ref> In 1957, Mitsuse became a tutor of Koganai high school of Tokyo prefecture. And next year, he obtained a stable job as a high school teacher of biology and earth science.<ref name="gtl" />

In 1959, Mitsuse again proposed marriage. He talked to the father of Chitose that he would take the "surname Iizuka". Thus he was allowed to marry with Chitose, and Mitsuse became '''Kimio Iizuka''' ({{lang|ja|飯塚 喜美雄}}).<ref name="gt-marriage">''Yume wo nomi'' pp. 183-185</ref><ref>Father of Chitose required fulfillment of the two conditions: 1) Stable occupation. 2) Taking wife's surname. Mitsuse satisfied these conditions.</ref>

=== Becoming a novelist ===
Before his marriage, Mitsuse joined "Kagaku Sōsaku Club" where [[Takumi Shibano]] was operating as a publisher and an editor of the coterie magazine "Uchū-jin" in 1957. He started publishing various short novels in {{nihongo||宇宙塵|Uchū-jin}} under the pen-name Mitsuse Ryū. He published first long novel "Hakengun Kaeru" in ''[[Uchūjin]]''.<ref name="gtl" /> ''(continued)''

As an SF novelist, he created the Space Chronicles series. His early long SF novel ''Tasogare ni Kaeru'' ({{lang|ja|黄昏に還る}}) belongs to this series. Most of his short SF stories constitute this series. ''Rakuyō 2217 nen'' ({{lang|ja|落陽2217年}}, ''The Sunset, 2217 A.D.'') is one of these stories.

== Works ==
In Japanese science fiction he might be better known for the novel {{nihongo|''[[Ten Billion Days and One Hundred Billion Nights]]''|百億の昼と千億の夜}}, which combines interest in technology and the [[Gautama Buddha|Buddha]].  It was ranked the top of the Japanese SF novels in a 2006 poll by the [[SF Magazine]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.locusmag.com/2006/News/03_HayakawaAllTimePoll.html |title=Hayakawa's SF Magazine's All-Time Best SF |publisher=Locus Magazine |accessdate=February 10, 2012}}</ref>  Ten Billion Days and a Hundred Billion Nights was adapted into a manga by [[Moto Hagio]] in the late 1970s.[http://matt-thorn.com/shoujo_manga/hagio_interview.php]
