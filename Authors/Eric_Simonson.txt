'''Eric Simonson''' (born June 27, 1960 in [[Milwaukee, Wisconsin]]) is an American writer and director in theatre, film and [[Opera director|opera]]. He is a member of Steppenwolf Theatre in Chicago, and the author of plays ''[[Lombardi (play)|Lombardi]]'', ''Fake'', ''Honest'', ''[[Magic/Bird]]'' and ''[[Bronx Bombers (play)|Bronx Bombers]]''. He won the 2005 Academy Award for his short documentary ''A Note of Triumph: The Golden Age of Norman Corwin'', and was nominated for a [[Tony Award]] for ''[[Tony Award for Best Direction of a Musical|Best Direction of a Musical]]'' in [[47th Tony Awards|1993 for ''The Song of Jacob Zulu'']].<ref>[http://ibdb.com/awardperson.asp?id=16179 ''Internet Broadway Database'']</ref>

== Personal life ==
Simonson was born in Milwaukee but grew up on a farm in the small town of [[Eagle (town), Wisconsin|Eagle]]. After graduating with a B.A. in Theatre from [[Lawrence University]], he moved to [[Madison, Wisconsin]] for a short period, where he worked with the then fledgling Ark Repertory Theatre. He moved to Chicago in 1983, where he helped found [[Lifeline Theatre]], and eventually worked with the [[Steppenwolf Theatre Company]]. He became a member of the theatre's ensemble in 1993.

He holds the distinction of being one of only a handful of directors who has received Tony, Emmy and Oscar nominations.

Simonson now lives in Los Angeles, but often works throughout the United States. In 2004 he married theatre producer [[Susan Raab Simonson|Susan Raab]]; she died of breast cancer in 2006, aged 37. They had one child, Henry Simonson.<ref name= "LATObit">Lynne Heffley, [http://articles.latimes.com/2006/dec/01/local/me-simonson1 "Susan Raab Simonson...was a producer for nationally known L.A. Theatre Works"], ''[[Los Angeles Times]]'', December 1, 2006</ref> In 2013, Simonson married actress Sue Cremin.

== Career ==
Simonson is the writer (with [[Jeffrey Hatcher]]) of ''[[Work Song: Three Views of Frank Lloyd Wright]]'', which was commissioned by [[Steppenwolf Theatre Company]] and [[Milwaukee Repertory Theater]], and was subsequently produced across the United States. His film ''[[A Note of Triumph: The Golden Age of Norman Corwin]]'' won the 2005 [[Academy Award]] for ''[[Academy Award for Best Documentary (Short Subject)|Short Subject Documentary]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://awardsdatabase.oscars.org/ampas_awards/DisplayMain.jsp?curTime=1224873388108|title=2005 Academy Awards|accessdate=October 24, 2008|publisher=oscars.org|archive-url=https://archive.is/20120701134011/http://awardsdatabase.oscars.org/ampas_awards/DisplayMain.jsp?curTime=1224873388108|archive-date=July 1, 2012|url-status=dead|df=mdy-all}}</ref> That film also received a nomination from the International Documentary Association (IDA) for Distinguished Achievement. Other recent films include the documentary Studs Terkel: Listening to America (Emmy nomination) and On Tiptoe: Gentle Steps to Freedom (Oscar nomination, IDA Award, Emmy nomination). All three films subsequently aired on HBO/CINEMAX. Other films include Hamlet (co-directed with Campbell Scott) for Hallmark Entertainment, and the independent feature, Topa Topa Bluffs. Simonson has also written and developed multiple television series for HBO, FX, USA, TNT and STARZ networks.

In 2007 he was invited to join the [[Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences]].

He also directed the premiere of [[Ricky Ian Gordon]] and [[Michael Korie]]'s opera ''[[The Grapes of Wrath (opera)|The Grapes of Wrath]]'' at [[Minnesota Opera]] in 2007, and, in 2011, the premiere of Kevin Puts and Mark Campbell's  "[[Silent Night (opera)|Silent Night]]", which won the Pulitzer Prize in music. Other opera directing credits include the North American premiere of "[[The Handmaid's Tale (opera)|The Handmaid's Tale]]."

His recent plays, ''Lombardi/The Only Thing'' (Madison Repertory Theatre), ''Carter's Way'' (Steppenwolf Theatre)<ref>[http://chicago.broadwayworld.com/viewcolumn.cfm?colid=24728 Review on broadwayworld.com]</ref> and ''[[Slaughterhouse-Five]]'' (Godlight Theatre of New York) received [[premiere]]s in 2008.  ''Honest'' premiered at [[Carthage College]] in 2009 and was then part of [[Steppenwolf Theatre Company]]'s 5th Annual First Look Repertory of New Work.  The Carthage production traveled to the 2010 Kennedy Center [[American College Theatre Festival]] (Region 3).  Simonson's play ''[[Lombardi (play)|Lombardi]]'' ran on Broadway from October 2010 to May 2011.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ibdb.com/production.php?id=487855|title=''Lombardi'', IBDb profile|publisher=[[The Broadway League]]|accessdate=December 11, 2010}}</ref>

Simonson's ''Louder Faster'', co-authored with [[Jeffrey Hatcher]], premiered at [[City Theatre (Pittsburgh)|City Theatre]] in [[Theatre in Pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] in May 2011. His play, ''[[Magic/Bird]]'', premiered on Broadway in March 2012.<ref>[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/147796-Magic-Bird-Basketball-Themed-Play-by-Eric-Simonson-Aiming-for-Broadway-in-2012 "''Magic/Bird'', Basketball-Themed Play by Eric Simonson, Aiming for Broadway in 2012"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110220045654/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/147796-Magic-Bird-Basketball-Themed-Play-by-Eric-Simonson-Aiming-for-Broadway-in-2012 |date=February 20, 2011 }} playbill.com</ref>
