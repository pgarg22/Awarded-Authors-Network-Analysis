'''Jane Gillson Langton''' (December 30, 1922 – December 22, 2018) was an American author of [[children's literature]] and [[mystery novels]]. She also illustrated her novels.<ref name = "post dispatch">[http://nl.newsbank.com/nl-search/we/Archives?p_product=SL&p_theme=sl&p_action=search&p_maxdocs=200&p_topdoc=1&p_text_direct-0=0EB04DC49EC8E0FF&p_field_direct-0=document_id&p_perpage=10&p_sort=YMD_date:D&s_trackval=GooglePM "Jane Langton Draws On Skills As Artist"], ''St. Louis Post-Dispatch'', August 23, 1992</ref>

==Biography==
Langton was born in [[Boston]], [[Massachusetts]]. She studied [[astronomy]] at [[Wellesley College]] and the [[University of Michigan]], receiving a bachelor's degree in 1944. She received an M.A. in [[art history]] from the University of Michigan in 1945, and another M.A. from [[Radcliffe College]] in 1948. She studied at the [[School of the Museum of Fine Arts, Boston|Boston Museum School]] from 1958 to 1959.<ref name="BG_Obit">{{Cite web |url=https://www.bostonglobe.com/metro/obituaries/2019/01/01/jane-langton-who-set-her-mystery-novels-concord-and-beyond/Uok8JLfSUGnBZB5eJHN5UI/story.html |title=Jane Langton, who set her mystery novels in Concord and beyond, dies at 95 |last=Marquard |first=Bryan |work=Boston Globe |quote=''The Fledgling'' was named a Newbery Honor Book in 1981 and was part of her "Hall Family Chronicles" series. She also has received a Grand Master Award from the Mystery Writers of America. Her popular series of mysteries featuring the sleuth Homer Kelly, a Harvard University professor, included ''Emily Dickinson Is Dead'', a nominee for a Mystery Writers Edgar Award. |date=January 1, 2019 |accessdate=November 13, 2020}}</ref>

In 1961 Langton wrote and illustrated her first book for children, ''The Majesty of Grace'', a story about a young girl during the [[Great Depression|Depression]] who (to quote ''The New York Times'') "cherishes the illusion that she is really the eldest of King [[George VI]]'s three daughters (and therefore the future [[Monarchy of the United Kingdom|Queen of England]])".<ref name="MajOfGrace">{{Cite web |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1961/04/09/archives/one-of-the-joneses-the-majesty-of-grace-by-jane-langton-illustrated.html |title=One of the Joneses; THE MAJESTY OF GRACE |last=James |first=Miriam |work=The New York Times |quote=Set in the Depression years, Jane Langton's story of childhood dreams of glory stars a little girl who cherishes the illusion that she is really the eldest of King George VI's three daughters (and therefore the future Queen of England) and not just plain, grouchy, clumsy Grace Jones with "six whole faults," whose father is out of work. |date=April 9, 1961 |accessdate=November 13, 2020}}</ref>

Langton later wrote a children's series, The Hall Family Chronicles, and the Homer Kelly murder mystery novels. She also created several stand-alone novels and picture books.

Langton's novel ''The Fledgling'' is a [[Newbery Honor|Newbery Honor book]]. <!-- 'Newbery' is the correct spelling; please don't change it.  Thanks. -->  Her novel ''Emily Dickinson is Dead'' was nominated for an [[Edgar Award]] and received a [[Nero Award]]. ''The Face on the Wall'' was an editors' choice selection by ''The Drood Review of Mystery'' for 1998.

Langton lived in [[Lincoln, Massachusetts]], near the town of [[Concord, Massachusetts|Concord]], the setting of many of her novels. Her husband, Bill, died in 1997. Langton has three adult sons: [[Christopher Langton|Chris]], David and Andy.<ref>[https://pqasb.pqarchiver.com/boston/access/73732980.html?FMT=ABS&FMTS=ABS:FT&type=current&date=Jun+07%2C+2001&author=Rachel+Travers%2C+Globe+Correspondent&pub=Boston+Globe&desc=AT+HOME+WITH+JANE+LANGTON&pqatl=google "At home with Jane Langton"], ''[[The Boston Globe]]'', June 7, 2001</ref>

Langton died in December 2018, a few days short of her 96th birthday, from complications of a respiratory disease.<ref>{{cite news |last=Seelye |first=Katharine Q. |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2018/12/26/obituaries/jane-langton-dead.html |title=Jane Langton, Author of New England Mysteries, Is Dead at 95 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=December 26, 2018 |access-date=December 26, 2018}}</ref>
