'''Helen von Kolnitz Hyer''' (December 30, 1896 – November 14, 1983) was an American poet. She was named by [[Governor of South Carolina|Governor]] [[John C. West]] as the second [[South Carolina Poet Laureate]] from 1974 to 1983.

==Early years and education==
Hyer was born in [[Charleston, South Carolina]] on December 30, 1896. Her parents were George and Sarah Conover (Holmes) von Kolnitz. She graduated from [[Ashley Hall (school)|Ashley Hall]] in Charleston and then proceeded to study at [[Simmons College (Massachusetts)|Simmons College]] in [[Boston, Massachusetts]], from 1917 to 1918, studying [[library science]].<ref name="SC Encyclopedia">{{cite book|title=The South Carolina Encyclopedia|year=2006|publisher=University of South Carolina Press|location=Columbia, South Carolina|isbn=978-1-57003-598-2|chapter=Hyer, Helen von Kolnitz|page=471}}</ref><ref name=SCCftB>{{cite web|title=Helen von Kolnitz Hyer |url=http://faculty.libsci.sc.edu/literarymap/authors/hyer.htm |publisher=South Carolina Center for the Book |accessdate=December 28, 2012 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140127113443/http://faculty.libsci.sc.edu/literarymap/authors/hyer.htm |archivedate=January 27, 2014 }}</ref>

==Career==
Hyer's poetry career began early in life as she was recognized for her ability to memorize and recite poetry. One of her poems became the school song for Ashley Hall. Publication of her poetry began when she was just 17; first in ''Romance'' magazine, then in ''Adventure Magazine''.<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" />

Leaving college during [[World War I]], Hyer made her way back to Charleston, becoming a statistician and subsequently was named Curator of Public Instruction at the [[Charleston Museum]].<ref name=SCCftB />

She was a founding member of the [[Poetry Society of South Carolina]] in 1920.<ref name="Golden Haze">{{cite book|last=Yuhl|first=Stephanie E.|title=A Golden Haze of Memory: The Making Of Historic Charleston|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=yXlROakGDrAC&pg=PA102|accessdate=December 28, 2012|year=2005|publisher=Univ of North Carolina Press|isbn=978-0-8078-5599-7|page=102}}</ref>
Hyer wrote many works for children as well as adults. Her work often centered on the [[Southern United States|South]], focusing on Confederate heroes, the history of South Carolina, as well as romance in the South. Her work appeared in journals such as ''[[Poet Lore]]'', ''[[Argosy (magazine)|Argosy]]'', and ''[[The Christian Science Monitor]]''.<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" />

Hyer was named South Carolina's second [[poet laureate]] by Governor John West in April 1974. She served until her death in 1983.<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" /><ref name=SCCftB />

==Personal life and legacy==
She married Edward Allen Hyer in 1921.<ref name=SCCftB /> They had four daughters and one son.<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" /> The couple lived in [[Michigan]] for many years, returning to Charleston in 1941.<ref name=SCCftB />

Hyer died in [[Beaufort, South Carolina]], on November 14, 1983. She was buried a [[Magnolia Cemetery (Charleston, South Carolina)|Magnolia Cemetery]] in Charleston.<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" />

In 2003, composer [[Jim Clemens]] was commissioned to create a choral arrangement for the [[Georgetown, South Carolina|Georgetown]] Indigo Choral Society. He used lyrics from Hyer's poem "Santee Lullaby" in his first movement.<ref name="SC Encyclopedia" /><ref>{{cite news|title=Sing Out About Georgetown|newspaper=The Myrtle Beach Sun News|date=July 3, 2003|page=C2}}</ref>
