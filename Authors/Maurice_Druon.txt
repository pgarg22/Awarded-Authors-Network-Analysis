'''Maurice Druon''' (23 April 1918 – 14 April 2009)  was a French [[novelist]] and a member of the [[Académie française]], of which he served as "Perpetual Secretary" (chairman) between 1985 and 1999.

==Life and career==
{{French literature sidebar}}
Born in [[Paris]], France, Druon was the son of Russian-Jewish<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.jinfo.org/Authors.html|title=Jewish Authors|author=JINFO|work=jinfo.org|access-date=24 June 2015}}</ref> immigrant Lazare Kessel (1899–1920)<ref name="julianjackson">{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2009/apr/16/maurice-druon-obituary|title=Obituary: Maurice Druon|author=Julian Jackson|work=[[The Guardian]]|access-date=24 June 2015}}</ref> and was brought up at [[La Croix-Saint-Leufroy]] in Normandy and educated at the lycée Michelet de Vanves. His father committed suicide in 1920<ref name="julianjackson"/> and his mother remarried in 1926; Maurice subsequently took the name of his adoptive father, the lawyer René Druon (1874–1961).

He was the nephew of the writer [[Joseph Kessel]], with whom he translated the ''[[Chant des Partisans]]'', a [[French Resistance]] anthem of [[World War II]], with music and words (in Russian) originally by [[Anna Marly]].  Druon was a member of the Resistance and came to London in 1943 to participate in the BBC's "Honneur et Patrie" programme.<ref>Francine de Martinoir, « Maurice Druon, mort d’un partisan de la langue française », ''La Croix'', 15 April 2009 (French)</ref>

Druon began writing for literary journals at the age of 18.  In September 1939, having been called up for military service, he wrote an article for ''[[Paris-Soir]]'' entitled "J'ai vingt ans et je pars (I am twenty years old and I am leaving)".<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k7642365z|title=Paris-soir|date=1939-09-09|website=Gallica|language=EN|access-date=2020-02-05}}</ref>  Following the fall of France in 1940, he was demobilized and remained in the unoccupied zone of France, and his first play, ''Mégarée'', was produced in Monte Carlo in February 1942.  He left the same year to join the forces of [[Charles de Gaulle]]. Druon became aide de camp to General [[François d'Astier de La Vigerie]].

In 1948 Druon received the [[Prix Goncourt]] for his novel ''{{Interlanguage link multi|Les Grandes Familles|fr|3=Les Grandes Familles}}'', and later published two sequels.<ref name="Weber">{{cite web |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/04/16/world/europe/16druon.html? |title=Maurice Druon, Prolific Writer, Dies at 90 |work=[[The New York Times]] |first=Bruce |last=Weber |date=15 April 2009 |access-date=5 July 2015}}</ref><ref name="Lichfield">{{cite web |url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/obituaries/maurice-druon-writer-and-pugnacious-defender--of-the-french-language-1671353.html |title=Maurice Druon: Writer and pugnacious defender of the French language |first=John |last=Lichfield |work=[[The Independent]] |date=20 April 2009 |access-date=5 July 2015}}</ref><ref name="WP">{{cite web |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2009/04/15/AR2009041503462.html |title=Gaullist Minister Wrote Popular Anthem |work=[[The Washington Post]] |date=16 April 2009 |access-date=5 July 2015}}</ref>

Druon was elected to the 30th seat of the Académie française on 8 December 1966,<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.academie-francaise.fr/les-immortels/maurice-druon|title=Maurice DRUON {{!}} Académie française|website=www.academie-francaise.fr|access-date=2020-02-05}}</ref> succeeding [[Georges Duhamel]]. He was elected as "Perpetual Secretary" in 1985, but chose to resign the office in late 1999 due to old age; he successfully pushed for [[Hélène Carrère d'Encausse]] to succeed him, the first woman to hold the post, and was styled Honorary Perpetual Secretary after 2000. On the death of [[Henri Troyat]] on 2 March 2007, he became the Dean of the ''Académie'', its longest-serving member.

While his scholarly writing earned him a seat at the Académie, Druon is best known for a series of seven historical novels published in the 1950s under the title ''[[Les Rois maudits]]'' (''The Accursed Kings'').  The novels were [[Les Rois maudits (miniseries)|adapted for French television in 1972]], gaining a wider audience through overseas sales, and again in 2005, starring [[Jeanne Moreau]]. Fantasy writer [[George R. R. Martin]] stated that the novels had been an inspiration for his fantasy series ''[[A Song of Ice and Fire]]'', and called Druon "France's best historical novelist since [[Alexandre Dumas|Alexandre Dumas, ''père'']]".<ref name="Martin">{{cite web |url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2013/apr/05/maurice-druon-george-rr-martin |title=My hero: Maurice Druon by George RR Martin |first=George R. R. |last=Martin |author-link=George R. R. Martin |work=The Guardian |date=3 April 2013 |access-date=24 June 2015}}</ref><ref name="Milne">{{cite news|last=Milne|first=Ben|title=''Game of Thrones'': The cult French novel that inspired George RR Martin|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/magazine-26824993|access-date=6 April 2014|date=4 April 2014 |publisher=[[BBC]]}}</ref><ref name="Kamin">{{cite web|url=http://www.timesofisrael.com/the-jewish-legacy-behind-game-of-thrones/ |title=The Jewish legacy behind ''Game of Thrones'' |work=[[The Times of Israel]] |date=20 May 2014 |first=Debra |last=Kamin |access-date=31 May 2015}}</ref>

Druon's only work for children – ''Tistou les pouces verts'' – was published in 1957 and translated into [[English language|English]] in 1958 (as ''Tistou of the Green Thumbs'') and 2012 (as ''Tistou: The Boy With Green Thumbs'').<ref>{{Cite book|author=Druon, Maurice |title=Tistou: The Boy With Green Thumbs |publisher=Hawthorn Press |year=2012 |isbn=978-1-907359-08-8}}</ref>

Druon was [[Minister of Culture (France)|Minister of Cultural Affairs]] (1973–1974)<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.britannica.com/biography/Maurice-Druon|title=Maurice-Samuel-Roger-Charles Druon {{!}} French author|website=Encyclopedia Britannica|language=en|access-date=2020-02-05}}</ref> in [[Pierre Messmer]]'s cabinet, and a deputy of Paris (1978–1981). He was survived by his second wife, Madeleine Marignac, whom he married in 1968.<ref name="julianjackson"/> Madeleine Druon died in 2016 aged 91.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.lefigaro.fr/flash-actu/2016/09/24/97001-20160924FILWWW00089-madeleine-druon-est-morte.php|title=Madeleine Druon est morte|last=Figaro.fr|first=Le|date=2016-09-24|website=Le Figaro.fr|language=fr|access-date=2020-02-05}}</ref>
Druon was a descendant of Brazilian author [[Odorico Mendes]].
