'''Roma Potiki''' (born 1958<ref>{{Cite book|title=Toi te Ao : Aotearoa world celebration of indigenous art and history, Te Taumata Gallery, Auckland, 1993.|last=|first=|date=1993|publisher=Toi Te Ao Art Committee|isbn=0473023113|location=Wellington [N.Z.]|pages=32|oclc=154557391}}</ref>) is a New Zealand poet, playwright, visual artist, curator, [[theatre actor]] and director, as well as a commentator on [[Māori theatre]].<ref>{{Cite book|title=The Oxford companion to New Zealand literature|last=|first=|date=1998|publisher=Oxford University Press|isbn=0195583485|editor-last=Robinson|editor-first=Roger|location=Melbourne, Victoria.|pages=448–449|oclc=40598609|editor-last2=Wattie|editor-first2=Nelson}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|title=World theories of theatre|last=Odom|first=Glenn A.|publisher=Taylor & Francis|year=2017|isbn=9781317586289|location=London|pages=216–218|oclc=988290202}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|title=Decolonizing the stage : theatrical syncretism and post-colonial drama|last=Balme|first=Christopher B.|publisher=Clarendon Press|year=1999|isbn=0198184441|location=Oxford|pages=64–65|oclc=39655593}}</ref> She is of [[Te Rarawa]], [[Te Aupōuri]] and [[Ngati Rangitihi]] descent.<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|url=https://www.komako.org.nz/person/822|title=Roma Potiki|last=Underhill|first=Bridget|date=|website=www.komako.org.nz|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-11-09}}</ref> As well as being a published poet, her work is included in the permanent collection of the [[Dowse Art Museum]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.read-nz.org/writer/potiki-roma|title=Potiki, Roma|last=|first=|date=January 2017|website=www.read-nz.org|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-11-09}}</ref>

== Background ==
Roma Potiki was born in [[Lower Hutt]], a city in the Wellington Region, and grew up in the suburb [[Wainuiomata]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/roma-potiki|title=Roma Potiki|last=Foundation|first=Poetry|date=2019-11-15|website=Poetry Foundation|language=en|access-date=2019-11-15}}</ref> As a child she attended Wainuiomata Primary School, Woodhatton Primary School, Wainuiomata College and Wellington High School.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=https://www.komako.org.nz/person/822|title=Kōmako A bibliography of writing by Māori in English|last=|first=|date=2019-11-15|website=www.komako.org.nz|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191109015120/https://www.komako.org.nz/person/822|archive-date=2019-11-09|access-date=2019-11-15}}</ref> Her first play was written while at primary school and she also started writing poetry at school.<ref name=":1" />

== Career ==
Roma Potiki was involved in contemporary Māori theatre in early formative years. She was in a play performed by Te Ika A Maui Players in 1978 called Death of the Land by Rore Hapipi's (Rowley Habib).<ref name=":1" /> She went on to help form the Māori theatre company Maranga Mai in 1979.<ref name=":4">{{Cite web|url=https://teara.govt.nz/en/maori-theatre-te-whare-tapere-hou/page-2|title=2. – Māori theatre - te whare tapere hōu – Te Ara Encyclopedia of New Zealand|last=Taonga|first=New Zealand Ministry for Culture and Heritage Te Manatu|website=teara.govt.nz|language=en|access-date=2019-11-16}}</ref> This company was linked to the [[Māori protest movement]] and they toured a theatre production in 1979 - 1980 to marae and schools.<ref name=":1" /> In 1990 she was a founding director of another theatre company He Ara Hou. They performed and toured a play called ''Whatungarongaro.''<ref name=":4" /><ref name=":3" /> This play was directed by Potiki and John Anderson and devised over nine months with the company members. Potiki had previously done workshops with a theatre company PETA (Philippines Educational Theatre Association) and was influenced by their way of working.<ref name=":5">{{Cite book|title=Performing Aotearoa : New Zealand theatre and drama in an age of transition|last=O'Donnell|first=David|date=2007|publisher=P.I.E. Peter Lang|isbn=978-90-5201-359-6|editor-last=Maufort|editor-first=Marc|location=Bruxelles|pages=271|chapter=Calming the Oceans: Interview with Briar Grace-Smith|oclc=165412282|editor-last2=O'Donnell|editor-first2=David}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|last=|first=|title=John Anderson|url=https://www.nzonscreen.com/profile/john-anderson/biography|url-status=live|access-date=2021-05-23|website=NZ On Screen|language=en-NZ}}</ref>

Roma Potiki's writing includes poetry contribution to many anthologies, as well as published volumes. She has written the forward to books of New Zealand Māori plays and contributed text to exhibition catalogues. As a fine artist Potiki has exhibited art work and one of her pieces ''Hinewai'' is in the collection of the [[Dowse Art Museum]].<ref name=":3" />

== Legacy ==
The poem ''Stones in her Mouth'' by Potiki inspired contemporary New Zealand choreographer [[Lemi Ponifasio]] to create a community leadership project also called ''[https://www.redcat.org/event/lemi-ponifasio-mau-stones Stones in her Mouth]'' in 2013. This involved a group of Māori women holding workshops at marae and writing text that was then performed.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Floating Islanders : Pasifika theatre in Aotearoa|last=Warrington, Lisa|publisher=|others=O'Donnell, David, 1956-|year=2017|isbn=978-1-988531-07-6|location=Dunedin, New Zealand|pages=139|oclc=994638351}}</ref>

=== He Ara Hou and Whatungarongaro ===
Before actor [[Rena Owen]] was in ''[[Once Were Warriors (film)|Once Were Warriors]]'' she performed a similar role in theatre company He Ara Hou's play ''Whatungarongaro'' that Potiki a co-creator of. Owens performance in ''Whatungarongaro'' influenced her being cast in Once Were Warriors.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Performing Aotearoa : New Zealand theatre and drama in an age of transition|last=Edmond|first=Murray|date=2007|publisher=P.I.E. Peter Lang|isbn=978-90-5201-359-6|editor-last=Maufort|editor-first=Marc|location=Bruxelles|pages=65|chapter="Autonomous Theatre" in New Zealand|oclc=165412282|editor-last2=O'Donnell|editor-first2=David}}</ref>

Playwright and director of theatre company Tawata Productions [[Hone Kouka]] cites seeing ''Whatungarongaro'' in 1991 at the Depot Theatre in Wellington as being life changing and convincing him that innovative Māori theatre had no boundaries.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Performing Aotearoa : New Zealand theatre and drama in an age of transition|last=Kouka|first=Hone|date=2007|publisher=P.I.E. Peter Lang|isbn=978-90-5201-359-6|editor-last=Maufort|editor-first=Marc|location=Bruxelles|pages=240|chapter=The State of Contemporary Theatre|oclc=165412282|editor-last2=O'Donnell|editor-first2=David}}</ref> Another New Zealand theatre and film writer [[Briar Grace-Smith]] was an assistant to Potiki and learnt from the process of making ''Whatungarongaro''.<ref name=":5" />
