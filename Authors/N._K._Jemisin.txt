'''Nora Keita Jemisin'''<ref>{{Cite web|last=Shapiro|first=Lila|date=2018-11-29|title=For Reigning Fantasy Queen N.K. Jemisin, There's No Escape From Reality|url=https://www.vulture.com/2018/11/nk-jemisin-fifth-season-broken-earth-trilogy.html|access-date=2020-10-07|website=Vulture|language=en-us}}</ref> (born September 19, 1972) is an American [[science fiction]] and [[fantasy]] writer, better known as '''N. K. Jemisin'''. She has also worked as a [[Counseling psychology|counseling psychologist]]. Her fiction includes a wide range of themes, notably cultural conflict and oppression.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.orbitbooks.net/interview/n-k-jemisin/|title=N.K. Jemisin on THE HUNDRED THOUSAND KINGDOMS|last=<!--no byline-->|date=August 22, 2012|website=Orbit Blog|publisher=Orbit Books|type=Blog|access-date=August 28, 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2015/08/09/books/review/the-fifth-season-by-nk-jemisin.html/|title=Book Review: 'The Fifth Season,' by N. K. Jemisin|last=Novik|first=Naomi|author-link=Naomi Novik|date=August 12, 2015|work=[[The New York Times]]|access-date=August 31, 2018}}</ref> Her [[debut novel]], ''[[The Hundred Thousand Kingdoms]]'', and the subsequent books in her ''[[The Inheritance Trilogy (Jemisin series)|Inheritance Trilogy]]'' received critical acclaim. She has won several awards for her work, including the [[Locus Award]]. The three books of her [[#Broken Earth series|Broken Earth series]] made her the first author to win the [[Hugo Award for Best Novel]] in three consecutive years, as well as the first to win for all three novels in a trilogy.<ref name=":2">{{Cite news|last=El-Mohtar|first=Amal|date=2020-03-24|title=When a Sinister Enemy Attacks New York, the City Fights Back|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2020/03/24/books/review/nk-jemisin-city-we-became.html|access-date=2020-05-22|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> Jemisin was a recipient of the [[MacArthur Fellows Program]] Genius Grant in 2020.<ref name=":1">{{cite web|url=https://www.macfound.org/fellows/1062/|title=N. K. Jemisin|website=MacArthur Foundation|date=October 6, 2020}}</ref>

==Early life==
Jemisin was born in [[Iowa City, Iowa]], and grew up in [[New York City]] and [[Mobile, Alabama]]. She lived in Massachusetts for ten years and then moved to New York City. Jemisin attended [[Tulane University]] from 1990 to 1994, where she received a B.S. in [[psychology]]. She went on to study counseling and earn her [[Master of Education]] from the [[University of Maryland College Park|University of Maryland]].<ref name="Locus 2010">{{cite web|url=http://www.locusmag.com/Perspectives/2010/08/n-k-jemisin-rites-of-passage/|title=N.K. Jemisin: Rites of Passage|work=Locus|quote=She studied psychology at Tulane in New Orleans, and went to grad school to study counseling at the University of Maryland-College Park. |date=August 18, 2010}}</ref>

==Career==
A graduate of the 2002 [[Viable Paradise]] writing workshop,<ref>{{cite web|work=Locus|url=http://www.locusmag.com/Perspectives/2010/08/n-k-jemisin-rites-of-passage/|title=N.K. Jemisin: Rites of Passage|date=August 18, 2010|access-date=September 7, 2016}}</ref> Jemisin has published short stories and novels. She was a member of the Boston-area writing group BRAWLers,<ref name=CM/> and as of 2010 was a member of Altered Fluid, a speculative fiction critique group.<ref name=CM>{{cite web|work=Clarkesworld Magazine|url=http://clarkesworldmagazine.com/altered_fluid_interview/|title=Even the Best Stories Have Flaws: Inside Altered Fluid|author=Jones, Jeremy L. C.|issue=47|date=August 2010}}</ref> In 2009 and 2010, Jemisin's short story "[[Non-Zero Probabilities]]" was a finalist for the [[Nebula Award for Best Short Story|Nebula]] and [[Hugo Award for Best Short Story|Hugo]] Best Short Story Awards.<ref>{{cite magazine|last=Jemisin|first=N. K.|date=September 2009|title=Non-Zero Probabilities|url=http://clarkesworldmagazine.com/jemisin_09_09/|magazine=[[Clarkesworld Magazine]]|issue=36|access-date=August 16, 2017}}</ref>

Jemisin's [[debut novel]], ''[[The Hundred Thousand Kingdoms]]'', the first volume in her ''Inheritance Trilogy'', was published in 2010. It was nominated for the 2010 [[Nebula Award]] and short-listed for the [[James Tiptree Jr. Award]].<ref>{{cite web|last=<!--no byline-->|date=<!--no specific source date-->|title=2010 Award Winners & Nominees|url=https://www.worldswithoutend.com/books_year_index.asp?year=2010|access-date=August 28, 2018|website=Worlds Without End|publisher=Tres Barbas, LLC}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last=<!--no byline-->|date=March 21, 2011|title=2010 James Tiptree, Jr. Award|url=https://tiptree.org/award/2010-james-tiptree-jr-award|access-date=August 28, 2018|website=James Tiptree, Jr. Literary Award|publisher=James Tiptree Jr. Literary Council|language=en-US}}</ref> In 2011, it was nominated for the [[Hugo Award]], [[World Fantasy Award]],<ref>{{cite web|date=July 28, 2011|title=World Fantasy Nominees and Lifetime Achievement Winners|url=http://www.locusmag.com/News/2011/07/world-fantasy-nominees-and-lifetime-achievement-winners/|website=[[Locus (magazine)|Locus]]}}</ref> and Locus Award, winning the 2011 [[Locus Award for Best First Novel]].<ref>{{cite web|date=June 25, 2011|title=Locus Awards 2011 Winners|url=http://www.locusmag.com/News/2011/06/locus-awards-2011-winners/|website=Locus}}</ref> It was followed by two further novels in the same trilogy – ''[[The Broken Kingdoms]]'' in 2010 and ''[[The Kingdom of Gods]]'' in 2011.

During her delivery of the Guest of Honour speech at the 2013 Continuum in Australia, Jemisin pointed out that 10% of the [[Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers of America]] (SFWA) membership voted for [[alt-right]] writer [[Vox Day|Theodore Beale]] in his bid for the SFWA presidential position, stating that silence about Beale's views was the same as enabling them.<ref name="LitReactor">{{cite web|url=https://litreactor.com/columns/controversies-inside-the-worl-of-science-fiction-and-fantasy|title=Controversies Inside the World of Science Fiction and Fantasy|last=Khanna|first=Rajan|date=November 26, 2013|access-date=September 8, 2016}}</ref> Beale's response to Jemisin was condemned as "an appallingly racist screed".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://amalelmohtar.com/2013/06/13/calling-for-the-expulsion-of-theodore-beale-from-sfwa|title=Calling for the Expulsion of Theodore Beale from SFWA|last=El-Mohtar|first=Amal|author-link=Amal El-Mohtar|date=June 13, 2013|access-date=January 14, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210206090312/https://amalelmohtar.com/2013/06/13/calling-for-the-expulsion-of-theodore-beale-from-sfwa/|archive-date=February 6, 2021}}</ref> A link to his comments was tweeted on the SFWA Authors [[Twitter]] feed, and Beale was subsequently expelled from the organization after a unanimous vote by the SFWA Board.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.locusmag.com/News/2013/08/beale-expelled-from-sfwa/|title=SFWA Board Votes to Expel Beale|date=August 14, 2013|work=Locus}}</ref>

Jemisin was a co-[[Science fiction convention#Guests of Honor|Guest of Honor]] of the 2014 [[WisCon]] [[science fiction convention]] in [[Madison, Wisconsin]].<ref>"Announcing WisCon 38's Guests of Honor: Hiromi Goto and N.K. Jemisin" ''A Momentary Taste of WisCon 37'' (Elizabeth Stone, ed.) Issue #4 (May 26, 2013), p. 2.</ref> At that time, ''[[GQ]]'' described her as having "a day job as a counseling psychologist."<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.gq.com/story/nk-jemisin-is-trying-to-keep-the-world-from-ending|title=N.K. Jemisin Is Trying to Keep the World From Ending|last=Rivera|first=Joshua|website=GQ|language=en|access-date=2020-03-27|quote = But there were those in the speculative fiction community who still didn’t want her, or anyone like her—a black woman born in Iowa City and raised between Mobile, Alabama and Brooklyn, New York with a day job as a counseling psychologist— to have a seat at the same table as them.}}</ref> She was the Author Guest of Honor at [[Arisia]] 2015 in [[Boston, Massachusetts]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://2015.arisia.org/GoHBios|title=Guest of Honor Bios|website=Arisia 2015}}</ref>

In January 2016, Jemisin started writing "Otherworldly", a bimonthly column for ''[[The New York Times]]''.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Zutter|first1=Natalie|title=N.K. Jemisin Launches SFF Column at ''The New York Times Book Review''|url=http://www.tor.com/2016/01/04/nk-jemisin-sff-column-the-new-york-times-book-review/|access-date=August 13, 2017|work=[[Tor.com]]|date=January 4, 2016}}</ref> In May 2016, Jemisin mounted a [[Patreon]] campaign which raised sufficient funding to allow her to quit her job as a counseling psychologist and focus full-time on her writing.<ref name=":0">{{cite web |url=https://www.wired.com/2016/06/wired-book-club-nk-jemisin/|title=WIRED Book Club: Fantasy Writer N.K. Jemisin on the Weird Dreams That Fuel Her Stories|author=Kehe, Jason|date=June 7, 2016|work=[[Wired (magazine)|Wired]]|access-date=September 7, 2016}}</ref>

Jemisin's novel ''[[The Fifth Season (novel)|The Fifth Season]]'' was published in 2016, the first of the ''Broken Earth'' trilogy. ''The Fifth Season'' won the [[Hugo Award for Best Novel]], making Jemisin the first [[African Americans|African-American]] writer to win a Hugo award in that category.<ref>Alter, Alexander (August 24, 2016), [https://www.nytimes.com/2016/08/25/books/n-k-jemisin-on-diversity-in-science-fiction-and-inspiration-from-dreams.html?_r=0 "N. K. Jemisin on Diversity in Science Fiction and Inspiration From Dreams"], ''[[The New York Times]]''.</ref> The sequels in the trilogy, ''[[The Obelisk Gate]]'' and ''[[The Stone Sky]]'', won the Hugo Award for Best Novel in 2017<ref name="Hugo2017">{{cite web|title=2017 Hugo Awards|url=http://www.thehugoawards.org/hugo-history/2017-hugo-awards/|access-date=August 11, 2017}}</ref> and 2018,<ref name="Hugo2018">{{cite web|date=August 19, 2018|title=2018 Hugo Awards|url=http://www.thehugoawards.org/hugo-history/2018-hugo-awards/}}</ref> respectively, making Jemisin the first author to win the [[Hugo Award for Best Novel]] in three consecutive years, as well as the first to win for all three novels in a trilogy.<ref name=":2" /> In 2017, [[Bustle (magazine)|''Bustle'']] called Jemisin "the sci-fi writer every woman needs to be reading".<ref>{{cite news|last1=Wilson|first1=Kristian|title=The Sci-Fi Writer Every Woman Needs To Be Reading Has 3 New Books Coming|url=https://www.bustle.com/p/the-sci-fi-writer-every-woman-needs-to-be-reading-has-3-new-books-coming-29286|access-date=August 12, 2017|work=Bustle|date=January 10, 2017|language=en}}</ref>

Jemisin published a short story collection, ''[[How Long 'til Black Future Month?]]'' in November 2018.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Gorgeous 'Black Future Month' Tracks A Writer's Development|url=https://www.npr.org/2018/11/29/671583610/gorgeous-black-future-month-tracks-a-writers-development|access-date=2020-10-08|website=NPR.org|language=en}}</ref> It contains stories written from 2004 to 2017 and four new works.

''[[DC's Young Animal|Far Sector]]'', a twelve-issue limited series comic written by Jemisin with art by Jamal Campbell, began publication in 2019. It was nominated for the 2021 [[Eisner Award]] for Best Limited Series.<ref>{{Cite web|title=DC Garners Seventeen 2021 Eisner Award Nominations|url=https://www.dccomics.com/blog/2021/06/09/dc-garners-seventeen-2021-eisner-award-nominations|url-status=live|access-date=June 10, 2021|website=DC Comics}}</ref>

Jemisin's urban fantasy novel ''[[The City We Became]]'' was published in March 2020.  

In October 2020, Jemisin was announced as a recipient of the [[MacArthur Fellows Program]] Genius Grant.<ref name=":1" />

In June 2021, Sony's [[TriStar Pictures]] won the rights to adapt ''The Broken Earth'' trilogy in a seven-figure deal with Jemisin adapting the novels for the screen herself.<ref name="broken earth adaptation">{{cite web |last1=Fleming |first1=Mike Jr |title=N.K. Jemisin Book Series 'The Broken Earth' Lands At Sony's TriStar In 7-Figure Deal; Author To Adapt |url=https://deadline.com/2021/06/the-broken-earth-nk-jemisin-sony-tristar-7-figure-deal-author-adapts-1234769656/ |website=[[Deadline Hollywood]] |publisher=[[Penske Media Corporation]] |date=2021-06-04}}</ref> In 2021, she was included in the [[Time 100|''Time'' 100]], ''[[Time (magazine)|Time]]''{{'}}s annual list of the 100 most influential people in the world.<ref>{{cite web |last1=Specker |first1=Lawrence |title=Time's '100 most influential' list includes trio with Alabama ties |url=https://www.al.com/life/2021/09/times-100-most-influential-list-includes-trio-with-alabama-ties.html |website=[[List of Advance subsidiaries|AL.com]] |access-date=November 14, 2021 |language=en |date=September 15, 2021}}</ref>

Jemisin|last=Payne|first=Marshall|website=SFWA|date=June 28, 2011|access-date=January 14, 2016}}</ref> She is [[Cousin#1stOnceRem|first cousin once removed]] to stand-up comic and television host [[W. Kamau Bell]].<ref>Bell, W. Kamau. ''The Awkward Thoughts of W. Kamau Bell''. Jemisin is the first author to win three successive [[Hugo Awards]] for Best Novel, as well as the first to win for all novels in a trilogy.<ref name=SFE>{{cite web|url= http://www.sf-encyclopedia.com/entry/jemisin_n_k |title= Jemisin, N K|work=[[The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction]]|date=June 28, 2021|publisher= Gollancz|display-editors=2|editor-last=Clute|editor-first=John|editor-link=John Clute|editor2-last=Langford|editor2-first=David|editor2-link=David Langford|editor3-last=Nicholls|editor3-first=Peter (emeritus)|editor3-link=Peter Nicholls (writer)|editor4-last=Sleight|editor4-first=Graham (managing)|editor4-link=Graham Sleight|edition=3rd|url-status=live|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20210114011233/http://www.sf-encyclopedia.com/entry/jemisin_n_k |archive-date=January 14, 2021}}</ref> 
She has also received the following accolades:

* ''The Hundred Thousand Kingdoms'' (2010) won the Sense of Gender Award, and was nominated for the [[Crawford Award]],<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.sfadb.com/db/William_L_Crawford_-_IAFA_Fantasy_Award_2011 |title=William L. Crawford – IAFA Fantasy Award 2011 |access-date=September 7, 2016 |work=[[Science Fiction Awards Database]] |publisher=Locus Science Fiction Foundation}}</ref> [[David Gemmell Legend Awards|Gemmell Award]] for Best Fantasy Newcomer, [[:fr:Prix Imaginales|Prix Imaginales]] for Best Foreign Novel and [[Tiptree Award]] for Best Novel.<ref name=SFADB/>
* ''The Broken Kingdoms'' (2010) and ''The Shadowed Sun'' (2012) both won the [[Romantic Times]] Reviewers' Choice Award for Best Fantasy Novel.
* ''The City We Became'' (2020) won the [[BSFA Award for Best Novel]].<ref>https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Alt55nkjXGI "BSFA 2020 Awards Announcement", accessed on 4 April 2021</ref>
