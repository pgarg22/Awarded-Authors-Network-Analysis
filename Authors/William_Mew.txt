'''William Mew''' (Mewe) (1602 &ndash; 1669?) was an English clergyman, a member of the [[Westminster Assembly]]. He is known also for a drama, ''Pseudomagia'', and for the contribution to [[beekeeping]] of the design for a transparent [[Beehive|hive]].

==Life==
Mew was a graduate of [[Emmanuel College, Cambridge]], where he was admitted in 1618 and was B.A. in 1622.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.philological.bham.ac.uk/cruso/intro.html |title=intro |publisher=Philological.bham.ac.uk |access-date=2 February 2012}}</ref><ref>{{acad|id=MW618W|name=Mew, William}}</ref> Mew held also a B.D. degree. ''Pseudomagia'', a [[New Latin language|Neo-Latin]] drama, is thought to have been performed at Emmanuel around 1626.<ref>John C. Coldewey and Brian P. Copenhaver (editors and translators), ''Pseudomagia, A 17th-Century Neo-Latin Tragicomedy'' (1979).</ref><ref>John C. Coldewey and Brian F. Copenhaver, ''William Mewe, Pseudomagia, Aquila Cruso, Euribates Pseudomagus, John Chappell (?), Susenbrotus, or Fortuna, Zelotypus, Prepared with an Introduction''  (Renaissance Latin Drama in England series II.14, Hildesheim, 1991).</ref><ref>[https://archive.org/details/collegeplaysperf00smituoft George Charles Moore Smith, ''College plays performed in the University of Cambridge'' (1923), p. 9. (PDF)]</ref>

On 29 November 1643 he preached a fast-day sermon to Parliament, later printed as ''The Robbing and Spoiling of Jacob and Israel''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.pcahistory.org/sermons/isa.html |title=under "Meuve, William" |publisher=Pcahistory.org |access-date=2 February 2012}}</ref> He is mentioned for constant attendance in the Westminster Assembly.<ref>James Reid, ''Memoirs of the Lives and Writings of Those Eminent Divines, who Convened in the Famous Assembly at Westminster, in the Seventeenth Century'' (1811), p. 83.</ref> He was approached to answer [[Milton's divorce tracts]], as he wrote in 1659 to [[Richard Baxter]].<ref>Jason Peacey, ''Politicians and Pamphleteers: Propaganda During the English Civil Wars and Interregnum'' (2004), p. 179.</ref> In this frank correspondence Baxter expressed his deepest fears and suspicions, becoming at one point (6 August 1659) "hysterical".<ref>William M. Lamont, ''Richard Baxter and the Millennium'' (1979), p. 199.</ref>

He became vicar of [[Eastington, Stroud]] in Gloucestershire, for which the patron was [[Nathaniel Stephens]], a local MP and one of [[Oliver Cromwell]]'s colonels.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.chavenage.com/history.html |title=History of Chavenage |publisher=Chavenage.com |access-date=2 February 2012}}</ref> Previously he had been a lecturer in London. Known as a preacher, he conformed in 1662.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.british-history.ac.uk/report.aspx?compid=15786 |title=Eastington – Churches &#124; A History of the County of Gloucester: Volume 10 (pp. 135–138) |publisher=British-history.ac.uk |date=15 December 1953 |access-date=2 February 2012}}</ref> He was a commissioner for Gloucestershire in 1654.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.british-history.ac.uk/report.aspx?compid=56576 |title=August 1654 – An Ordinance for ejecting Scandalous, Ignorant and insufficient Ministers and Schoolmasters. &#124; Acts and Ordinances of the Interregnum, 1642–1660 (pp. 968–990) |publisher=British-history.ac.uk |date=22 June 2003 |access-date=2 February 2012}}</ref>

Mew's hive was made known by [[Samuel Hartlib]].<ref>[[Samuel Hartlib]], ''Reformed Commonwealth of Bees'' (1655), p. 43 (''A copy of a letter written by Mr. William Mew, Minister at Eastlington in Gloucestershire to Mr. Nathanil Angelo, Fellow of Eaton Colledg'').</ref> Mew's design followed a suggestion in [[Pliny the Elder|Pliny]] and proved influential, being adapted by [[John Wilkins]] and [[Christopher Wren]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mhs.ox.ac.uk/gatt/circle/catalog.asp?CN=65 |title=Hartlib Circle Catalogue Number 65 |publisher=Mhs.ox.ac.uk |access-date=2 February 2012}}</ref>
