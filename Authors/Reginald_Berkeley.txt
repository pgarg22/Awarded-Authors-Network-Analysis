'''Reginald Cheyne Berkeley''' {{post-nominals|country=GBR|size=100%|MC}} (18 August 1890 – 30 March 1935<ref name="imdb">{{IMDb name|id=0075351|name=Reginald Berkeley}}</ref><ref>{{cite LAF|id=n83-139979}}</ref>) was a [[Liberal Party (UK)|Liberal Party]] politician in the United Kingdom, and later a writer of stage plays, then a screenwriter in Hollywood. He had trained as a lawyer. He died in Los Angeles from [[pneumonia]] after an operation.<ref>{{cite book |title= Who's Who & Who Was Who |year=2012 |publisher= Oxford University Press |location= Oxford }}</ref>

His son [[Humphry Berkeley]] was a Conservative MP in the United Kingdom.

== Early life ==

Berkeley was born in London to Humphry George Berkeley and Agnes Mary née Cheyne. He was educated privately and at [[Bedford Modern School]]. He later went to [[Fiji]] where his father was a prominent lawyer in [[Suva]]; then to [[Auckland]], New Zealand, where he studied at [[Auckland University College]] and passed the Barristers Examination of the [[University of New Zealand]].<ref>''The Evening Post'' (Wellington) 27 January 1911 pp. 2 & 3 April 1911 p. 3</ref> He was admitted to the Bar of Fiji and New Zealand in 1912, and to the [[Middle Temple]] (London) on 2 July 1919.<ref>{{cite book |author= Livai’a, Christine |title= Qaravi na'i tavi: They did their duty; soldiers from Fiji in the Great War |year=2009 |publisher= Polygraphia |location= Auckland |page= 200 |isbn= 978-1-877332-62-3}}</ref> He was a lieutenant in the 3rd (Auckland) Regiment of the territorials in New Zealand from 1911 to 1913.<ref>''The New Zealand Gazette'' (Wellington) 8 June 1911 p 1897; 2 & 30 May 1912, pp 1495 & 1788; 15 May 1913 p 1617</ref>

Berkeley served in [[World War I]] as a captain in the [[Rifle Brigade]]. He was awarded the [[Military Cross]] in 1916. His citation reads: {{quote|For conspicuous gallantry in action. When his company commander was wounded, he led the company with great dash to its final objective, and later displayed great determination in consolidating the captured line.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.london-gazette.co.uk/issues/29824/supplements/11046|title=Page 11046 {{!}} Supplement 29824, 14 November 1916 {{!}} London Gazette|work=The Gazette|access-date=22 May 2018}}</ref>}} Postwar he joined the staff of the [[League of Nations]] Union in 1919 as Editor of Pamphlets then Director of Propaganda, and the League Secretariat in 1921, resigning when he entered Parliament.

== Politician ==
He was elected as [[Member of Parliament (United Kingdom)|Member of Parliament]] (MP) for [[Nottingham Central (UK Parliament constituency)|Nottingham Central]] at the [[1922 United Kingdom general election|1922 general election]], winning the seat with a majority of only 22 votes over the sitting [[Conservative Party (UK)|Conservative]] MP [[Albert Atkey]]. He was re-elected in another two-way contest in [[1923 United Kingdom general election|1923]], but did not contest the [[1924 United Kingdom general election|1924 general election]]. Labour fielded a candidate for the first time in Nottingham Central, and the Conservatives retook the seat.

He unsuccessfully contested the [[1929 United Kingdom general election|1929 general election]] in [[Aberdeen North (UK Parliament constituency)|Aberdeen North]], and stood again in Nottingham Central at the [[1930 Nottingham Central by-election|by-election in 1930]], where he won only 16.9% of the votes. He was defeated again in [[Aberdeen and Kincardine Central (UK Parliament constituency)|Aberdeen and Kincardine Central]] at the [[1931 United Kingdom general election|1931 election]]. These defeats reflected the national decline in the fortunes of the Liberal Party and the rise of the Labour Party.

== Writer ==
His stage plays include ''[[Florence Nightingale#Theatre|The Lady with the Lamp]]'' (1929), based on the life of Florence Nightingale and starring [[Edith Evans]] in the title role, and ''The Man I Killed'' (1931), which was adapted for the screen as ''[[Broken Lullaby]]'' the following year. His play ''French Leave''(1920) was filmed twice, once [[French Leave (1930 film)|in 1930]], and again [[French Leave (1937 film)|in 1937]].<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Yyqc0Qa6b60C|title=The Complete Index to Literary Sources in Film|first=Alan|last=Goble|date=1 January 1999|publisher=Walter de Gruyter|isbn=9783110951943}}</ref> His screenwriting credits include ''[[Dreyfus (1931 film)|Dreyfus]]'' (1931), ''[[Cavalcade (1933 film)|Cavalcade]]'' (1933), ''[[The World Moves On]]'' (1934), ''[[Carolina (1934 film)|Carolina]]'' (1934) and ''[[Nurse Edith Cavell]]'' (1939).<ref name="imdb" />

He died in 1935 in the [[Good Samaritan Hospital (Los Angeles)|Good Samaritan Hospital]], Los Angeles aged 44 from pneumonia following a major operation.<ref>''The Times'' (London) 1 April 1935 p 9</ref> He was residing at 606 North Crescent Drive, [[Beverly Hills]].

== Personal life ==
He had married Gwendoline Cock in 1914 and Clara Hildegarde Digby in 1926.
