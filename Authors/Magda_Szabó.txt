'''Magda Szabó''' (October 5, 1917 &ndash; November 19, 2007) was a [[Hungarians|Hungarian]] novelist. Doctor of philology, she also wrote [[drama]]s, [[essay]]s, studies, [[memoir]]s, poetry and children's literature. She was a founding member of the {{ill|Digital Literary Academy|hu|Digitális Irodalmi Akadémia}}, an online digital repository of Hungarian literature. She is the most translated Hungarian author, with publications in 42 countries and over 30 languages.<ref name=":3" /><ref name=":4" />

== Early life ==
Magda Szabó was born in [[Debrecen]], [[Austria-Hungary]] in 1917.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=http://www.frankfurt.matav.hu/angol/irok/szabo/elet.htm|title=Magda Szabó|website=Frankfurt '99 Non-Profit Organization}}</ref>

In 1940, she graduated from the [[University of Debrecen]] as a teacher of [[Latin]] and of Hungarian.<ref name=":0" /> She began teaching in the same year at the [[Protestantism|Protestant]] Girls Boarding School in Debrecen and [[Hódmezővásárhely]].<ref name=":0" /> From 1945 to 1949, she worked in the Ministry of Religion and Education.<ref name=":0" />

She married the writer and translator Tibor Szobotka (1913&ndash;1982) in 1947.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/news/2007/nov/28/guardianobituaries.booksobituaries|title=Obituary: Magda Szabó|last=Gömöri|first=George|date=2007-11-28|work=The Guardian|access-date=2019-04-18|language=en-GB|issn=0261-3077}}</ref>


== Writing career ==
Szabó began her writing career as a poet and published her first book of [[poetry]], ''Bárány'' ("Lamb"), in 1947, which was followed by ''Vissza az emberig'' ("Back to the Human") in 1949.<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=http://www.kontextus.hu/kia/kia.php?ID=122&action=full|title=Szabó Magda|website=Kortárs Irodalmi Adattár|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20050826030834/http://www.kontextus.hu/kia/kia.php?ID=122&action=full|archive-date=2005-08-26|url-status=dead}}</ref> In 1949 she was awarded the [[Baumgarten Prize]], which was immediately withdrawn when Szabó was labeled an enemy to the [[Communist party|Communist Party]].<ref name=":5" /> She was dismissed from the Ministry in the same year.<ref name=":5" /> The [[Stalinism|Stalinist]] era from 1949 to 1956 censored any literature, such as Szabó's work, that did not conform to [[socialist realism]].<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=http://mek.oszk.hu/02000/02042/html/71.html|title=A History of Hungarian Literature|last=Czigány|first=Lóránt|date=1986|website=Library of Hungarian Studies}}</ref> Since her husband was also censored by the communist regime, she was forced to teach in a [[Calvinism|Calvinist]] girls' school until 1959.<ref name=":1" /><ref name=":3">{{Cite web|url=http://www.hunlit.hu/szabomagda,en|title=Magda Szabó|date=2016|website=Publishing Hungary}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|date=2017-07-04|title=A Powerful and Haunting Story of Two Very Different Women: Magda Szabó's "The Door"|url=https://onartandaesthetics.com/2017/07/04/a-powerful-and-haunting-story-of-two-very-different-women-magda-szabos-the-door/|access-date=2020-06-09|website=On Art and Aesthetics|language=en}}</ref>

She wrote her first [[novel]], ''Freskó'' ("Fresco") during these years, which was published in 1958.<ref name=":2" /> The novel tells the story of a puritan family coming together for a funeral, while examining questions of hypocrisy and reflecting on [[History of Hungary|Hungarian histor<nowiki/>y]].<ref name=":4">{{Cite web|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/obituaries/magda-szabo-acclaimed-author-of-the-door-758994.html|title=Magda Szabó: Acclaimed author of 'The Door'|website=The Independent}}</ref> In the same year, she published another book of poetry titled ''Bárány Boldizsár'' ("Lawrence the Lamb") and a novel written for a younger female audience titled ''Mondják meg Zsófikának'' ("Tell Young Sophie").<ref name=":4" /> ''Az őz'' ("The Fawn") was published in 1959, a novel centered around an actress and her struggle to overcome a difficult, impoverished childhood.<ref name=":3" /> In this novel, Szabó effectively portrays the psychological, internal world of the modern woman.<ref name=":3" /> In 1961 and 1962, Szabó published two more novels for young women, ''Álarcosbál'' ("Masked Ball") and ''Születésnap'' ("Birthday") respectively.<ref name=":2" /><ref name=":3" />  ''Pilátus'' ("Iza's Ballad"), the story of a female doctor and her relationship with her mother, was published in 1963.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/11/13/books/review/magda-szabo-izas-ballad.html|title=In Magda Szabo's Novel, A Widow is Uprooted From What She Loves|last=Groff|first=Lauren|date=November 11, 2016|website=The New York Times}}</ref> ''Tündér Lala'' ("Lara the Fairy"), her 1965 novel, is one of the most popular [[Children's literature|novels for children]] written in Hungarian.<ref name=":2" /><ref name=":4" /> In 1969, she published ''Katalin utca'' ("Katalin Street"), a [[Realism (arts)|realistic]] depiction of post-World War II life.<ref name=":3" /> Her most widely read novel ''[[Abigél (novel)|Abigél]]'' ("Abigail", 1970) is an [[Adventure fiction|adventure story]] about a young girl living in eastern Hungary during [[World War II]].<ref name=":4" /> The novel's success resulted in a [[Television program|TV series]], produced in 1978;<ref name=":3" /> the novel was also adapted into a musical that premiered in March 2008. In 1971, Szabó began a series of [[Autobiography|autobiographical]] works, which depict her family history. The first of this series is the short novel, ''Ókút'' ("The Ancient Well"), followed by ''Régimódi történet'' ("Old-Fashioned Story").<ref name=":3" /><ref name=":4" /> In 2002, Szabó continued this autobiographical series with ''Für Elise'', a recollection of the author's life from 1917 to 1935.<ref name=":3" /> Today, this is one of her most popular works in Hungarian.<ref name=":3" />

In 1975, Szabó published a collection of plays titled ''Az órák és a farkasok'' ("The Wolf Hours").<ref name=":4" /> She published two more dramas in 1984, ''Erőnk szerint'' ("According to Our Strength") and ''Béla Király'' ("King Béla").<ref name=":4" />

Her novel ''Az ajtó'' ([[The Door (novel)|''The Door'']]) was published in 1987 and would become one of her most famous works worldwide.<ref name=":3" /> The novel revolves around the relationship between two women, one a prominent Hungarian writer much like Szabó herself, and the other her cryptic housekeeper.<ref name=":3" /> [[Claire Messud]] writes in the ''[[The New York Times|New York Times]]'' that reading ''The Door'', has completely changed her outlook on life while [[Cynthia Zarin]], contributor to ''[[The New Yorker]],'' calls it "a bone-shaking book."<ref name=":5" /> ''The Door'' was translated into English in 1995 by Stefan Draughon and again in 2005 by [[Len Rix]].<ref name=":6">{{Cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2015/02/08/books/review/the-door-by-magda-szabo.html|title='The Door,' by Magda Szabo|last=Messud|first=Claire|date=February 6, 2015|website=The New York Times}}</ref>
