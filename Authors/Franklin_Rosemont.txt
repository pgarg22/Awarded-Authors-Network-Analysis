'''Franklin Rosemont''' (2 October 1943 &ndash; 12 April 2009) was an American poet, artist, historian, street speaker, and co-founder of the [[Chicago Surrealist Group]].<ref>[http://www.counterpunch.org/rosemont04162009.html The Surreal Life of Franklin Rosemont] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090422072647/http://www.counterpunch.org/rosemont04162009.html |date=2009-04-22 }} by Paul Garon, David Roediger and Kate Khatib, April 16, 2009</ref> Over four decades, Franklin produced a body of work, of declarations, manifestos, poetry, collage, hidden histories, and other interventions intended to inspire a new generation of revolution, and became perhaps "the most productive scholar of labor and the left in the United States."<ref>[http://www.counterpunch.org/rosemont04162009.html The Surreal Life of Franklin Rosemont] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090422072647/http://www.counterpunch.org/rosemont04162009.html |date=2009-04-22 }} by Paul Garon, David Roediger and Kate Khatib, April 16, 2009</ref>

== Early life ==

He was born in [[Chicago, Illinois]], to Henry, a [[typography|typographer]] and labor activist, and Sally, a [[jazz]] musician.<ref>[http://www.columbia.edu/~lnp3/mydocs/culture/american_surrealism.htm Bio]</ref> In 1960, he dropped out of [[Proviso East High School]], Maywood, Illinois, but was admitted to  [[Roosevelt University]] in Chicago in 1962, studying under African-American scholar<ref>{{Cite web | url=http://www.counterpunch.org/2009/04/16/the-surreal-life-of-franklin-rosemont/ |title = The Surreal Life of Franklin Rosemont}}</ref> [[St. Clair Drake]].<ref name="ruff">{{cite book|last1=Ruff|first1=Allen|title=We Called Each Other Comrade: Charles H. Kerr & Company, Radical Publishers|date=2011|publisher=PM Press|location=Oakland|page=x}}</ref>

== Career ==
He edited and wrote an introduction for ''What is Surrealism?: Selected Writings of Andr√© Breton'', and edited ''Rebel Worker'',<ref>Abigail Susik, ''Surrealist Sabotage and the War on Work'', Manchester University Press, 2021, p. 195</ref>''[[Arsenal/Surrealist Subversion]]'', ''The Rise & Fall of the DIL Pickle: Jazz-Age Chicago's Wildest & Most Outrageously Creative Hobohemian Nightspot'' and ''Juice Is Stranger Than Friction: Selected Writings of T-Bone Slim''. With his wife [[Penelope Rosemont]], herself the author and editor of several books and active in the Chicago Surrealists, and poet and storyteller [[Paul Garon]], he edited ''The Forecast is Hot!''. His work has been deeply concerned with both the history of surrealism (writing a forward for ''[[Max Ernst]] and [[Alchemy]]: A Magician in Search of Myth'') and of the radical labor movement in America,<ref>Abigail Susik, ''Surrealist Sabotage and the War on Work'', Manchester University Press, 2021, pp. 189-190</ref> for instance, writing a biography of [[Joe Hill (activist)|Joe Hill]]. According to ''PoetrySoup.com'' Franklin Rosemont "became perhaps "the most productive scholar of labor and the left in the United States."<ref>{{Cite web | url=https://www.poetrysoup.com/quotes/franklin_rosemont | title=Franklin Rosemont Quotes &#124; Quotations about or by Franklin Rosemont}}</ref>

== Publications ==
Rosemont is the author of the poetry collections ''The Morning of a Machine Gun: Twenty Poems & Documents. Profusely Illustrated By the Author'', ''The Apple of the Automatic Zebra's Eye'', and ''Penelope: A Poem'', as well as ''An Open Entrance to the Shut Palace of Wrong Numbers'', a book that explores the phenomenon of "wrong numbers" from a surrealist perspective, which was published by Black Swan Press in 2003. He also edited and introduced ''Hobohemia: Emma Goldman, Lucy Parsons, Ben Reitman & other agitators & outsiders in 1920s/30s Chicago'', by Frank O. Beck.<ref>{{cite book

In 1990 he published a collected edition of short stories by the socialist utopian author [[Edward Bellamy]], titled ''Apparitions of Things to Come''.  He is co-editor, with [[Archie Green]], [[David Roediger]], and Salvatore Salerno, of ''The Big Red Songbook'' (Chicago: Charles H. Kerr, 2007).
