'''Joseph Bathanti''' (born July 20, 1953, [[Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania]]) is an American poet, novelist and professor. He was named by [[Governor of North Carolina|Governor]] [[Bev Perdue]] as the seventh [[North Carolina Poet Laureate]], 2012–2014.

==Biography==
===Early life and education===
Bathanti was born July 20, 1953, in [[Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania]], and grew up in the [[East Liberty (Pittsburgh)|East Liberty]] area of Pittsburgh.<ref name="NCAC about">{{cite web|title=About Joseph Bathanti|url=http://ncarts.org/resources/poetry-resources/about-joseph-bathanti/|publisher=North Carolina Arts Council|accessdate=March 29, 2014|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140328203213/http://ncarts.org/resources/poetry-resources/about-joseph-bathanti/|archivedate=March 28, 2014}}</ref><ref name="Sostek">{{cite news|last=Sostek|first=Anya|title=Pittsburgh native Joseph Bathanti named poet laureate of North Carolina|url=http://old.post-gazette.com/pg/12258/1261600-44.stm|accessdate=November 5, 2012|newspaper=Pittsburgh Post-Gazette|date=September 14, 2012}}</ref> His grandparents were immigrants from Italy and France. His working-class family included a steelworker father and a seamstress mother.<ref name="NCAC about" />

After graduating from [[Central Catholic High School (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)|Central Catholic High School]] in Pittsburgh,<ref name="Sostek" /> he attended [[California University of Pennsylvania|California State College]]. In 1972, he transferred to the [[University of Pittsburgh]] and, in 1975, received a [[Bachelor of Arts|bachelor's degree]] in English.<ref name=PCftB>{{cite web|last=Cavanaugh|first=Kerry|title=Biography for Joseph Bathanti|url=https://secureapps.libraries.psu.edu/PACFTB/bios/biography.cfm?AuthorID=7377|work=Pennsylvania Center for the Book|publisher=The Pennsylvania State University|accessdate=November 6, 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last=Bathanti|first=Joseph|title=Half Of What I Say Is Meaningless|journal=The Sun|date=July 2003|pages=18–23|url=http://www.thesunmagazine.org/_media/CDS/Wildacres2011/Bathanti_HalfOfWhatISayIsMeaningless.pdf|accessdate=November 6, 2012}}</ref>

===Personal life===
Bathanti lives in [[Vilas, North Carolina]], with his wife, Joan, and two children. Bathanti and his wife met while both were working with the VISTA program.<ref name="SalPost" />

==Career==
After graduating from the University of Pittsburgh, Bathanti traveled to North Carolina in 1976 as part of the [[AmeriCorps VISTA|Volunteers In Service To America]] (VISTA) program focusing on prison outreach.<ref name=PCftB /> He has continued to teach writing and hold workshops in prisons ever since.<ref name="SalPost">{{cite news|title=Joseph Bathanti named N.C. Poet Laureate|url=http://www.salisburypost.com/News/090212-book-Bathanti|accessdate=November 6, 2012|newspaper=The Salisbury Post|date=September 2, 2012|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130630142611/http://www.salisburypost.com/News/090212-book-Bathanti|archive-date=June 30, 2013|url-status=dead}}</ref>

From 1985 to 1989, he worked closely with the North Carolina Visiting Artist Program which sought to bring talented artists from different disciplines to more rural towns and areas in the state.<ref name="SalPost" /> His book ''They Changed the State: The Legacy of North Carolina’s Visiting Artists, 1971-95'' chronicled the history of the program.<ref name=PCftB />

Bathanti is currently a professor of creative writing at [[Appalachian State University]]. Also at Appalachian State, he is Director of Writing in the Field as well as [[Artist-in-residence|Writer-in-Residence]] for the [[Watauga Global Community]].<ref name="NCAC about" /> He also serves as a mentor in the [[Master of Fine Arts]] program at [[Carlow University]] in Pittsburgh.<ref>{{cite web|title=Joseph Bathanti MFA|url=http://www.carlow.edu/9366.aspx|work=Directory|publisher=Carlow University|accessdate=March 29, 2014}}</ref>

He was installed as the seventh North Carolina Poet Laureate on September 20, 2012, at a ceremony in [[Raleigh, North Carolina]].<ref name="SalPost" /> Over the next two years he became an "ambassador of N.C. literature" and was free to create his own long-term projects. The position requires the laureate to participate in various literary activities across the state, working with "schools, community groups, and the press."<ref>{{cite web|title=N.C. Poet Laureate Job Description |url=http://ncarts.org/resources/poetry-resources/north-carolina-poet-laureate/ |publisher=North Carolina Arts Council |accessdate=March 29, 2014 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140402140307/http://ncarts.org/resources/poetry-resources/north-carolina-poet-laureate/ |archivedate=April 2, 2014 }}</ref>

Since his appointment, he has been a part of over 250 events around North Carolina. In 2014, Bathanti was named the first scholar-in-residence for the [[Heinz History Center]]'s Italian American Program in Pittsburgh. Focusing on [[Italian American]] history, he will create a body of work developed from the center's Italian American Collection.<ref>{{cite news|last=Barcousky|first=Len|title=N.C. poet laureate sets up shop in Heinz History Center|url=http://www.post-gazette.com/ae/books/2014/01/20/N-C-poet-laureate-sets-up-shop-in-history-center/stories/201401200065#ixzz2xNji14hA|accessdate=March 29, 2014|newspaper=The Pittsburgh Post-Gazette|date=January 19, 2014}}</ref>
