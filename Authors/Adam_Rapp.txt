'''Adam Rapp''' (born June 15, 1968) is an American novelist, playwright, screenwriter, musician and film director.<ref name=Gale>{{cite web|title=Adam Rapp|url=http://ic.galegroup.com/ic/bic1/ReferenceDetailsPage/ReferenceDetailsWindow?displayGroupName=Reference&disableHighlighting=false&prodId=BIC1&action=e&windowstate=normal&catId=&documentId=GALE%7CH1000143129&mode=view&userGroupName=lln_louis&jsid=fb15b53857f9e2daf7a8269f389d2d4d|work=Contemporary Authors Online|publisher=Gale Biography in Context|access-date=November 21, 2011}}</ref> His play ''[[Red Light Winter]]'' was a [[Pulitzer Prize]] finalist in 2006.<ref name=Pulitzer>{{cite web|title=Drama|url=http://www.pulitzer.org/bycat/Drama|work=The Pulitzer Prizes|publisher=Columbia University|access-date=November 21, 2011}}</ref>

==Early life==
The son of Mary Lee (née Baird; died 1997) and Douglas Rapp, Adam Rapp was born in Chicago and spent most of his youth in [[Joliet, Illinois]].<ref name="goodman">{{Cite web |url=https://next.goodmantheatre.org/lifestory/ |title=Life Story |date=2021-05-04 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210612153446/https://next.goodmantheatre.org/lifestory/ |archive-date=2021-06-12 |website=Goodman Theatre |access-date=2021-06-12 |url-status=live}}</ref>{{citation needed |date=June 2021 |reason=detail on Joliet upbringing}}

He is a graduate of [[St. John's Northwestern Military Academy|St. John's Military Academy]] ([[Delafield, Wisconsin]]) and [[Clarke University|Clarke College]] ([[Dubuque, Iowa]]).<ref name="goodman"/> At Clarke, he captained the varsity basketball team.<ref name="E-view With Adam Rapp">{{cite web|last=Angel|first=Ann|title=E-view With Adam Rapp|url=http://scholar.lib.vt.edu/ejournals/ALAN/v28n1/angel2.html|work=The Alan Review|publisher=Virginia Tech|access-date=November 21, 2011}}</ref>

After college he moved to New York City's East Village, where he landed a day job in book publishing and wrote fiction and plays at night. He later completed a two-year playwriting fellowship at [[Juilliard School]].<ref name="goodman"/><ref name="E-view With Adam Rapp"/> His younger brother is actor-singer [[Anthony Rapp]].

==Career==
===Plays===
Rapp attended the O'Neill Playwrights Conference in 1996.<ref name="Looking Both Ways">{{cite web|last=Hart|first=Sarah|title=Looking Both Ways|url=http://www.tcg.org/publications/at/julyaugust08/looking.cfm|work=American Theatre|publisher=Theatre Communications Group|access-date=November 21, 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120405123641/http://www.tcg.org/publications/at/julyaugust08/looking.cfm|archive-date=April 5, 2012|url-status=dead|df=mdy-all}}</ref> His play ''Finer Noble Gases'' was staged by the [[Eugene O'Neill Theatre]] in 2000, by [[Actors Theatre of Louisville]] in 2001, by [[Carolina Actors Studio Theatre]] in Charlotte in 2003, and by [[Rattlestick Playwrights Theater]] in New York City in 2004.<ref name="20th century drama"/> In 2001, ''Nocturne'' was premiered by the [[New York Theatre Workshop]].<ref name="20th century drama"/>  It has also been staged at by [[American Repertory Theater]] and [[Berkeley Repertory Theatre]].<ref name="20th century drama">{{cite web|title=Author Details|url=http://solomon.nadr.alexanderstreet.com.libezp.lib.lsu.edu/cgi-bin/asp/philo/nadr/authoridx.pl?docauthorid=per0058288&showfullrecord=on|work=Twentieth Century North American Drama|publisher=Alexander Street Press|access-date=November 22, 2011}}</ref> His play ''Stone Cold Dead Serious'' was produced in 2002 by the [[American Repertory Theater]].<ref name="20th century drama"/>

His play ''[[Red Light Winter]]'' received the [[Joseph Jefferson Awards|Joseph Jefferson Award]] (Best New Work) in 2005 for its production at [[Steppenwolf Theatre Company]].<ref>[http://www.jeffawards.org/News/nomineesrecipints2005.cfm "Press Release"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160304113327/http://www.jeffawards.org/News/nomineesrecipints2005.cfm |date=March 4, 2016 }} jeffawards.org, November 7, 2005</ref> The play ran [[Off-Broadway]] at the Barrow Street Theatre from January 20, 2006 to June 25, 2006, directed by Rapp. The play was nominated for the 2006 [[Lucille Lortel Award]], Outstanding Play, and Rapp received the 2006 [[Obie Award]], Special Citation.<ref>[http://iobdb.com/Production/4199 "''Red Light Winter'' Off-Broadway"] Internet Off-Broadway Database, accessed September 22, 2019</ref> The play was a finalist for the [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]] in 2006.<ref name=Pulitzer/> 

Rapp directed a production of ''Los Angeles'', by Julian Sheppard, in 2007 at the Flea Theatre. In 2011, Rapp's ''The Metal Children'' was given its regional debut by [[Swine Palace]] on [[Louisiana State University]]'s campus.<ref name="Culture Candy">{{cite web|title=Calendar of Events|url=http://culturecandy.org/calendar/event/8948|publisher=Culture Candy|access-date=November 21, 2011|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120425062214/http://culturecandy.org/calendar/event/8948|archive-date=April 25, 2012}}</ref>

He has said that the Edge Theater Company in New York City is his "artistic home": "Edge Theater changed my life back in 2003. They are my family."<ref>Rapp, Adam. [https://www./buzz/6224/adam-rapp-finding-my-theater-family/ "Adam Rapp: Finding My Theater Family"] broadway.com, March 21, 2007</ref>

He made his [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]] debut with his play ''[[The Sound Inside (play)|The Sound Inside]]'', which began playing at [[Studio 54]] starting on September 14, 2019 (opening officially on October 17, 2019) starring [[Mary-Louise Parker]].<ref>Clement, Olivia. [http://www.playbill.com/article/adam-rapps-the-sound-inside-starring-mary-louise-parker-begins-on-broadway# "Adam Rapp’s The Sound Inside, Starring Mary-Louise Parker, Begins on Broadway"] Playbill, September 14, 2019</ref> The play premiered at the Williamstown Theatre Festival in 2018.<ref>[https://www.broadwayworld.com/article/Review-Roundup-Critics-Weigh-In-on-Adam-Rapps-THE-SOUND-INSIDE-at-Williamstown-Theatre-Festival-20180703 "Review Roundup: Critics Weigh-In on Adam Rapp's ''The Sound Inside'' at Williamstown Theatre Festival"] broadwayworld.com, July 3, 2018</ref>

===Teaching===
He has taught at the [[Yale School of Drama]].<ref name="Cutting Loose">{{cite journal|last=Ng|first=David|title=Cutting Loose With Adam Rapp|journal=American Theatre|date=October 2007|pages=38–41|url=http://www.tcg.org/publications/at/oct07/rapp.cfm|access-date=November 21, 2011|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20071012201202/http://www.tcg.org/publications/at/oct07/rapp.cfm|archive-date=October 12, 2007|url-status=dead|df=mdy-all}}</ref> 

===Style===
The majority of Rapp's plays feature small casts and are set in small spaces.<ref name=Gale/> Many characters in the plays are [[working class]] Americans.<ref name="Project Muse">{{cite journal|last=Cummings|first=Scott T|title=26th Annual Humana Festival of New American Plays|journal=Theatre Journal|date=April 2002|volume=54|pages=635–39|doi=10.1353/tj.2002.0118|s2cid=201743308|url=http://muse.jhu.edu.libezp.lib.lsu.edu/journals/theatre_journal/v054/54.4cummings.html|access-date=November 22, 2011}}</ref> His plays often combine stories of Midwestern longing with the idea of finding escape in New York. He combines humor with gloom, preferring dark themes<ref name="Cutting Loose"/>

In a conversation with fellow playwright [[Gina Gionfriddo]] published in ''[[The Brooklyn Rail]]'', Rapp says: "When you see something powerfully acted on stage, it hits a nerve in the way music hits a nerve … Watching someone twelve feet from you falling in love or being abused … There’s something raw about that experience that you don’t get from film or TV."<ref>{{cite journal|last=Gionfriddo|first=Gina|author2=Adam Rapp|title=Peering in at the Zoo: Adam Rapp and Gina Gionfriddo on American Theater|journal=The Brooklyn Rail|date=November 2007|url=http://brooklynrail.org/2007/11/theater/in-dialogue-nov07}}</ref>

===Novels===
Rapp's first [[young adult novel]], ''Missing the Piano'', was published in 1994.  After writing his second book, ''The Buffalo Tree'', which was published in 1997, Rapp was invited to be the first author in residence at [[Ridgewood High School (Illinois)|Ridgewood High School]].<ref name=eric>{{cite web|last=Blubaugh|first=Penny|title=An Author in Residence? Why Bother?|url=http://www.eric.ed.gov/PDFS/ED452529.pdf|publisher=Educational Resources Information Center|access-date=November 21, 2011}}</ref> ''The Buffalo Tree'' was censored by the Muhlenberg School Board in [[Reading, Pennsylvania]] due to its themes, graphic language and sexual content.<ref name="censorship battle">{{cite news|last=Hatza|first=George|title=Censorship Battle over the Buffalo Tree|url=http://readingeagle.com/article.aspx?id=223596|access-date=November 23, 2011|newspaper=Reading Eagle|date=May 30, 2010}}</ref> His 2003 novel ''[[33 Snowfish]]'' was one of [[Young Adult Library Services Association]]'s [[Top Ten Best Books for Young Adults]].<ref name="The English Journal">{{cite journal| last=Gallo| first=Don| title=Bold Books for Innovative Teaching: Summer Reading 2004|journal=The English Journal|date=July 2004|volume=93|issue=6|pages=112–15| doi=10.2307/4128905|jstor=4128905}}</ref>  He released ''Under the Wolf, Under the Dog'' in 2004.

His first adult novel, ''The Year of Endless Sorrows'', was released in 2006.<ref>Gans, Andrew. [http://www.playbill.com/news/article/rapp-discusses-new-endless-sorrows-novel-jan.-22-137848# "Rapp Discusses New ''Endless Sorrows'' Novel Jan. 22"] Playbill, January 27, 2007</ref> Rapp made his [[graphic novel]] debut with the release of ''Ball Peen Hammer'' in September 2009.<ref>[http://www.goodreads.com/book/show/6514961-ball-peen-hammer ''Ball Peen Hammer''] goodreads.com, accessed November 11, 2015</ref><ref>Hogan, John. [http://www.graphicnovelreporter.com/authors/adam-rapp/news/interview-092909 "The Art of the 'Ball Peen Hammer'"] graphicnovelreporter.com, accessed November 11, 2015</ref> His second graphic novel, ''Decelerate Blue'' was published in February 2017.

===Film, television and music===
Rapp directed his first film, ''[[Winter Passing]]'' with [[Zooey Deschanel]] and [[Will Ferrell]] (2005),<ref>[http://www.tcm.com/tcmdb/title/534733/Winter-Passing/ ''Winter Passing''] tcm.com, accessed November 11, 2015</ref> and was a creative consultant for the television show ''[[The L Word]]''.<ref name=lword>Buckley, Michael. [http://www.playbill.com/features/article/stage-to-screens-chatting-with-playwright-and-screenwriter-adam-rapp-130950# "STAGE TO SCREENS: Chatting with Playwright and Screenwriter Adam Rapp"] Playbill, February 20, 2006</ref>

While working on ''[[The L Word]]'', Rapp left in the middle of the season to attend the [[Edinburgh Festival]], where he directed his play, ''Finer Noble Gases'', which won the Fringe First Award.<ref name="Looking Both Ways"/> He wrote for the 2010 season of [[HBO]]'s ''[[In Treatment (U.S. TV series)|In Treatment]]''.<ref>Shattuck, Kathryn. [https://www.nytimes.com/2010/11/14/arts/television/14treatment.html "Therapy? Not His Cup of Tea"] ''New York Times'', November 12, 2010</ref>

He was a member of the band Bottomside, which released the independent CD ''The Element Man'' in September 2004.<ref name=Bottomside>{{cite web|title=Bottomside |url=http://mog.com/artists/mn152327/bottomside |work=MOG |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140729011516/http://mog.com/artists/mn152327/bottomside|archive-date=July 29, 2014}}</ref> He is a member of "Less the Band", which released the album ''Bear'' in April 2006.<ref name="Less the Band">{{cite news|title=Less the Band|publisher=Louisville Music}}</ref>
