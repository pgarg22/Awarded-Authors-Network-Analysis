'''J. Clifford Ashby''', generally known as '''Cliff Ashby''', (10 November 1919 â€“ 30 April 2012<ref name="happenstance"/>) was a British poet and novelist.

He was born in [[Norfolk]] in 1919,<ref name="jrank">{{Cite web |url=http://www.jrank.org/literature/pages/3215/Cliff-Ashby.html |title=Cliff Ashby Biography |publisher=JRank.org Literature Reference}}</ref> and left school aged 14, taking a job as a window dresser in Leeds.

He was a [[conscientious objector]] in the [[Second World War]], undertaking agricultural work in lieu of military service. In so doing he met several artists and poets, and began the path to his own literary career.<ref name="happenstance">{{Cite web|url=http://www.happenstancepress.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=313:cliff-ashby&catid=29:poets&Itemid=63 |archive-url=https://archive.today/20130125043141/http://www.happenstancepress.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=313:cliff-ashby&catid=29:poets&Itemid=63 |url-status=dead |archive-date=2013-01-25 |title=Ashby, Cliff |format=obituary |year=2012 |publisher=HappenStance Press }}</ref>

As a poet he came to light through ''[[X (magazine)|X]]'' magazine.

His poetry collections include ''In the Vulgar Tongue'' (1968), ''The Dogs of Dewsbury'' (1976), ''Lies and Dreams'' (1980), ''Plain Song: Collected Poems'' (1985)<ref name="jrank"/> and ''A Few Late Flowers'' (2007).<ref name="happenstance"/> His novels are ''The Old Old Story'' and ''How and Why'' (both 1969).<ref name="jrank"/>

He died at home on 30 April 2012.<ref name="happenstance"/>

On Ashby's ''Few Late Flowers'' (2008) [[Robert Nye]] says: "He has just published what must be the most remarkable swansong offered by a writer in their 89th year...A sequence of quietly original poems, it is the bittersweet distillation of a lifetime's experience" <ref>[http://thescotsman.scotsman.com/features/Poetry-Beauty-in-a-rare.3877533.jp Robert Nye in The Scotsman]</ref>
