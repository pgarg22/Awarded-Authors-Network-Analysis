'''Eve Ensler''' ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|ɛ|n|z|l|ər}}; born May 25, 1953), also known [[Mononymous person|mononymously]] as '''V''', is an American playwright, performer, feminist, and activist. Ensler is best known for her play ''[[The Vagina Monologues]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2002/02/10/magazine/eve-ensler-wants-to-save-the-world.html|work=[[The New York Times]]|title=Eve Ensler wants to save the world|first=Susan|last=Dominus|date=February 10, 2002|access-date=March 24, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2001/12/09/nyregion/theater-melding-drama-with-politics.html|work=The New York Times|title=Melding drama with politics|first=Alvin|last=Klein|date=December 9, 2001|access-date=March 24, 2016}}</ref><ref name="NYT-2011-12-03">[https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2011/12/02/opinion/magazine-global-agenda-big-question.html "Politics, Power and Passion"], ''The New York Times'', December 2, 2011. Please see the fifth segment by Eve Ensler.</ref> In 2006 [[Charles Isherwood]] of ''[[The New York Times]]'' called ''The Vagina Monologues'' "probably the most important piece of political theater of the last decade."<ref>[https://www.nytimes.com/2006/09/03/theater/03ishe.html?pagewanted=all&_r=0 "The Culture Project and Plays that Make a Difference,"] ''The New York Times'', September 3, 2006.</ref>

In 2011, Ensler was awarded the [[Isabelle Stevenson Award]] at the [[65th Tony Awards]], which recognizes an individual from the theater community who has made a substantial contribution of volunteered time and effort on behalf of humanitarian, social service, or charitable organizations.  Ensler was given this award for her creation of the non-profit, V-Day movement which raises money and educates the public about violence against women and efforts to stop it.

Ensler says that from the ages of five to 10, she was sexually and physically abused by her father. Growing up, she has said she was "very sad, very angry, very defiant. I was the girl with the dirty hair. I didn't fit anywhere."<ref name=people/>

Ensler attended [[Middlebury College]] in [[Vermont]], where she became known as a militant feminist.<ref name=people/> After graduating in 1975, she had a string of abusive relationships and became dependent on drugs and alcohol.<ref name=people/> In 1978, she married Richard Dylan McDermott, a 34-year-old bartender, who convinced her to enter [[Drug rehabilitation|rehab]].<ref name=people/> When she was 23,{{when|date=March 2021}} she adopted [[Dylan McDermott|Mark Anthony McDermott]], her husband's 16-year-old son from his first marriage.<ref>McDermott, Shiva Rose. According to a 2012 article in the ''Sydney Morning Herald'', "After her marriage ended, she had a long relationship with the artist and psychotherapist Ariel Orr Jordan but is single now, which seems to suit her nomadic lifestyle – she has homes in New York and Paris but travels much of the year."<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.smh.com.au/entertainment/stage/interview--eve-ensler-20120112-1pvvm.html|title=Interview: Eve Ensler|first=Emine|last=Saner|work=Sydney Morning Herald|date=January 14, 2012|access-date=March 24, 2016}}</ref>

A June 2010 article by Ensler in ''[[The Guardian]]'' said that she was receiving treatment for [[uterine cancer]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2010/jun/12/cancer-atrocities-congo-violence|work= The Guardian|title=My cancer is arbitrary. == ''The Vagina Monologues'' ==
Ensler wrote ''[[The Vagina Monologues]]'' in 1996.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1996/10/23/theater/theater-in-review-940798.html|title=Theater in Review|last=Bruckner|first=D. J. R.|date=October 23, 1996|work=The New York Times|access-date=March 2, 2019|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> First performed in the basement of the Cornelia Street Café in [[Greenwich Village]], the play has been translated into 48 languages and performed in over 140 countries. Celebrities who have starred in it include [[Jane Fonda]], [[Whoopi Goldberg]], [[Idina Menzel]], [[Glenn Close]], [[Susan Sarandon]], [[Marin Mazzie]], [[Cyndi Lauper]], [[Mary Testa]], [[Sandra Oh]] and [[Oprah Winfrey]]. Ensler was awarded the [[Obie Award]] in 1996 for 'Best New Play' and in 1999 was awarded a [[Guggenheim Fellowship]] Award in Playwriting. She has also received the Berrilla-Kerr Award for Playwriting, the Elliot Norton Award for Outstanding Solo Performance, and the Jury Award for Theater at the [[U.S. Comedy Arts Festival]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1999/10/04/theater/theater-review-a-body-part-returns-as-the-leading-lady.html|work=The New York Times|title=A body part returns as the leading lady|first=Anita|last=Gates|date=October 4, 1999|access-date=March 24, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://variety.com/2018/legit/reviews/in-the-body-of-the-world-review-eve-ensler-1202689740/|title=Variety Off Broadway Review|last=Rizzo|first=Frank|date=February 6, 2018|work=Variety}}</ref>

== Subsequent work ==
Ensler's memoir ''In the Body of the World'' was released on April 30, 2013.  Booklist reviewed the book, saying, "This is a ravishing book of revelation and healing, lashing truths and deep emotion, courage and perseverance, compassion and generosity. Warm, funny, furious, and astute, as well as poetic, passionate, and heroic, Ensler harnesses all that she lost and learned to articulate a galvanizing vision of the essence of life: "The only salvation is kindness."".<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.booklistonline.com/ProductInfo.aspx?pid=5945197&AspxAutoDetectCookieSupport=1 |title=In the Body of the World, by Eve Ensler |publisher=Booklist Online |date=March 1, 2013 |access-date=May 16, 2013}}</ref> On February 6, 2018, she premiered a theatrical version of her memoir, which she performs as a solo monologue, directed by [[Diane Paulus]], at the [[Manhattan Theatre Club]] in New York City.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2018/02/06/theater/in-the-body-of-the-world-review-eve-ensler.html|title=Review: Eve Ensler Goes Deep 'In the Body of the World'|last=Green|first=Jesse|date=February 6, 2018|work=The New York Times|access-date=February 18, 2018|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>

Ensler has been involved in films, including V-Day's ''[[Until The Violence Stops]]'' and the [[PBS]] documentary ''What I Want My Words To Do To You'', and has appeared on television shows including interviews on CNN,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.cnn.com/video/#/video/international/2013/02/13/ctw-anderson-eve-ensler-vday.cnn |title=CNN Video – Breaking News Videos from |publisher=CNN.com |access-date=May 16, 2013}}</ref> Democracy Now,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.democracynow.org/2012/9/24/one_billion_rising_eve_ensler_activists |title=One Billion Rising: Eve Ensler, Activists Worldwide Plan Global Strike to End Violence Against Women |publisher=Democracy Now! |access-date=May 16, 2013}}</ref> TODAY,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.today.com/id/17535427/site/todayshow/ns/today-today_news/t/students-vagina-suspensions-unwarranted/#.UTqmOhmaylI |title=Students: 'Vagina' suspensions unwarranted – today > news |publisher=TODAY.com |access-date=May 16, 2013 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140606223236/http://www.today.com/id/17535427/site/todayshow/ns/today-today_news/t/students-vagina-suspensions-unwarranted/#.UTqmOhmaylI |archive-date=June 6, 2014 |url-status=dead  }}</ref> ''[[Real Time with Bill Maher]]'' (August 26, 2005) and ''[[Russell Simmons]] Presents Def Poetry'' (August 12, 2005).

She contributed the piece "Theater: A Sacred Home for Women" to the 2003 anthology ''[[Sisterhood Is Forever: The Women's Anthology for a New Millennium]]'', edited by [[Robin Morgan]].<ref name="illinois1">{{cite web|url=http://vufind.carli.illinois.edu/vf-dpu/Record/dpu_536804/TOC |title=Library Resource Finder: Table of Contents for: Sisterhood is forever : the women's anth |publisher=Vufind.carli.illinois.edu |access-date=October 15, 2015}}</ref>

From October 2005 to April 2006, Ensler toured twenty North American cities with her play ''The Good Body'', following engagements on Broadway, at [[American Conservatory Theater|ACT]] in San Francisco, and in a workshop production at [[Seattle Repertory Theatre]].{{citation needed|date=July 2020}} ''The Good Body'' addresses why women of many cultures and backgrounds perceive pressure to change the way they look in order to be accepted in the eyes of society.{{citation needed|date=July 2020}}

Ensler's play, ''The Treatment'' debuted on September 12, 2006, at the Culture Project in New York City.{{citation needed|date=July 2020}} This play explores the moral and psychological trauma that are the result of participation in military conflicts.{{citation needed|date=July 2020}}  It stars her adoptive son, [[Dylan McDermott]].{{citation needed|date=July 2020}}

In 2006, Ensler released her first major work written exclusively for the printed page. ''Insecure at Last: Losing It In Our Security-Obsessed World'' ([[Villard (imprint)|Villard]]; Hardcover; October 3, 2006).{{citation needed|date=July 2020}}  In ''Insecure at Last'', she explores how people live today, the measures people take to keep themselves safe, and how people can experience freedom by letting go of the deceptive notion of "protection."{{citation needed|date=July 2020}} In 2006 Ensler also co-edited ''A Memory, A Monologue, A Rant, and A Prayer'', an anthology of writings about [[violence against women]].{{citation needed|date=July 2020}}

Ensler's work ''I Am An Emotional Creature: The Secret Life of Girls Around The World'', a collection of original monologues about and for girls that aims to inspire girls to take agency over their minds, bodies, hearts and curiosities, was released February 2010 in book form by [[Villard (imprint)|Villard/Random House]] and made [[The New York Times Best Seller list|''The New York Times'' Best Seller list]].{{citation needed|date=July 2020}} The book was workshopped in July 2010 at New York Stage and Film and [[Vassar College]], moving toward an [[Off-Broadway]] production.{{citation needed|date=July 2020}} The theatrical production of the piece, titled ''Emotional Creature'', had its United States debut at the [[Berkeley Repertory Theater]] in [[Berkeley, CA]] in June 2012.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://barclayagency.com/site/speaker/eve-ensler|title=Eve Ensler biography|publisher=Steven Barclay Agency|year=2016|access-date=March 24, 2016}}</ref> In February 2012, The South African production of ''Emotional Creature'' was nominated for a 2011 [[Naledi Theatre Award]] for Best Ensemble Production/Cutting Edge Production.

Ensler was a consultant on feminism and women's issues for the 2015 action film ''[[Mad Max: Fury Road]]''.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://time.com/3858352/mad-max-the-stronger-sex/ |title=Mad Max and the Stronger Sex |date=May 14, 2015 |access-date=May 22, 2015 |website=Time |last=Dockterman |first=Eliana}}</ref>

In 2019, Ensler published the book ''The Apology'', where she imagines what her now dead father would say if he was able to apologise for the sexual and physical abuse he inflicted on her as a child. After completing the work, Ensler said that she had ceased to feel any bitterness towards her father, but that she no longer wished to carry his name, inviting folk to call her V.<ref name="auto"/><ref name="auto2"/><ref name="auto1"/>

== Activism ==
Ensler is an activist addressing issues of violence against women and girls. In 1998, her experience performing ''The Vagina Monologues'' inspired her to create [[V-Day (movement)|V-Day]], a global activist movement to stop violence against women and girls.  V-Day raises funds and awareness through annual benefit productions of ''The Vagina Monologues''.  In 2010, more than 5,400 V-Day events took place in over 1,500 locations in the U.S. and around the world.  As of 2014, the V-Day movement had raised over $100<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.glamour.com/inspired/blogs/the-conversation/2014/02/one-billion-rising-for-v-day|title=Here are one billion reasons to get up and dance on Valentine's Day|publisher=Glamour|first=Caitlin|last=Moscatello|date=February 13, 2014|access-date=March 24, 2016}}</ref> million and educated millions about the issue of violence against women and the efforts to end it, crafted international educational, media and [[Public service announcement|PSA]] campaigns, launched the Karama program in the Middle East, reopened shelters, and funded over 12,000 community-based anti-violence programs and safe houses in [[Democratic Republic of Congo]], [[Haiti]], [[Kenya]], [[South Dakota]], [[Egypt]] and [[Iraq]].  These safe houses provide women sanctuary from abuse, [[female genital mutilation]] and 'honor' killing.<ref>[http://www.radioproject.org/2009/03/women-rising-xix-masters-of-the-spoken-word/ "Women Rising XIX: Masters of the Spoken Word"] Making Contact, produced by National Radio Project. March 11, 2009.</ref> The 'V' in V-Day stands for [[Victory]], Valentine and [[Vagina]].

In February 2004, Ensler, alongside [[Sally Field]], Jane Fonda and [[Christine Lahti]], protested to have the Mexican government re-investigate the [[Female homicides in Ciudad Juárez|slayings of hundreds of women]] in [[Ciudad Juárez]], a city along the Texas border.{{citation needed|date=July 2020}}

Ensler is a supporter of the [[Revolutionary Association of the Women of Afghanistan]] (RAWA) and went to Afghanistan under the rule of the [[Taliban]]. She supports Afghan women and has organized many programs for them.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rawa.org/eve.htm|title=Eve Ensler: "Afghanistan is everywhere"|first=Janelle|last=Brown|publisher=RAWA|date=November 26, 2001|access-date=March 24, 2016}}</ref> She organized one event named the "Afghani Women's Summit For Democracy".{{citation needed|date=July 2020}}

Ensler has led a writing group since 1998 at the [[Bedford Hills Correctional Facility for Women]], which was portrayed in ''What I Want My Words To Do To You.''<ref>''[https://www.pbs.org/pov/whatiwant/ What I Want My Words To Do To You]'', [[Public Broadcasting Service|PBS]], premiered December 16, 2003</ref> [[Judith Alice Clark|Judy Clark]], [[Kathy Boudin]], and [[Pamela Smart]] were among the writing group's participants featured in the film.

In 2011, [[V-Day (movement)|V-Day]] and the Fondation Panzi (DRC), with support from [[UNICEF]], opened the City of Joy, a new community for women survivors of gender violence in Bukavu, Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC). City of Joy will provide up to 180 Congolese women a year with an opportunity to benefit from group therapy; self-defense training; comprehensive sexuality education (covering HIV/AIDS, family planning); economic empowerment; storytelling; dance; theater; ecology and horticulture. Created from their vision, Congolese women run, operate and direct City of Joy themselves.{{citation needed|date=July 2020}} The City of Joy celebrated its first graduating class in February 2012.{{citation needed|date=July 2020}} The story of the City of Joy, including V's involvement, is portrayed in the documentary [[City of Joy (2016 film)|City of Joy]], screening on Netflix.

In 2012, along with the V-Day movement, Ensler created [[One Billion Rising]], a global protest campaign to end violence, and promote justice and gender equality for women.  On February 14, 2013, V-Day's 15th anniversary, women and men in countries around the world held dance actions to demand an end to violence against women and girls.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://newsfeed.time.com/2013/02/14/one-billion-rising-an-end-to-violence-against-women/ |title=One Billion Rising: An End to Violence Against Women &#124; TIME.com |publisher=Newsfeed.time.com |date=February 14, 2013 |access-date=May 16, 2013}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |last=Malhotra |first=Aditi |url=https://blogs.wsj.com/indiarealtime/2013/02/14/a-flash-mob-dance-for-womens-rights/ |title=In Delhi, "One Billion Rising" Organize a Flash Mob of Dancers for Women's Rights – India – India Real Time – WSJ |publisher=Blogs.wsj.com |date=February 14, 2013 |access-date=May 16, 2013}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |last=Cohen |first=Sandy |url=http://bigstory.ap.org/article/enslers-billion-rising-movement-spans-globe |title=Ensler's Billion Rising movement spans the globe |publisher=Bigstory.ap.org |access-date=May 16, 2013}}</ref>

In 2016, Ensler co-signed a letter to [[Ban Ki-Moon]] calling for a more humane drug policy, along with [[Warren Buffett]], [[John Legend]] and [[Elizabeth Warren]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://drugpolicy.org/ungass2016|title=A Public Letter to UN Secretary General Ban Ki-moon|website=Drug Policy Alliance}}</ref>

In 2017 in an opinion piece in ''[[The Guardian]]'' Ensler voiced harsh criticism of the newly inaugurated president of the United States, [[Donald Trump]], referring to him as a "self-confessed sexual assaulter" and "our predator-in-chief".<ref>{{cite web|url= https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2017/jan/21/womens-march-donald-trump-inauguration |title= The Women's March heralds a renaissance of resistance |website= [[The Guardian]] |first= Eve |last= Ensler |date= January 21, 2017 |access-date= January 21, 2017}}</ref>

In 2020, Ensler endorsed Senator [[Bernie Sanders]] for [[President of the United States]] in the [[2020 United States presidential election|2020 election]].<ref>{{cite web |title=2020 presidential candidate Bernie Sanders holds rally in San Jose ahead of California primary |url=https://abc7news.com/5977365/ |website=abc7news.com |publisher=ABC7 News |access-date=March 1, 2020 |date=March 1, 2020}}</ref>
