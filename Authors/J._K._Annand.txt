'''James King Annand''' [[Order of the British Empire|MBE]] (2 February 1908 &ndash; 8 June 1993) was a [[Scotland|Scottish]] [[poet]] best known for his children's poems.

== Biography ==
Born at [[Edinburgh]] to Maggie Gold and her husband, plumber William Annand, He was educated at [[Broughton, Edinburgh|Broughton]] Secondary School, and he graduated from the [[University of Edinburgh]] in 1930. He later taught at schools in Edinburgh and [[Whithorn]]. Annand also translated poetry and fiction from [[German language|German]] and medieval [[Latin]] into [[Scots language|Scots]]. He was the founding editor of ''[[Lallans]]'', a magazine for writing in [[Scots language|Scots]] published by the Scots Language Society, from 1973 to 1983.

He died in Edinburgh in 1993.<ref>{{cite news|title=Obituary: J. K. Annand|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/obituary-j-k-annand-1491595.html|accessdate=4 April 2012|newspaper=Independent|date=14 June 1993}}</ref>

==Collections of children's poetry==
* ''Sing it Aince for Pleisure'' (1970)
* ''Twice for Joy'' (1973)
* ''Thrice to Show Ye'' (1979)
* ''A Wale o Rhymes'' (1989); reissued in 1998 as ''Bairn Rhymes''

==Other works==
* ''Two Voices'' (1968)
* ''Poems and translations'' (1975)
* ''Songs from Carmina burana'' (1978)
* ''A Scots handsel'' (1980)
* ''Selected Poems, 1925-1990'' (1992)

==Recognition==
* 1958 - chairman of the Edinburgh Branch of the [[Saltire Society]].
* 1979 - [[Scottish Arts Council]] special award for his contribution to Scottish Poetry.
* 1993 - Posthumous [[Member of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire|MBE]] for his services to [[Scots Language]] and Literature.
* 2008 - Commemorative stone in the [[Makars' Court]]
* 2015 - Posthumous award of the Arctic Star for his war service in the Royal Navy during WW2
