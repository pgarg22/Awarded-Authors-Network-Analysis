'''Mariska Ady''' (26 February 1888 in Hadad/[[Hodod]] – 4 February 1977 in [[Budapest]]) was a Hungarian writer and poet,<ref name="oszk">{{cite web|url=http://mek.oszk.hu/00300/00355/html/ABC00003/00103.htm|website=mek.oszk.hu|title=Magyar Életrajzi Lexikon 1000-1990 (Hungarian Biographical Lexicon) |accessdate=2017-11-03}}</ref><ref name = "National Széchényi Library">{{cite web|url=http://www.oszk.hu/en/exhibitions/most-beautiful-anthem-life-ady-exhibition|website=oszk.hu|title=Országos Széchényi Könyvtár (National Széchényi Library) |accessdate=2017-11-03}}</ref><ref>Új Magyar Dictionary Irodalmi (New Dictionary of the Hungarian Literature), Vol. I, pp. 16–17 (Akadémiai Kiadó, Budapest 1994). {{ISBN|963-05-6805-5}}</ref> who published several volumes. She was a cousin of the poet [[Endre Ady]].

==Work==
By then a widow, Mariska Ady wrote poems during the First World War, on the horrors of the war. Her poems first appeared in local magazines in [[Szilágy County]] and later in [[Transylvanian]] and national periodicals and newspapers.<ref>E. g. ''Csíki Lapok'', ''Ellenzék'', ''Magyar Nép'', ''Pásztortűz'', ''Székely Nép'', ''Szilágy'', ''Vasárnapi Újság'', ''Zord Idő''.</ref> She followed the lead of her cousin, Endre Ady, in some of the aspects of her lyric poetry. She was the only other member of the Ady family to take up literature.<ref name="Rom. Magyar" >Romániai Magyar Irodalmi Lexikon (Romanian/Hungarian Literary Dictionary) [http://mek.oszk.hu/03600/03628/html/a.htm#AdyMariskaL.Ady Retrieved 28 April 2018.]</ref>

==Family==
Ady was the daughter of Sándor Ady, a notary public in Hadad (today [[Hodod]]). She qualified as an elementary-school teacher in [[Budapest]]. In the [[First World War]] she lost her husband, Lajos Landt, which left her with three children to bring up alone. Her teaching career ended early due to illness.<ref name="Rom. Magyar" />
