'''Kevin Young'''  (born November 8, 1970)<ref>{{cite web|url=https://aalbc.com/authors/author.php?author_name=Kevin+Young|title=Kevin Young|website=[[AALBC.com]]|access-date=29 July 2021}}</ref><ref name=":7" /> is an American [[poet]] and teacher of poetry and the director of the Smithsonian Institution [[National Museum of African American History and Culture]]. Author of 11 books and editor of eight others,<ref name=":5">{{Cite news|url=http://lj.libraryjournal.com/2017/03/interviews/kevin-young-director-of-nypls-schomburg-center-new-yorker-poetry-editor/#_|title=Kevin Young: Director of NYPL's Schomburg Center, ''New Yorker'' Poetry Editor|last=Peet|first=Lisa|date=March 21, 2017|work=Library Journal|access-date=2017-06-13|language=en-US|archive-date=3 March 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180303225127/https://lj.libraryjournal.com/2017/03/interviews/kevin-young-director-of-nypls-schomburg-center-new-yorker-poetry-editor/#_|url-status=live}}</ref> Young has been a winner of a [[Guggenheim Fellowship]] as well as a finalist for the [[National Book Award]] for his 2003 collection ''[[Jelly Roll (poetry collection)|Jelly Roll: A Blues]]''. Young has served as Atticus Haygood Professor of English and Creative Writing at [[Emory University]] and curator of Emory's [[Raymond Danowski Poetry Library]], as well as Director of the [[Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture]] at the [[New York Public Library]]. In March 2017, Young became poetry editor of ''[[The New Yorker]]''.

==Early life==
Born in [[Lincoln, Nebraska]], Young was the only child of two working parents, his father, Dr. Paul E. Young, being an [[ophthalmologist]] and his mother, Dr. Azzie Young, a [[chemist]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|url=http://www.ajc.com/lifestyles/kevin-young-named-new-poetry-editor-the-new-yorker/URNsAgGWcgJG0k9JtD9gUM/|title=Kevin Young is named new poetry editor at The New Yorker|last=Oliviero|first=Helena|date=March 15, 2017|work=Atlanta Journal-Constitution|access-date=June 8, 2017|archive-date=10 February 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180210002359/http://www.ajc.com/lifestyles/kevin-young-named-new-poetry-editor-the-new-yorker/URNsAgGWcgJG0k9JtD9gUM/|url-status=live}}</ref><ref name=":6">{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2005/05/08/fashion/weddings/kate-tuttle-and-kevin-young.html|title=Kate Tuttle and Kevin Young|date=2005-05-08|work=The New York Times|access-date=2017-06-13|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331|archive-date=21 December 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151221154236/http://www.nytimes.com/2005/05/08/fashion/weddings/kate-tuttle-and-kevin-young.html|url-status=live}}</ref> Due to the careers of both of his parents, his family moved frequently throughout his youth. Young lived in six different places before he reached the age of ten,<ref name=":0" /> but his family ultimately settled in [[Topeka, Kansas]]. He first began to pursue writing when he was thirteen years old, after he attended a summer writing class at [[Washburn University]].<ref>{{cite book|last=Gioia|first=Dana|title=Twentieth-Century American Poetry|year=2004|publisher=McGraw-Hill|location=Boston|isbn=0-07-240019-6|pages=[https://archive.org/details/twentiethcentury0000unse_l1u7/page/1041 1041–1042]|url=https://archive.org/details/twentiethcentury0000unse_l1u7/page/1041}}</ref>

Young attended [[Harvard College]], where he studied with [[Seamus Heaney]] and [[Lucie Brock-Broido]]<ref name=":0" /> and became friends with writer [[Colson Whitehead]].<ref name=":1">{{Cite news|url=http://www.westernadvocate.com.au/story/4656062/colson-whitehead-the-truth-of-things-not-the-facts/?cs=36|title=Colson Whitehead: 'The truth of things, not the facts'|last=Purcell|first=Andrew|date=2017-05-20|work=Western Advocate|access-date=2017-06-12|language=en|archive-date=8 August 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170808002155/http://www.westernadvocate.com.au/story/4656062/colson-whitehead-the-truth-of-things-not-the-facts/?cs=36|url-status=live}}</ref> He graduated in 1992, then held a [[Stegner Fellowship]] at [[Stanford University]] (1992–94), where he worked with [[Denise Levertov]], and received his [[Master of Fine Arts]] from [[Brown University]], where [[Michael S. Harper]] served as a significant influence.<ref name=":4">{{Cite journal|last=Lee|first=Don|date=Winter 1996–1997|title=Kevin Young, Zacharis Award|url=https://www.pshares.org/issues/winter-1996-97/kevin-young-zacharis-award|journal=Ploughshares|issue=71|access-date=13 June 2017|archive-date=28 October 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171028042736/https://www.pshares.org/issues/winter-1996-97/kevin-young-zacharis-award|url-status=live}}</ref>

==Career==
While in Boston and Providence, he was part of the African-American poetry group the [[Dark Room Collective]].<ref name=":0" /> He is heavily influenced by the poets [[Langston Hughes]], [[John Berryman]], and [[Emily Dickinson]] and by the artist [[Jean-Michel Basquiat]].

Young wrote much of his debut collection, ''Most Way Home,'' while still an undergraduate.<ref name=":3">{{Cite journal|last=Arnold|first=Rob|date=Spring 2006|title=About Kevin Young|url=https://www.pshares.org/issues/spring-2006/about-kevin-young|journal=Ploughshares|issue=99|access-date=13 June 2017|archive-date=29 October 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171029120024/https://www.pshares.org/issues/spring-2006/about-kevin-young|url-status=live}}</ref> Published by [[William Morrow (publisher)|William Morrow]] in 1995,<ref name=":1" /> ''Most Way Home'' was selected by [[Lucille Clifton]] for the National Poetry Series and won ''[[Ploughshares]]''{{'}} John C. Zacharis First Book Award.<ref name=":4" /> Writing in ''Ploughshares'', Rob Arnold observes that in that first book Young "explores his own family's narratives, showing an uncanny awareness of voice and persona."<ref name=":3" />

Young has described his next three books – ''To Repel Ghosts'' (named for a [[Jean-Michel Basquiat]] painting)'', [[Jelly Roll (poetry collection)|Jelly Roll]]'' (a collection of love poems named for [[Jelly Roll Morton]])'','' and ''Black Maria'' – as an "American trilogy", calling the series ''Devil's Music.''<ref name=":3" />

Young's collection ''The Book of Hours'' (Knopf, 2014)<ref name=":2">{{Cite news|url=https://www.arktimes.com/arkansas/a-qanda-with-kevin-young/Content?oid=4370434|title=A Q&A with Kevin Young|last=Matthews|first=James|work=Arkansas Times|access-date=June 12, 2017|archive-date=23 April 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160423150151/http://www.arktimes.com/arkansas/a-qanda-with-kevin-young/Content?oid=4370434|url-status=live}}</ref> won the 2015 [[Lenore Marshall Poetry Prize]].

Young is also the author of ''For The Confederate Dead, Dear Darkness'', ''Blues Laws: Selected and Uncollected Poems 1995–2015'' (2016)<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/authors/profiles/article/72187-pw-s-top-authors-pick-their-favorite-books-of-2016.html|title=PW's Top Authors Pick Their Favorite Books of 2016|work=Publishers Weekly|access-date=2017-06-12|language=en|archive-date=11 March 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170311004222/http://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/authors/profiles/article/72187-pw-s-top-authors-pick-their-favorite-books-of-2016.html|url-status=live}}</ref> and editor of ''Giant Steps: The New Generation of African American Writers'' (2000)'', Blues Poems'' (2003)'', Jazz Poems'' (2006), and ''John Berryman's Selected Poems'' (2004).<ref name=":3" />

His poem "Black Cat Blues," originally published in ''[[The Virginia Quarterly Review]]'', was included in ''[[The Best American Poetry 2005]]''. Young's poetry has also appeared in ''[[The New Yorker]]'', ''[[Poetry Magazine]]'', ''[[The Paris Review]]'', ''[[Ploughshares]]'', and other literary magazines. In 2007, he served as guest editor for an issue of ''[[Ploughshares]]''.<ref name=":3" /> He has written on art and artists for museums in Los Angeles and Minneapolis.

His 2003 book of poems ''Jelly Roll'' was a finalist for the [[National Book Award]]. Young was named a [[Guggenheim Fellowship|Guggenheim Foundation Fellow]] in 2003, as well as an [[National Endowment for the Arts|NEA]] Literature Fellow in Poetry.<ref>"University Honors & Awards: [https://honorsandawards.iu.edu/search-awards/honoree.shtml?honoreeID=3894 Honoree - Kevin Young"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141006094103/https://honorsandawards.iu.edu/search-awards/honoree.shtml?honoreeID=3894 |date=6 October 2014 }}, Indiana University.</ref>

After stints at the [[University of Georgia]] and [[Indiana University]], Young now teaches writing at [[Emory University]], where he is the Atticus Haygood Professor of English and Creative Writing, as well as the curator of the Raymond Danowski Poetry Library, a large collection of first and rare editions of poetry in English.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/kevin-young|title=Kevin Young|last=Poetry Foundation|date=25 May 2019|website=Poetry Foundation|access-date=26 May 2019|archive-date=26 May 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190526081646/https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/kevin-young|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.npr.org/2014/03/04/285712680/kevin-young-on-blues-poetry-and-laughing-to-keep-from-crying|title=Kevin Young On Blues, Poetry And 'Laughing To Keep From Crying'|work=Fresh Air|access-date=June 12, 2017|publisher=NPR|archive-date=8 December 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171208110429/https://www.npr.org/2014/03/04/285712680/kevin-young-on-blues-poetry-and-laughing-to-keep-from-crying|url-status=live}}</ref>

In September 2016,<ref name=":5" /> Young became the Director of the [[Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture]] at the [[New York Public Library]].<ref>{{cite news|last= Kelly|first= William P.|date= August 1, 2016|title= Introducing the New Director of the Schomburg Center, Kevin Young|url= https://www.nypl.org/blog/2016/08/01/schomburg-director-kevin-young|newspaper= NYPL blog|access-date= August 1, 2016|archive-date= 7 August 2016|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20160807022941/https://www.nypl.org/blog/2016/08/01/schomburg-director-kevin-young|url-status= live}}</ref>

In March 2017, he was named poetry editor of ''[[The New Yorker]]'',<ref name=":0" /> to begin in November 2017.<ref name=":5" />

Young is working on two books: a non-fiction book called ''[[Bunk (book)|Bunk]]'' on the U.S. history of lies and hoaxes, and a poetry collection that he has described as being "about African American history and also personal history, growing up in Kansas, which has a long black history including Langston Hughes and others."<ref name=":5" />

In September 2020, he was named director of the [[National Museum of African-American History and Culture]], to begin in January 2021.<ref name=":7">{{cite news |last1=Bowley |first1=Graham |title=Kevin Young, Poet and Author, Is Named to Lead African American Museum |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2020/09/30/arts/design/kevin-young-to-lead-african-american-museum.html |access-date=1 October 2020 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=30 September 2020 |archive-date=30 September 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200930233205/https://www.nytimes.com/2020/09/30/arts/design/kevin-young-to-lead-african-american-museum.html |url-status=live }}</ref>

==Personal life==
Young lives in New York.<ref name=":5" /> He married Kate Tuttle, book columnist at ''[[The Boston Globe]]''<ref>{{cite news|last1=Redmon|first1=Jeremy|title=Broad range of music fuels Decatur poet Kevin Young's new collection|url=http://www.myajc.com/entertainment/books--literature/broad-range-music-fuels-decatur-poet-kevin-young-new-collection/vYyfdDui20NB2qtXYjngPK/|access-date=8 June 2017|work=Atlanta Journal-Constitution|date=February 13, 2016|language=en|archive-date=29 March 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170329233446/http://www.myajc.com/entertainment/books--literature/broad-range-music-fuels-decatur-poet-kevin-young-new-collection/vYyfdDui20NB2qtXYjngPK/|url-status=live}}</ref> in 2005.<ref name=":6" />
