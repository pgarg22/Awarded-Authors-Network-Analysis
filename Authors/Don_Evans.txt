'''Donald Thomas Evans''' (April 27, 1938 &ndash; October 16, 2003) was an American [[playwright]], [[theater director]], actor and [[educator]].

==Early life and education==
Evans was born April 27, 1938, in [[Philadelphia]], Pennsylvania,<ref name=filmref>{{cite web| url = http://www.filmreference.com/film/19/Don-Evans.html | title=Don Evans Biography (1938-)| publisher=FilmReference.com|access-date= March 25, 2016}}</ref> the son of Mary Evans.<ref name=nytimesobit /> After serving in the [[United States Marine Corps]], he graduated from [[Cheyney University of Pennsylvania|Cheyney State College]] in 1962 and went on to [[Temple University]], earning a [[master's degree]] in 1968 and a [[Master of Fine Arts]] in 1972.<ref name=nytimesobit />

==Educational career==
In 1972, Evans became an associate professor at [[Trenton State College]] (later named The College of New Jersey), where he chaired the Afro-American Studies Department.<ref name=nytimesobit /> He also was an adjunct professor at [[Princeton University]] and a visiting professor of theater arts at [[Rutgers University]].<ref name=nytimesobit /> During this time, Evans wrote essays and articles for ''Black World'', ''Essence'', ''Players, and Pride''.<ref>{{Cite book|last=Kaye|first=Phyllis Johnson|url=http://archive.org/details/nationalplaywrig00kaye|title=National playwrights directory|date=1981|publisher=Waterford, Conn. : Eugene O'Neill Theater Center|others=Internet Archive|isbn=978-0-9605160-0-1|pages=98}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Evans|first=Don|date=April 1974|title=The Theater of Confrontation: Ed Bullins, Up Against the Wall|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=qDkDAAAAMBAJ&printsec=frontcover#v=onepage&q&f=false|journal=Black World|publisher=Johnson Publication|pages=14}}</ref>

He worked from 1978 to 1998 with his friend and fellow playwright [[August Wilson]] in forming a National [[Black Theatre Summit]] at [[Dartmouth College]], from which was formed the [[African Grove Institute|African Grove Institute for the Arts]].<ref>{{Cite book|url=http://archive.org/details/africanamericanp0000unse_w4z8|title=African-American performance and theater history : a critical reader|date=2001|publisher=Oxford [England] ; New York : Oxford University Press|others=Internet Archive|isbn=978-0-19-512724-9|pages=16}}</ref>

==Theatre==
Evans studied acting, directing, and playwriting at the [[HB Studio|Hagen-Berghof Studios]] in New York City from 1969 to 1970,{{citation needed|date=March 2016}} during which time he also taught English and Drama at [[Princeton High School (New Jersey)|Princeton High School]] in [[Princeton, New Jersey]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Evans|first=Don|date=1968|title=Educational Theater in the High School|url=https://www.jstor.org/stable/812240|journal=The English Journal|volume=57|issue=3|pages=387–390|doi=10.2307/812240|issn=0013-8274}}</ref> Part of the [[Black Arts Movement]] of the 1970s, Evans had his first plays, the [[One-act play|one-acts]] ''Orrin'' and ''Sugarmouth Sam Don’t Dance No More'' performed in 1972 at the [[Crossroads Theatre]], a professional playhouse in [[New Brunswick, New Jersey]].{{citation needed|date=March 2016}} The following year, this became his Manhattan debut production, at the [[Theater de Lys]].<ref name=nytimesobit />

In 1976, Evans wrote ''It’s Showdown Time,'' a raucous adaptation of [[William Shakespeare]]'s ''[[The Taming of the Shrew]]''.<ref name=nytimesobit>{{cite news| first=Wolfgang|last= Saxon| url = https://www.nytimes.com/2003/10/24/arts/theater/24EVAN.html?ex=1068008383&ei=1&en=3113033d012f7ca8 |title=Don Evans, 65, a Playwright Who Focused on Black Lives, Dies | work=[[The New York Times]]| date=October 24, 2003}}</ref> In 1978, Evans wrote ''Mahalia,'' his first musical, a portrait of [[Gospel music|gospel]] vocalist [[Mahalia Jackson]].<ref>{{Cite book|last=Hill|first=Anthony D.|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=hlqYYWiNOEwC&newbks=0&printsec=frontcover&pg=PA175&dq=evans+don+players+theatre&hl=en|title=Historical Dictionary of African American Theater|last2=Barnett|first2=Douglas Q.|date=2008-12-04|publisher=Scarecrow Press|isbn=978-0-8108-6276-0|pages=175|language=en}}</ref> ''Louis,'' Evans' musical portrayal of jazz legend [[Louis Armstrong]], was written in 1981. Other works include ''Blues for a Gospel Queen'', ''The Trials and Tribulations of Staggerlee Booker T. Brown''. ''[[One Monkey Don't Stop No Show (play)|One Monkey Don't Stop No Show]]'' a tragi-comic look at a middle-class black family, and ''A Lovesong for Miss Lydia'',<ref>{{cite web| url = http://www.doollee.com/PlaywrightsE/evans-don.html#98360 |title=Don Evans|publisher= Dollee.com| first=Julian, ed.|last= Oddy|access-date=March 25, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.southsidepride.com/0111/urban.html |date=November 2001 |title=Women on the verge…again |first1=Marty |last1=Roth |first2=Martha |last2=Rotharchive |work=Southside Pride |location=South Minneapolis, Minnesota |archive-date=February 19, 2012 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120219233253/http://www.southsidepride.com/0111/urban.html }}</ref> described by ''[[The New York Times]]'' as a "Pinteresque variation on the Big Bad Wolf story."<ref>{{Cite news|last=Catinella|first=Joseph|date=1982-04-04|title=All New Brunswick is a Stage|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1982/04/04/nyregion/all-new-brunswick-is-a-stage.html|access-date=2020-11-14|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> Evans wrote his final play, ''When Miss Mollie Hit the Triple Bars'', in 1999. It was based on the life of his mother, Mary.{{citation needed|date=March 2016}}

Over the course of his career, Evans received playwriting fellowships from the [[National Endowment for the Arts]], the [[New Jersey Council of the Arts]], and the [[New Jersey Historical Society]].{{citation needed|date=March 2016}} Eighteen of his plays have been produced, both in the US and in countries including Germany, England and Hong Kong.{{citation needed|date=March 2016}} He also served, from 1983 to 1988, as artistic director for the [[Karamu House]]{{citation needed|date=March 2016}} in [[Cleveland]], Ohio.

Don Evans was named an AMPARTS Fellow for the [[United States Information Agency]] to India in 1984.{{citation needed|date=March 2016}}

==Personal life==
Evans was divorced from Frances Gooding Chapman. He had by two sons, Todd and Orrin, and a daughter, Rachel Marianno. He died at the age of 65 of a [[heart attack]] on October 16, 2003, at his home in [[Merchantville, New Jersey]].<ref name=nytimesobit />
