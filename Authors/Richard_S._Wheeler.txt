'''Richard Shaw Wheeler''' <!--(born 1935)--> was an [[United States of America|American]] author and former newspaper editor.<ref name=Wheeler>{{cite web|url=http://www.richardswheeler.com/walkerbio.htm|title=Richard S. Wheeler biography|publisher=Authors official website|year=2013|accessdate=1 February 2013}}</ref> He is best known for his novels set in the [[American Old West|American West]], including the "Barnaby Skye" series. Wheeler was the 2001 recipient of the [[Owen Wister Award]] for lifetime contributions to [[Western fiction|Western literature]],<ref name=Amazon>{{cite web|url=https://www.amazon.com/Richard-S.-Wheeler/e/B000AQ1HDI|title=Author bio|year=2013|accessdate=1 February 2013}}</ref> and is a six-time [[Western Writers of America]] [[Spur Award]] winner.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://westernwriters.org/spur-awards/|title=Spur Award winners|publisher=Western Writers of America website|year=2013|accessdate=1 February 2013}}</ref>

==Early life==
Born in [[Milwaukee, Wisconsin]], Richard Wheeler was raised in the suburb of [[Wauwatosa, Wisconsin|Wauwatosa]] in a family descended from New England Puritans.<ref name=Wheeler/><ref name=West/> Following graduation from Wauwatosa High School in 1953,<ref name=School>{{cite web|url=http://www.wauwatosa.k12.wi.us/cms_files/resources/Wheeler%20Bio.pdf|title=Richard S. Wheeler Class of 1953|publisher=Wauwatosa School District|date=11 April 2005|accessdate=1 February 2013}}</ref> Wheeler moved to [[California]] in the mid-1950s for three years. At first intending to be a playwright, he studied at the [[Pasadena Playhouse]],<ref name=West>{{cite web|url=http://writersofthewest.blogspot.com/2011/01/visit-with-richard-s-wheeler.html|title=Interview with Richard Wheeler|publisher=Writers of the West|date=28 January 2012|accessdate=1 February 2013}}</ref> later taking acting lessons and trying his hand at being a screenwriter. While in California he supported himself by working in a Hollywood record store and as a freelance photographer.<ref name=Wheeler/><ref name=West/> Meeting with little success, he returned to his native Wisconsin and attended the [[University of Wisconsin&ndash;Madison]].<ref name=Wheeler/>

==Professional career==
Wheeler returned to the west after attending the University of Wisconsin, working at a succession of newspapers including the ''[[Nevada Appeal]]'', ''[[Phoenix Gazette]]'', ''[[Oakland Tribune]]'', and ''[[Billings Gazette]]''.<ref name=Wheeler/> In 1972 he switched careers and became a book editor for a number of publishers, most notably [[Walker & Company]]. Inspired by both the westerns he was editing and the frequent layoffs in the industry which left him with free time,<ref name=West/> Wheeler penned his first novel, ''Bushwhack'', published by [[Doubleday Publishing|Doubleday]] in 1978.<ref name=Wheeler/> He wrote five more novels in the 1970s and 1980s while still working as a book editor, before turning his attention to writing full-time in 1987. Two years later he won the first of five Spur Awards from the Western Writers of America with his 1989 book, ''Fool's Coach''. 

==Personal life==
Wheeler was married to Sue Hart, a professor at [[Montana State University Billings]], who died in the summer of 2014. The couple divided their time between homes in [[Livingston, Montana]], on the northern edge of [[Yellowstone National Park]], and [[Billings, Montana]].<ref name=Mountains>{{cite web|url=http://www.richardswheeler.com/talk.htm#pic|title=Montana home|publisher=Richard S. Wheeler official website|year=2013|accessdate=11 April 2013}}</ref>
