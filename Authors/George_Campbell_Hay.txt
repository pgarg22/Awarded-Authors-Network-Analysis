'''George Campbell Hay''' (1915&ndash;1984) was a [[Scotland|Scottish]] poet and translator, who wrote in [[Scottish Gaelic language|Scottish Gaelic]], [[Scots language|Scots]] and English. He used the [[patronymic]] '''Deòrsa Mac Iain Dheòrsa'''. He also wrote poetry in [[French language|French]], [[Italian language|Italian]] and [[Norwegian language|Norwegian]], and translated poetry from many languages into Gaelic.

==Life==
He was born in [[Elderslie]], [[Renfrewshire]], and brought up in [[Tarbert, Kintyre]] and [[Argyll]], where his father, the novelist [[John MacDougall Hay]], had been born.<ref name="DT04">{{cite journal |last1=Thomson |first1=Derick |title=Hay, George Campbell [Mac Iain Deòrsa] |journal=Oxford Dictionary of National Biography |date=2004 |doi=10.1093/ref:odnb/40321}}</ref> He was educated at [[Fettes College]] (which he despised) and the [[University of Oxford]]. He served in the British Army in North Africa, Italy and Macedonia during [[World War II]], a region which featured in much of his work and then lived for a long period in Edinburgh where he worked in the [[National Library of Scotland]].<ref name="DT04"/>

He was a [[Scottish nationalist]].<ref name="DT04"/> His life was difficult, with long periods of hard living, including hospitalisation and homelessness.<ref name="JC03">{{cite news |last1=Campbell |first1=James |title=The Eighth Man|work=The Guardian |url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2003/mar/01/featuresreviews.guardianreview13 |access-date=17 March 2021 |date=1 March 2003}}</ref>

==Work==
He was a multilingual poet and published three collections between 1947 and 1952: ''Fuaran slèibh'' (1947), ''Wind on Loch Fyne'' (1948) and ''O na ceithir àirdean'' (1952). <ref name="JC03"/><ref name="DT04"/> Some of his poetry was set to music by [[Francis George Scott]].<ref name="KW58">{{cite book |last1=Wittig |first1=Kurt |title=The Scottish Tradition in Literature |year=1978| orig-year=1958 |publisher=The Mercat Press|pages=305-7}}</ref>

He was a frequent contributor to ''[[Gairm]]'' magazine, and other Gaelic periodicals. The critic Kurt Wittig suggested Gaelic traits were more evident in his English than his Scots poetry.<ref name="KW58"/> ''Mochtàr is Dùghall'', an unfinished epic about a Highland soldier, and a North African Arab in World War II was published in 1982.<ref name="DT04"/>

His ''Collected Poems and Songs'' appeared in 2000, edited by Michel Byrne, and has attracted new attention to his work.<ref name="JC03"/>
