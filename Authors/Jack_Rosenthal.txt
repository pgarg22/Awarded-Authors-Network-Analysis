'''Jack Morris Rosenthal''' {{post-nominals|country=GBR|CBE}}  (8 September 1931 – 29 May 2004) was an English playwright who wrote 129 early episodes of the [[ITV (TV network)|ITV]] soap opera ''[[Coronation Street]]'' and over 150 screenplays, including original TV plays, feature films, and adaptations.  
A street in [[Manchester]] is named after him, appropriately next to a centre of contemporary art, theatre and film that opened in 2015, [[HOME (Manchester)|HOME]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://homemcr.org/event/jack-rosenthal-jack-rosenthal-street/|title=Jack Rosenthal on Jack Rosenthal Street|website=HOME}}</ref>

==Biography==
Rosenthal was born in [[Cheetham Hill]], [[Manchester]], into a Jewish family. After studying English Literature at [[University of Sheffield|Sheffield University]], he carried out his [[National Service]] in the [[Royal Navy]]. He worked briefly in advertising before joining [[Granada Television]]. He earned his first television credit with Granada in 1961, assigned as a writer of episode 31 of Britain's longest-running soap opera, ''[[Coronation Street]]''. He became a regular writer for the series<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/entertainment-arts-45056391|title=Maureen Lipman joining Coronation Street|date=3 August 2018|via=www.bbc.com}}</ref> and began writing for other series as well. During the 1960s, he contributed material for various television comedy shows including the satirical ''[[That Was The Week That Was]]''.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.scotsman.com/news/obituaries/jack-rosenthal-author-and-playwright-2469229|title=Jack Rosenthal, author and playwright|website=www.scotsman.com}}</ref> At [[Granada Television]], he wrote a spin-off series from ''Coronation Street'' for the character [[Leonard Swindley]], played by [[Arthur Lowe]], called ''[[Pardon the Expression]]'' and created two comedy series ''[[The Dustbinmen]]'' and ''[[The Lovers (TV series)|The Lovers]]'', the latter starring [[Richard Beckinsale]] and [[Paula Wilcox]]. In 1976 he also wrote a TV drama for ITV, called ''[[Ready When You Are, Mr McGill]]'', which was later remade in 2003.

Rosenthal won three [[BAFTA]] awards for ''[[Bar Mitzvah Boy]]'' (about a Jewish boy's [[Bar Mitzvah]]), ''[[The Evacuees]]'' (based on his own war-time [[Evacuations of civilians in Britain during World War II|evacuation]]) and ''[[Spend, Spend, Spend (play)|Spend, Spend, Spend]]'' (about the [[football pools]] winner, [[Viv Nicholson]], directed by [[John Goldschmidt]]). He also wrote ''[[The Knowledge (film)|The Knowledge]]'', a film about London taxi-drivers which has become a classic for cabbies-in-training. He wrote the 1986 television film ''[[List of London's Burning episodes#The TV Movie (1986)|London's Burning]]'' for [[London Weekend Television]], which proved so successful that it was adapted into [[London's Burning (TV series)|a television series of the same name]], which ran from 1988 until 2002.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.manchestereveningnews.co.uk/news/greater-manchester-news/writer-jack-rosenthal-dies-72-1110269|title=Writer Jack Rosenthal dies, 72|first=Manchester Evening|last=News|date=15 February 2007|website=Manchester Evening News}}</ref> Rosenthal adapted the novel ''The Devil's Lieutenant'' for director John Goldschmidt as a mini-series for Channel 4 and ZDF, and wrote the screenplay of ''Captain Jack''  (based on a true story) for producer John Goldschmidt.

In 1983, Rosenthal co-wrote the film ''[[Yentl (film)|Yentl]]'' with [[Barbra Streisand]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0086619/fullcredits|title=Yentl (1983) - IMDb|via=www.imdb.com}}</ref> He also did uncredited work on the screenplay of ''[[Chicken Run]]''.

Rosenthal also wrote the book for the musical version of ''[[Bar Mitzvah Boy (musical)|Bar Mitzvah Boy]]'', with music by [[Jule Styne]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.screenonline.org.uk/tv/id/1362271/index.html|title=BFI Screenonline: Bar Mitzvah Boy (1976)|website=www.screenonline.org.uk}}</ref>

He married actress [[Maureen Lipman]] in 1974;<ref name="obit_bbc">[http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/entertainment/3040267.stm Obituary, BBC], 29 May 2004</ref> they have two grown-up children, writers [[Amy Rosenthal]] and Adam Rosenthal. Jack Rosenthal was a Manchester United fan all his life.

Rosenthal was awarded the [[Order of the British Empire|CBE]] in 1994.<ref>{{cite news |author=<!--Staff writer(s)/no by-line.--> |title=1993 CBE announcements |url=https://www.thegazette.co.uk/London/issue/53527/page/10/data.pdf |work=SUPPLEMENT TO THE LONDON GAZETTE, 31ST DECEMBER 1993 |location= |date=1993 |access-date=January 16, 2021}}</ref>

==Death and legacy==
Rosenthal died in [[London Borough of Barnet|Barnet]], London, aged 72, on 29 May 2004, of [[multiple myeloma]], a form of cancer. 

He is buried in [[Golders Green Jewish Cemetery]].

His autobiography, ''By Jack Rosenthal'', was published posthumously and a four-part adaptation by his daughter, titled ''Jack Rosenthal's Last Act'' was broadcast on BBC Radio 4 in July 2006 starring Maureen Lipman as herself and [[Stephen Mangan]] as Jack Rosenthal.
