'''Margaret Steuart "Peggy" Pollard''', née Gladstone (1 March 1904 &ndash; 13 November 1996), was a scholar of [[Sanskrit]], a poet and [[bard]] of the [[Cornish language]]. She was the founding member of [[Ferguson's Gang]], a secret society of supporters of the [[National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty|National Trust]], who had their headquarters at [[Shalford Mill]].<ref>{{Cite book|title = Ferguson- Exhibition Catalogue|last = Bagnall|first = Polly|year = 2012}}</ref> She was the great great-niece of Liberal prime minister [[William Gladstone]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|title = Ferguson's Gang: The Remarkable Story of the National Trust Gangsters|last = Polly Bagnall & Sally Beck|publisher = Pavilion Books|year = 2015|isbn = 978-1909881716|pages = 10|url = https://books.google.com/books?id=psZ7oAEACAAJ}}</ref>

From 1920, she attended [[Newnham College, Cambridge]],<ref>{{Cite book|title = Breaking Bounds: Six Newnham Lives|last = Limb|first = Sue|publisher = Newnham College, Cambridge|year = 2014|isbn = 978-0993071508}}</ref> where she was the first woman to gain first-class honours in Oriental Languages. She married Captain Frank Pollard, an expert on Cornish history, and they lived in [[Truro, Cornwall]]. By 1938, she had become a bard, and a member of the [[Cornish Gorsedd]]. She published ''Bewnans Alysaryn'', a Cornish-language [[miracle play]], in 1941.<ref name=Indie/> She was an enthusiastic supporter of campaigns to defend the landscape, language and traditions of Cornwall and rural England. On one occasion she donated £100 to the National Trust as part of [[Ferguson's Gang]], wearing a full mask to preserve her anonymity.<ref name=":0" />

In 1947, a book about her home county, entitled ''Cornwall'', that she had written was published by [[Paul Elek]].<ref>{{Cite book|title = Cornwall|last = Pollard|first = Peggy|publisher = Paul Elek|year = 1947|location = London}}</ref> She has been described as "humorous, perceptive, and intelligent".<ref name="Indie">{{cite web|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/obituarymargaret-pollard-1313345.html|title=Obituary: Margaret Pollard|last=Jenkin|first=Ann Trevenen|date=7 December 1996|work=[[The Independent]]|access-date=15 October 2010}}</ref> In 1951 she converted to [[Catholic Church|Roman Catholicism]], and in 1973 built a Roman Catholic church dedicated to Our Lady of the Portal and St Piran on the site of a medieval chapel in Truro. For this she received the [[Benemerenti Medal]] from the Pope.<ref name=":0" />

She remained an active poet and translator throughout her long life. She had given away much of her inherited wealth after her husband's death in 1968 and lived in a {{linktext|one-up-one-down}}, which was an old tin miner's cottage at Quenchwell, [[Kea, Cornwall|Kea]], near Truro.<ref>Pollard, Peggy (1947) ''Cornwall''. London: Paul Elek, pp. 11-13</ref> She remained a romantic figure, dressed as she was in a long skirt and a scarf wrapped around her head. She died at the age of 93 on 13 November 1996 at Truro.<ref name=Indie/>
