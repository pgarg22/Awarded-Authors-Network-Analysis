'''Richard George Adams''' (9 May 1920 – 24 December 2016)<ref name="BBC report"/> was an English novelist and writer of the books ''[[Watership Down]]'', ''[[Maia]]'', ''[[Shardik]]'' and ''[[The Plague Dogs]]''. He studied modern history at university before serving in the British Army during World War II. Afterwards, he completed his studies, and then joined the [[British Civil Service]]. In 1974, two years after ''Watership Down'' was published, Adams became a full-time author.<ref name="Structo talks to Richard Adams">{{cite magazine|url=http://structomagazine.co.uk/interviews/richard-adams/|title=Structo talks to Richard Adams|magazine=[[Structo]]|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref><ref name=nyt>{{cite news|author=McFadden, Robert D.|title=Richard Adams, Whose Novel 'Watership Down' Became a Phenomenon, Dies at 96|date=27 December 2016|newspaper=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/27/arts/richard-adams-author-of-watership-down-dies-at-96.html|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref>

==Early life==
Richard Adams was born on 9 May 1920 in [[Wash Common]], near [[Newbury, Berkshire|Newbury]], Berkshire, England, the son of Lillian Rosa (Button) and Evelyn George Beadon Adams, a doctor.<ref name=nyt/> He attended [[Horris Hill School]] from 1926 to 1933, and then [[Bradfield College]] from 1933 to 1938. In 1938, he went to [[Worcester College, Oxford]], to read [[Modern History#Oxford|Modern History]]. In July 1940, Adams was called up to join the [[British Army]]. He was commissioned into the [[Royal Army Service Corps]]<ref>{{London Gazette|issue=35034|supp=y|page=115|date=3 January 1941}}</ref> and was selected for the Airborne Company, where he worked as a brigade liaison. He served in [[Palestine (region)|Palestine]], Europe and the East Asia but saw no direct action against either the Germans or the Japanese.<ref>Michael D Sharp, ''Popular Contemporary Writers'', Marshall Cavendish Corporation 2006 {{ISBN|0-7614-7601-6}}(p.26)</ref>

After leaving the army in 1946, Adams returned to Worcester College to continue his studies for a further two years. He received a bachelor's degree in 1948, proceeding [[Master of Arts (Oxbridge and Dublin)|MA]] in 1953.<ref>[https://biography.jrank.org/pages/4088/Adams-Richard-George.html "Richard George Adams"], Trevor Royle, ''Brief Biographies, Contemporary Novelists vol. 1''. Retrieved 2 April 2008.</ref><ref>"ADAMS, Richard George", ''Who's Who 2008'', A & C Black, 2008; [http://www.ukwhoswho.com/view/article/oupww/whoswho/U5018 (online edition)], Oxford University Press, December 2007. Retrieved 2 April 2008.</ref> After his graduation in 1948, Adams joined the British Civil Service, rising to the rank of [[Assistant Secretary]] to the [[Ministry of Housing and Local Government]], later part of the [[Secretary of State for the Environment|Department of the Environment]]. He began to write his own stories in his spare time, reading them to his children and later on, to his grandchildren.<ref name=Blezard/>

==Career==
Adams originally began telling the story that would become ''[[Watership Down]]'' to his two daughters on a car trip.<ref name=batty/> They eventually insisted that he publish it as a book. He began writing in 1966, taking two years to complete it.<ref name=batty>{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2016/dec/27/watership-down-author-richard-adams-dies-aged-96 |title=Richard Adams, Watership Down author, dies aged 96|work=[[The Guardian]]|author=Batty, David|date=28 December 2016|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref> In 1972, after four publishers and three writers' agencies turned down the manuscript, [[Rex Collings]] agreed to publish the work.<ref name=Blezard>{{cite news|author=Blezard, Paul|title=Richard Adams: Forever animated by the life of animals|date=15 May 2010|newspaper=[[The Independent]]|url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/profiles/richard-adams-forever-animated-by-the-life-of-animals-1974572.html|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref> The book gained international acclaim almost immediately for reinvigorating anthropomorphic fiction with naturalism.<ref name=batty/><ref>{{cite news|author=Jones, Timothy|date=27 December 2016|title=''Watership Down'' author Richard Adams dies, aged 96 |url=http://www.dw.com/en/watership-down-author-richard-adams-dies-aged-96/a-36925179|website=DW|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref>

Over the next few years ''Watership Down'' sold over a million copies worldwide. Adams won both of the most prestigious British children's book awards, one of six authors to do so: the [[Carnegie Medal (literary award)|Carnegie Medal]] and the [[Guardian Children's Fiction Prize]].<ref name=medal1972/><ref name=relaunch/><ref group=lower-alpha name=GCFP/> In 1974, following publication of his second novel, ''[[Shardik]]'', he left the Civil Service to become a full-time author. He was elected a Fellow of the [[Royal Society of Literature]] in 1975.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rslit.org/content/fellows|title=Royal Society of Literature All Fellows|publisher=Royal Society of Literature|access-date=10 August 2010|url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100305070326/http://www.rslit.org/content/fellows|archive-date=5 March 2010}}</ref>

At one point, Adams served as writer-in-residence at the [[University of Florida]]<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.english.ufl.edu/crw/ |title=Department of English &#124; Graduate Programs – MFA in Fiction & Poetry|publisher=English.ufl.edu|access-date=19 April 2011}}</ref> and at [[Hollins University]] in Virginia.<ref name="Structo talks to Richard Adams"/> Adams was the recipient of the inaugural Whitchurch Arts Award for inspiration in January 2010, presented at the Watership Down pub in [[Freefolk]], Hampshire.<ref>[http://www2.newsquest.co.uk/hampshire/southampton/page_turning/wiltshire_society/20100304/index.html Water under the bridge] Wiltshire Society. March 2010. Retrieved 2 April 2010.</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.whitchurcharts.org.uk/events/whitchurch-arts-award/whitchurch-arts-award-2010-richard-adams/|title=Whitchurch Arts Award for inspiration given to Richard Adams (accessed April 2010) |publisher=Whitchurcharts.org.uk|date=9 May 1920|access-date=19 April 2011|url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110513223752/http://whitchurcharts.org.uk/events/whitchurch-arts-award/whitchurch-arts-award-2010-richard-adams/|archive-date=13 May 2011|df=dmy-all}}</ref> In 2015 he was awarded an honorary doctorate by the [[University of Winchester]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Inspirational figures celebrated at University of Winchester Graduation ceremonies|date=17 October 2015|publisher=The University of Winchester|url=http://www.winchester.ac.uk/newsandevents/Pages/Inspirational-figures-celebrated-at-University-of-Winchester-Graduation-ceremonies.aspx|access-date=27 December 2016|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161228032118/http://www.winchester.ac.uk/newsandevents/Pages/Inspirational-figures-celebrated-at-University-of-Winchester-Graduation-ceremonies.aspx|archive-date=28 December 2016|df=dmy-all}}</ref>

==Public figure==
In 1982, Adams served one year as president of the [[RSPCA]].<ref name="Structo talks to Richard Adams"/> Besides campaigning against furs, Adams wrote ''[[The Plague Dogs]]'' to satirize animal experimentation (as well as government and tabloid press).<ref>{{cite web|url=https://rabbit.org/journal/4-2/richardadams.html|title=My Afternoon with Richard Adams|author=Fezza, Charlemange|website=www.rabbit.org|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref>  He also made a voyage through the [[Antarctic]] in the company of the ornithologist [[Ronald Lockley]].<ref>{{cite book|author=Adams, Richard|author2=Lockley, Ronald|title=Voyage Through the Antarctic|publisher=Allen Lane|year=1982|isbn=978-0394528588|url=https://archive.org/details/voyagethroughant00adam}}</ref> Just before his 90th birthday, he wrote a new story for a charity book, ''Gentle Footprints'', to raise funds for the [[Born Free Foundation]].<ref name=Blezard/>

==Personal life==
In 1949, Adams married Elizabeth (Barbara), daughter of R.A.F. Squadron-Leader Edward Fox Dyke Acland, son of the barrister and judge [[Reginald Acland|Sir Reginald Brodie Dyke Acland]], whose father, the scientist [[Henry Acland|Henry Wentworth Dyke Acland]] (himself created a baronet of St Mary Magdalen, Oxford) descended from the [[Acland baronets]] of Columb John.<ref>Burke's Peerage, 1999, vol. 1, pg 26.</ref><ref>Science Fiction and Fantasy Literature, vol. 2, R. Reginald, 1979, pg 790.</ref> Until his death, he lived with his wife in Whitchurch, within {{convert|10|mi|km}} of his birthplace. Their daughters, to whom Adams originally related the tales that became ''Watership Down'', are Juliet and Rosamond.<ref>{{cite magazine|author=Cooper, Jonathan|date=11 March 1985|title=Richard Adams Follows Up Watership Down and Shardik with An Erotic Epic Called Maia|magazine=[[People (magazine)|People]]|volume=23|issue=10}}</ref><ref name=Blezard/> Adams celebrated his 90th birthday in 2010 with a party at the White Hart in his hometown of [[Whitchurch, Hampshire]], where [[Sir George Young, 6th Baronet|Sir George Young]] presented him with a painting by a local artist. Adams wrote a poetic piece celebrating his home of the past 28 years.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.whitchurcharts.org.uk/2010/05/celebration-of-richard-adams-90th-birthday/|title=Whitchurch Arts, Celebration of Richard Adams' 90th Birthday|publisher=Whitchurcharts.org.uk|access-date=19 April 2011|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110513223627/http://whitchurcharts.org.uk/2010/05/celebration-of-richard-adams-90th-birthday/|archive-date=13 May 2011|df=dmy-all}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.basingstokegazette.co.uk/news/8175644.party-time-for-richard-as-he-celebrates-90th/|author=Knox, Patrick|title=Party time for Richard as he celebrates 90th|work=Basingstoke Gazette|date=20 May 2010|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref> Adams was an Orthodox Christian.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/27/arts/richard-adams-author-of-watership-down-dies-at-96.html|title = Richard Adams, Whose Novel 'Watership Down' Became a Phenomenon, Dies at 96|newspaper = The New York Times|date = 27 December 2016|last1 = McFadden|first1 = Robert D.}}</ref>{{acn|date=October 2021}}

Adams died on 24 December 2016 at the age of 96 in [[Oxford]], England from complications of a blood disorder.<ref name="Washington Post obit">{{cite web|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/obituaries/richard-adams-best-selling-british-author-of-watership-down-dies-at-96/2016/12/27/730e7cd6-71b9-11e5-8248-98e0f5a2e830_story.html|title=Richard Adams, best-selling British author of 'Watership Down,' dies at 96|work=[[The Washington Post]]|access-date=10 July 2019|date=28 December 2016|author=Harrison Smith}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|work=Watership Down Enterprises|title=Requiescat in Pace|url=https://www.watership-down.com/2016/12/27/requiescat-in-pace/|date=27 December 2016|access-date=27 December 2016}}</ref><ref name="BBC report">{{cite news|title=Watership Down author Richard Adams dies aged 96|date=27 December 2016|work=BBC News|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/entertainment-arts-38446309|access-date=27 December 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Richard Adams: The man who turned a story about rabbits into a best-seller|date=27 December 2016|publisher=BBC News|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-10788325|access-date=10 July 2019}}</ref>
