'''Anita Diamant''' (born June 27, 1951) is an American author of fiction and non-fiction books.<ref name="ContempAuthors">"Anita Diamant." ''Contemporary Authors Online''. Farmington Hills, Mich.: Gale, 2015. Retrieved via ''Biography in Context'' database, 2017-09-22.</ref>  She has published five novels, the most recent of which is ''The Boston Girl'', a [[New York Times Best Seller list|''New York Times'' best seller]].<ref>Cowles, Gregory (December&nbsp;19, 2014). "[https://www.nytimes.com/2014/12/28/books/review/inside-the-list.html Inside the List]". ''[[The New York Times Book Review|Sunday Book Review, The New York Times]]''. Retrieved 2017-09-22. With mention of "Diamant's latest novel, 'The Boston Girl,' new on the hardcover fiction list at No. 12."</ref> She is best known for her 1997 novel ''[[The Red Tent (Anita Diamant novel)|The Red Tent]]'', which eventually became a best seller and [[Book discussion club|book club]] favorite.<ref>Genzlinger, Neil (December&nbsp;5, 2014). "[https://www.nytimes.com/2014/12/06/arts/television/the-red-tent-imagines-the-life-of-dinah.html A Biblical Girls’ Club: ‘The Red Tent’ Imagines the Life of Dinah]". ''[[The New York Times]]''. Retrieved 2017-09-22.</ref><ref name="Ghent">Ghent, Janet Silver (February&nbsp;14, 2014). "[https://www.jweekly.com/2014/02/14/celebrating-the-power-of-sisterhood-and-jewish-ritual-author-anita-diamant/ Celebrating the power of women and ritual: Author Diamant in conversation at JCCs]". ''[[J. The Jewish News of Northern California]]''. jweekly.com. == Early life and education ==

Diamant spent her early childhood in [[Newark, New Jersey|Newark]], [[New Jersey]], and moved to [[Denver]], [[Colorado]],<ref name="Ghent"/> when she was 12 years old. She attended the [[University of Colorado Boulder]] and transferred to [[Washington University]] in [[Saint Louis, Missouri|St. Louis]], [[Missouri]], where she earned a bachelor's degree in [[Comparative Literature]] in 1973.<ref name="ContempAuthors"/> She then earned a master's degree in English from [[Binghamton University]] in 1975.<ref name="ContempAuthors"/>

== Career ==

[[Image:Aninadiamantnightingale.jpg|right|thumb|300px|Anita Diamant at a book signing at Nightingale House, London, March 2010]]
Diamant started her writing career in 1975 as a freelance journalist.  Her articles have been published in the ''[[Boston Globe]]'' magazine, ''[[Parenting (magazine)|Parenting]]'' magazine, ''[[New England Monthly]]'', ''[[Yankee (magazine)|Yankee]]'', ''[[Self (magazine)|Self]]'', ''[[Parents (magazine)|Parents]]'', ''[[McCall's]]'', and ''[[Ms. (magazine)|Ms]]''.<ref>[[Mary Ellen Snodgrass|Snodgrass, Mary Ellen]] (2006). "Diamant, Anita", in ''[https://books.google.com/books?id=bZRH7C3YVGcC&pg=PA140 Encyclopedia of Feminist Literature]''. New&nbsp;York: Facts on File. {{ISBN|0-8160-6040-1}}. p.&nbsp;140-141.</ref>

Her first book was ''The New Jewish Wedding'', published in 1985, and has since published five other guidebooks about contemporary Jewish practice. Her debut as a fiction writer came in 1997 with ''The Red Tent,'' followed by the novels, ''Good Harbor'' and ''The Last Days of Dogtown''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ias.umn.edu/2013/09/26/biographical-novels/ |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160425230313/http://ias.umn.edu/2013/09/26/biographical-novels/ |archive-date=April 25, 2016 |title=IAS Thursdays: Women in Religious Con/texts |publisher=[[Institute for Advanced Study at University of Minnesota|Institute for Advanced Study]]. University of Minnesota. ias.umn.edu|date=September 26, 2013 |type=event announcement|access-date=2017-09-22}}</ref> The latter is an account of life in a dying [[Cape Ann, Massachusetts]] village, [[Dogtown, Massachusetts|Dogtown]], in the early 19th century.<ref name="ContempAuthors"/>

Her next novel, ''Day After Night'' (2009), tells the stories of four women [[Sh'erit ha-Pletah|survivors]] of the [[The Holocaust|Holocaust]] who, in the period following the end of the war and before the founding of the State of [[Israel]], find themselves detained in the [[Atlit detainee camp|Atlit detention center]], just south of Haifa, in the [[Mandatory Palestine|British Mandate of Palestine]].<ref name="ContempAuthors"/>

In 2014 she published the novel ''The Boston Girl'', a coming-of-age story about an immigrant girl in the early 20th century.

==Personal life==

Diamant is the founding president of Mayyim Hayyim: Living Waters Community Mikveh and Education Center, a community-based ritual bath in [[Newton, Massachusetts]].<ref name="Ghent"/><ref name="Epstein">Epstein, Nadine (July 2008). "Anita Diamant: A Mikveh of Her Own". ''[[Moment (magazine)|Moment]]''. pp.&nbsp;29-34, 52-55. Retrieved via ''Proquest'' database, 2017-09-22.</ref>

She lives in [[Newton, Massachusetts]], is married, and has one daughter.<ref name="ContempAuthors"/>
