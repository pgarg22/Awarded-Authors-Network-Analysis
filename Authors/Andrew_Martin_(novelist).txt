'''Andrew Martin''' (born 6 July 1962) is an English novelist, documentary maker, journalist and musician.

Martin was brought up in [[Yorkshire]], studied at [[Merton College, Oxford]] and qualified as a [[barrister]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.jimstringernovels.com/home|title = Andrew Martin}}</ref> He has since worked as a freelance journalist for a number of publications while writing novels, starting with ''Bilton'', a [[comic novel]] about journalists, and ''The Bobby Dazzlers'', a comic novel set in the North of England, for which he was named ''[[Spectator (magazine)|Spectator]]'' Young Writer of the Year.

''[[The Guardian]]'' claimed ''Bilton'' and ''The Bobby Dazzlers'' "rank high in the lists of the best comic novels published in the past 10 years".<ref name=marchant>{{cite news|last=Marchant|first=Ian|title=Oysters and Bass|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2004/sep/11/featuresreviews.guardianreview15|accessdate=10 January 2014|newspaper=The Guardian|date=11 September 2004}}</ref>

His series of [[detective fiction|detective novel]]s about Jim Stringer, a railwayman reassigned to the [[North Eastern Railway (UK)|North Eastern Railway]] police in [[Edwardian England]], includes ''The Necropolis Railway'' (set on the real [[London Necropolis Railway]]), ''The Blackpool Highflyer'', ''The Lost Luggage Porter'', ''Murder at Deviation Junction'', ''Death on a Branch Line'', ''The Last Train to Scarborough'', ''The Somme Stations'' (Winner of the [[Crime Writers' Association|CWA]] [[Ellis Peters Historical Award]] 2011)<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.rlf.org.uk/fellowships/andrew-martin/|title = Andrew Martin}}</ref> and ''The Baghdad Railway Club''.<ref>{{cite web|title=Andrew Martin home page|url=http://www.jimstringernovels.com|accessdate=23 July 2015}}</ref>

In 2015, he released ''The Yellow Diamond, A Crime of the Super-Rich'', a detective novel set in London's [[Mayfair]]. In summer 2017, he released 'Soot' an acclaimed crime novel set in 18th century York.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2017/aug/25/best-recent-crime-novels-review-roundup|title = The best recent crime novels – review roundup|date = 25 August 2017}}</ref>

He has also written a number of works of non-fiction. Railway-related titles include ''Underground Overground, A Passenger's History of the Tube;'' ''Belles and Whistles, Five Journeys Through Time on Britain's Trains'' and ''Night Trains, The Rise and Fall of the Sleeper''.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/profile/andrewmartin|title=Andrew Martin &#124; the Guardian}}</ref>

Other non-fiction works include ''How to Get Things Really Flat'';<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.standard.co.uk/standard-home/how-to-get-things-really-flat-by-andrew-martin-6717682.html|title = How to Get Things Really Flat by Andrew Martin|date = 5 April 2012}}</ref> ''Ghoul Britannia'' and ''Flight by Elephant'' about [[Gyles Mackrell]] and his Burmese, elephant-assisted wartime rescue mission, published in 2013.

In addition, he is the editor of a dictionary of humorous quotations: ''Funny You Should Say That: A Compendium of Jokes, Quips and Quotations from Cicero to the Simpsons''.

His works for television and radio include: ''Between the Lines, Railways in Fiction and Film (2008)'',<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b00dwflh#:~:text=Between%20the%20Lines%20%2D%20Railways%20in%20Fiction%20and%20Film,-Series%208&text=Novelist%20Andrew%20Martin%20presents%20a,Dickens%20to%20The%20Railway%20Children.|title=BBC Four - Timeshift, Series 8, Between the Lines - Railways in Fiction and Film}}</ref> ''Disappearing Dad, Fathers in Literature (2010)'',<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/blogs/tv/2010/06/disappearing-dads-is-fiction.shtml|title = BBC - BBC TV blog: Disappearing Dad: Is fiction better off without fathers?}}</ref> ''The Trains that Time Forgot: Britain's Lost Railway Journeys'' (2015),<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b068fvln|title = BBC Four - Timeshift, Series 15, the Trains That Time Forgot: Britain's Lost Railway Journeys}}</ref> all in the Timeshift series, and three essay series for [[BBC Radio 3|Radio 3]], ''The Sound and The Fury'' (2013),<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b019nmxz|title = BBC Radio 3 - the Essay, the Sound and the Fury}}</ref> ''England Ejects'' (2014), ''The Further Realm'' (2015).<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b06kcmq0|title = BBC Radio 3 - the Essay, the Further Realm, the Further Realm: Episode 3}}</ref> He also writes short stories for the Calm app.[https://www.calm.com/narrators/EJG7W-SXX/andrew-martin]

Andrew writes and performs music under the name [https://open.spotify.com/artist/7a0EgrWPxQJ2zWgoNn6CCJ?si=39COo21tTUaYI2sQm3xHlg Brunswick Green].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://jimstringernovels.com/music|title = Brunswick Green}}</ref>

Andrew Martin lives in north London with his wife and sons.
