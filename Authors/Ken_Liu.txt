'''Ken Liu''' (born 1976) is a multiple [[Hugo Award|Hugo Award-winning]] [[Americans|American]] author of [[science fiction]] and [[fantasy]].  His epic fantasy series ''The Dandelion Dynasty'', the first work in the "silkpunk" genre, is published by [[Simon & Schuster]].<ref name="tordotcomsummary">{{cite web |title=Ken Liu Talks Silkpunk, Old Poems, and Contemporary Chinese SFF in His Reddit AMA |url=https://www.tor.com/2015/04/13/ken-liu-reddit-ama-highlights/ |website=Tor.com}}</ref> His short stories have appeared in ''[[F&SF]]'', ''[[Asimov's]]'', ''[[Analog Science Fiction and Fact|Analog]]'', ''[[Lightspeed (magazine)|Lightspeed]]'', ''[[Clarkesworld]]'', and multiple "Year's Best" anthologies.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Clarkesworld Magazine - Science Fiction & Fantasy|url = http://clarkesworldmagazine.com/awards_and_recognition/|website = Clarkesworld Magazine|access-date = 2015-10-11|language = en}}</ref>

==Childhood and career==
Liu was born in 1976 in [[Lanzhou]], [[China]].<ref name="nw103016"/> He spent his childhood with his grandparents.<ref>{{cite web |title=Ken Liu Talks Silkpunk, Old Poems, and Contemporary Chinese SFF in His Reddit AMA |url=https://www.tor.com/2015/04/13/ken-liu-reddit-ama-highlights/ |website=Tor.com |access-date=15 March 2019}}</ref> His mother, who received her Ph.D. in chemistry in the United States, is a pharmaceutical chemist, while his father is a computer engineer.<ref name="asiaone">{{cite web
|url=http://www.asiaone.com/ken-liu-won-science-fiction-awards-best-short-story?nopaging=1
|title=Ken Liu won science fiction awards for best short story
|access-date=2019-01-05
|date=2013-09-09
|publisher=[[AsiaOne]]
}}</ref> The family immigrated to the United States when Liu was 11 years old.<ref name="nw103016">{{cite web
|url=https://www.newsweek.com/man-bringing-chinese-science-fiction-west-514893
|title=MEET THE MAN BRINGING CHINESE SCIENCE FICTION TO THE WEST
|access-date=2019-01-05
|date=2016-10-30
|publisher=[[Newsweek]]
}}</ref> They lived in [[California]] and [[Stonington, Connecticut]] before settling in [[Waterford, Connecticut|Waterford]], Connecticut. Liu graduated from [[Waterford High School (Connecticut)|Waterford High School]] in 1994, where he ran cross-country and track.<ref name="day">{{cite web
|url=https://www.theday.com/article/20150414/ENT02/150419746
|title=Waterford alum — and award-winning short story writer — Ken Liu releases his debut novel
|access-date=2019-01-05
|date=2015-04-14
|publisher=[[The Day (New London)]]
}}</ref> At [[Harvard College]], he studied English Literature and Computer Science, receiving his A. B. in 1998.<ref name="day"/><ref name=hm>{{cite web
|url=https://harvardmagazine.com/2016/11/fusion-fantasy
|title=Fusion Fantasy: Ken Liu's sprawling hybrid fiction
|access-date=2019-01-05
|date=November–December 2016
|publisher=[[Harvard Magazine]]
}}</ref> After graduation, he worked as a software engineer for [[Microsoft]], and then joined a start-up in [[Cambridge, Massachusetts]]. He later received his J.D. from [[Harvard Law School]] in 2004 and after working as a corporate lawyer, eventually became a high-tech litigation consultant.<ref name="day"/><ref name=hm/> He began publishing fiction in 2002. His first published work was "Carthaginian Rose", a short story on [[mind uploading]] which was published alongside nine other authors in ''[[The Phobos Science Fiction Anthology Volume 1]]''.<ref>{{cite web |last1=Liu |first1=Ken |title=Carthaginian Rose |url=https://kenliu.name/stories/carthaginian-rose/ |website=Ken Liu, Writer |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191214061409/https://kenliu.name/stories/carthaginian-rose/ |archive-date=14 December 2019 |access-date=14 December 2019}}</ref>

Liu says he wanted to become a writer so he could make stories that “turn values upside down and inside out to gain new perspectives.”<ref name="wbur.org">{{Cite web|title=Modern Mythmaking In Ken Liu's 'The Hidden Girl And Other Stories'|url=https://www.wbur.org/artery/2020/02/24/ken-liu-the-hidden-girl-and-other-stories|access-date=2020-12-07|website=www.wbur.org|language=en}}</ref>

After a long career writing and publishing short fiction, Liu turned to epic fantasy novels, starting with ''The Grace of Kings'' in 2015.<ref>{{cite web |title=Interview: Ken Liu |url=http://www.lightspeedmagazine.com/nonfiction/interview-ken-liu/ |website=Lightspeed |access-date=15 March 2019}}</ref> He has also written for the ''[[Star Wars]]'' universe, with ''The Legends of Luke Skywalker'' in 2017.<ref>{{cite web |title=Interview on The Legends of Luke Skywalker |url=https://www.starwars.com/news/ken-liu-exploring-perfect-mythic-figure-the-legends-of-luke-skywalker |website=StarWars.com |access-date=15 March 2019}}</ref>

Along with his original work, Liu has translated the works of multiple Chinese authors into English, including [[w: Liu Cixin|Liu Cixin]], [[w:Hao Jingfang|Hao Jingfang]], [[w: Chen Qiufan|Chen Qiufan]], and [[w:Xia Jia|Xia Jia]].<ref>{{cite web |title=Chinese SF and the art of translation |url=http://blogs.nature.com/futureconditional/2016/08/20/chinese-sf-and-the-art-of-translation-a-qa-with-ken-liu/ |website=Nature.com |access-date=15 March 2019}}</ref> His translation of ''[[w: The Three-Body Problem (novel)|The Three Body Problem]]'' by Liu Cixin helped the book become a best seller to English readers.<ref>{{cite web |title=How Ken Liu went from engineer to lawyer to SF writer to the foremost translator of Chinese sf into English| url=https://boingboing.net/2019/12/04/rod-serling-w-chinese-characte.html |website= boingboing.net |access-date=6 December 2019}}</ref> He also has worked on editing works. While editing the anthology ''Invisible Planets'', Ken Liu translated the stories contained within it from Chinese into English.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://electricliterature.com/ken-liu-will-keep-an-open-mind/|title=Ken Liu Will Keep an Open Mind|website=electricliterature.com|access-date=6 December 2019}}</ref>

Some of Liu's work have been adapted into visual media. "Memories of My Mother," a short story, was the basis of "Beautiful Dreamer" by David Gaddie.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt4794788/ |title=Beautiful Dreamer (2016) |website=[[IMDb]] |access-date=15 March 2019}}</ref> "Real Artists," a short story, was adapted into a short film by Cameo Wood.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt5648094/ |title=Real Artists (2017) |website=[[IMDb]]}}</ref> "Good Hunting," a short story, was adapted into an animated short as part of [[Netflix]]'s ''[[Love, Death & Robots]]'' series in 2019.

Liu's short story collection, “The Hidden Girl and Other Stories” published in 2020, explores ideas such as tradition and progress, the fallibility of memory, and the essence of what it means to be human.<ref name="wbur.org"/>

Liu now lives with his family near [[Bolton, Massachusetts|Boston, Massachusetts]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=About|url=https://kenliu.name/about/#more-2|access-date=2020-12-07|website=kenliu.name}}</ref>

==Awards==

Liu's short story "[[The Paper Menagerie]]" is the first work of fiction, of any length, to win all of the Nebula, Hugo, and World Fantasy Awards.<ref name="strangehorizons">{{cite web |url=http://www.strangehorizons.com/2013/20130325/1reid-a.shtml |title=Not Just Vast Armies Clashing on Dark Plains at Night: An Interview with Ken Liu |first=Luc |date=2013-03-25 |last=Reid |work=[[Strange Horizons]] |access-date=2013-05-15 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130530105508/http://www.strangehorizons.com/2013/20130325/1reid-a.shtml |archive-date=2013-05-30 }}</ref> In addition, his short story, "Mono no aware" won the 2013 [[Hugo Award]],<ref name=hugo2013>{{cite web |url=http://www.thehugoawards.org/hugo-history/2013-hugo-awards/ |title=2013 Hugo Awards |date=22 December 2012 |access-date=4 September 2013 }}</ref><ref name=2013guardian>{{cite web |url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/booksblog/2013/sep/02/hugo-awards-2013-science-fiction |title=The Hugo awards: 'beauty contest' or prize of the people? |author=David Barnett |work=[[The Guardian]] |date=2 September 2013 |access-date=4 September 2013 }}</ref> and his novella "The Man Who Ended History: A Documentary" was also nominated for a Hugo.<ref name="hugo2012">{{cite web |url=http://www.thehugoawards.org/hugo-history/2012-hugo-awards/ |title=2012 Hugo Awards |date=7 April 2012 |access-date=4 September 2013 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://www.webcitation.org/66nnsGGRI?url=http://www.thehugoawards.org/hugo-history/2012-hugo-awards/ |archive-date=9 April 2012 }}</ref> The first novel in his ''The Dandelion Dynasty'' series, ''The Grace of Kings'', was a 2016 [[Nebula Award]] finalist.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.sfwa.org/2016/05/nebula-award-winners-announced-3/|title = Nebula Award Winners Announced|date = 15 May 2016}}</ref> The novel was the 2016 [[Locus Award]] Best First Novel winner.<ref>{{cite web |title=2016 Locus Award Winners |url=https://locusmag.com/2016/06/2016-locus-awards-winners/ |website=locusmag.com |date=25 June 2016 |access-date=15 March 2019}}</ref>

Besides his original work, Liu's translation of [[Liu Cixin]]'s [[Chinese language]] novel ''[[The Three-Body Problem (novel)|The Three-Body Problem]]'' (the first in the ''[[Remembrance of Earth's Past]]'' trilogy) won the 2015 [[Hugo Award for Best Novel]], making it the first translated novel to have won the award.<ref name=hugo2015>{{cite web |url=http://www.thehugoawards.org/hugo-history/2015-hugo-awards/ |title=2015 Hugo Awards |date=31 March 2015 |access-date=2015-08-23 }}</ref> Liu also translated the third volume of the ''Remembrance of Earth's Past'' series, ''[[Death's End]],'' in 2016, which was a 2017 [[Hugo Award for Best Novel]] finalist.

One of Liu’s short stories, “[https://slate.com/technology/2019/01/thoughts-and-prayers-ken-liu-short-story.html Thoughts and Prayers],” is a part of Jonathan Strahan’s The Year’s Best Science Fiction (2020), Vol 1.
