'''Benjamin S. Lerner''' (born February 4, 1979)<ref>[https://web.archive.org/web/20160926193646/http://www.lyrikline.org/en/poems/im-going-kill-president-7372#.WZblr1FLfyN Ben Lerner in Lyrikline]</ref> is an American poet, novelist, essayist, and critic. He has been a [[Fulbright Scholar]], a finalist for the [[Pulitzer Prize for Fiction]], a finalist for the [[National Book Award]], a finalist for the [[National Book Critics Circle Award]], a Howard Foundation Fellow, a [[Guggenheim Fellow]], and a [[MacArthur Fellow]], among other honors. In 2011 he won the "Preis der Stadt Münster für internationale Poesie", the first American to receive the honor.<ref name="muenster1">{{cite web|url=http://www.muenster.de/stadt/kulturamt/lyrikertreffen.html |title=Stadt Münster: Kulturamt – Lyrikertreffen |publisher=Muenster.de |access-date=2011-06-19| archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20110617111018/http://www.muenster.de/stadt/kulturamt/lyrikertreffen.html| archive-date= 17 June 2011 | url-status= live}}</ref> Lerner teaches at [[Brooklyn College]], where he was named a Distinguished Professor of English in 2016.<ref>{{cite web

==Life and work==
Lerner was born and raised in [[Topeka]], [[Kansas]], which figures in each of his books of poetry. His mother is the clinical psychologist [[Harriet Lerner]].<ref>{{cite web|author=Link |url=http://ronsilliman.blogspot.com/2006/12/nature-of-influence-changes-over-time.html |title=Silliman's Blog |publisher=Ronsilliman.blogspot.com |date=2006-12-05 |access-date=2011-06-19}}</ref> He is a 1997 graduate of [[Topeka High School]], where he participated in debate and forensics, winning the 1997 [[National Speech and Debate Association|National Forensic League]] National Tournament in International Extemporaneous Speaking.<ref name="blankenship">{{cite news |url=http://cjonline.com/stories/030905/loc_poet.shtml |title=Young poet to read works at Washburn |last=Blankenship |first=Bill |newspaper=[[The Topeka Capital-Journal]] |date=March 9, 2005 |access-date=May 7, 2014}}</ref> At [[Brown University]] he studied with poet [[C. D. Wright]] and earned a [[Bachelor of Arts|B.A.]] in [[political theory]] and an [[Master of Fine Arts|MFA]] in poetry.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.newyorker.com/books/page-turner/postscript-c-d-wright-1949-2016|title=Postscript: C.D. Wright, 1949-2016|last=Lerner|first=Ben|date=January 14, 2016|website=The New Yorker}}</ref>

Lerner was awarded the [[Hayden Carruth]] prize for his cycle of 52 [[sonnets]], ''The Lichtenberg Figures''.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zZW49zCMDy8 |title=Ben Lerner's First Time |work=The Paris Review |access-date=June 27, 2016}}</ref>
In 2004 ''[[Library Journal]]'' named it one of the year's 12 best books of poetry.

In 2003 Lerner traveled on a [[Fulbright Scholarship]] to Madrid, Spain, where he wrote his second book of poetry, ''Angle of Yaw'', which was published in 2006. It was named a finalist for the [[National Book Award]]. His third poetry collection, ''Mean Free Path'', was published in 2010.

Lerner's first novel, ''[[Leaving the Atocha Station]],'' published in 2011,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.narrativemagazine.com/authors/ben-lerner |title=Ben Lerner |publisher=Narrative Magazine |date= 2008-12-15|access-date=2011-06-19| archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20110714155631/http://www.narrativemagazine.com/authors/ben-lerner| archive-date= 14 July 2011 | url-status= live}}</ref> won the [[Believer Book Award]]<ref name="mediabistro.com">{{cite web|url=http://www.mediabistro.com/galleycat/ben-lerner-wins-the-believer-book-award_b50967|title=Ben Lerner Wins the Believer Book Award|access-date=22 March 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150103191145/http://www.mediabistro.com/galleycat/ben-lerner-wins-the-believer-book-award_b50967|archive-date=3 January 2015|url-status=dead}}</ref> and was a finalist for the ''[[Los Angeles Times]]'' Book Award for first fiction and the [[New York Public Library]]'s Young Lions prize. Writing in ''[[The Guardian]]'', [[Geoff Dyer]] called it "a work so luminously original in style and form as to seem like a premonition, a comet from the future."<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2012/jul/05/leaving-atocha-station-ben-lerner |title=Leaving the Atocha Station by Ben Lerner – review |access-date=2016-12-11 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161121202411/https://www.theguardian.com/books/2012/jul/05/leaving-atocha-station-ben-lerner |archive-date=2016-11-21 |newspaper=The Guardian |date=2012-07-05 |last1=Dyer |first1=Geoff }}</ref> Excerpts of Lerner's second novel, ''[[10:04 (novel)|10:04]]'', won the [[Terry Southern]] Prize from ''[[The Paris Review]]''.<ref name="theparisreview">{{cite web|url=http://www.theparisreview.org/blog/2014/03/12/emma-cline-wins-plimpton-prize-ben-lerner-wins-terry-southern-prize/|title=Emma Cline Wins Plimpton Prize; Ben Lerner Wins Terry Southern Prize|author=The Paris Review|work=The Paris Review|access-date=22 March 2016|date=2014-03-12}}</ref> Writing in the ''[[Los Angeles Review of Books]]'', [[Maggie Nelson]] called ''10:04'' a "near perfect piece of literature."<ref>Nelson, Maggie (August 24, 2014). "[https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/95063 Slipping the Surly Bonds of Earth: On Ben Lerner's Latest]". ''Los Angeles Review of Books''. Retrieved 2019-10-09.</ref> Lerner's 2019 novel, ''[[The Topeka School]]'', was acclaimed in ''[[The New York Times Book Review]]'' as "a high-water mark in recent American fiction."<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2019/10/03/books/review/topeka-school-ben-lerner.html|title=Ben Lerner's 'The Topeka School' Revisits the Debates of the '90s|last=Hallberg|first=Garth Risk|date=2019-10-03|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-10-05|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> Giles Harvey, in ''The New York Times Magazine'', called ''The Topeka School'' "the best book yet by the most talented writer of his generation." Lerner’s essays, art criticism, and literary criticism have appeared in [[Art in America]], ''boundary 2'', ''Frieze'', ''[[Harper's]]'', ''The Los Angeles Review of Books'', and ''[[The New Yorker]]'', among other publications.<ref name="gf.org">{{cite web |url=http://www.gf.org/fellows/17431-ben-lerner |title=Archived copy |access-date=2013-04-12 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130415120708/http://www.gf.org/fellows/17431-ben-lerner |archive-date=2013-04-15 }}</ref> ''The Topeka School'' was a finalist for the [[2020 Pulitzer Prize|2020]] [[Pulitzer Prize]] [[Pulitzer Prize for Fiction|for Fiction]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Moser, Whitehead, McDaniel, Grandin, Boyer, Brown Win 2020 Pulitzers |url=https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/industry-news/awards-and-prizes/article/83233-pulitzer-prizes-2020.html |last=Maher |first=John |date=May 4, 2020 |website=[[Publishers Weekly]] |access-date=May 4, 2020}}</ref>

In 2008 Lerner began editing poetry for ''[[Critical Quarterly]]'', a British scholarly publication.<ref>{{cite journal|title=The 'angle of immunity': face and façade in Beckett's ''Film''|date=2008-04-16 | doi=10.1111/j.1467-8705.2008.00833.x | volume=50 |issue=3 | journal=Critical Quarterly | pages=77–89|last1 = Gavin|first1 = Alice}}</ref> In 2016 he became the first poetry editor at [[Harper's]].<ref>{{Cite journal|url=http://harpers.org/archive/2016/03/the-drums-of-marrakesh/ |title=The Drums of Marrakesh |journal=Harper's Magazine |access-date=2016-04-04 |url-status=live |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160502111102/http://harpers.org/archive/2016/03/the-drums-of-marrakesh/ |archive-date=2016-05-02 |date=March 2016 }}</ref> He has taught at [[California College of the Arts]] and the [[University of Pittsburgh]], and in 2010 joined the faculty of the MFA program at [[Brooklyn College]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://depthome.brooklyn.cuny.edu/english/graduate/mfa/faculty.htm |title=Brooklyn College English Department – MFA Faculty |publisher=Depthome.brooklyn.cuny.edu |access-date=2011-06-19 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110903101904/http://depthome.brooklyn.cuny.edu/english/graduate/mfa/faculty.htm |archive-date=2011-09-03 }}</ref>

In 2016 Lerner became a Fellow of the [[New York Institute for the Humanities]].<ref>{{cite web | url=http://nyihumanities.org/all-features/new-fellows-2016 | title=Meet the New Fellows of 2016}}</ref> He received a 2015 [[MacArthur Fellows Program|MacArthur Fellowship]].<ref>{{Cite web|title = Ben Lerner — MacArthur Foundation|url = https://www.macfound.org/fellows/938/|website = www.macfound.org|access-date = 2015-09-29}}</ref>
