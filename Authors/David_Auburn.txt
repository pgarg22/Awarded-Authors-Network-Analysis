'''David Auburn''' (born November 30, 1969)<ref name="Encyclopedia of Arkansas">{{cite web | url = http://www.encyclopediaofarkansas.net/encyclopedia/entry-detail.aspx?entryID=5932 | title = David Auburn (1969–) | work = Encyclopedia of Arkansas}}</ref> is an American playwright, screenwriter, and theatre director. He is best known for his 2000 play ''[[Proof (play)|Proof]]'', which won the 2001 [[Tony Award for Best Play]] and [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]].  He also wrote the screenplays for the [[Proof (2005 film)|2005 film version of ''Proof'']], ''[[The Lake House (film)|The Lake House]]'' (2006), ''[[The Girl in the Park]]'' (2007), and ''[[Georgetown (film)|Georgetown]]'' (2019).

==Early life==
Auburn was born in [[Chicago, Illinois]], to parents Mark and Sandy Auburn. He was raised in [[Ohio]] until 1982 when his family moved to [[Arkansas]]. After graduating from high school in 1987, he attended the [[University of Chicago]], where he was a member of [[Off-Off Campus]], and received a [[Bachelor of Arts]] degree in English literature in 1991. Following a one-year fellowship with [[Amblin Entertainment]], he moved to [[New York City]] in 1992. Auburn spent two years in the [[Juilliard School]]'s playwriting program, studying under the noted dramatists [[Marsha Norman]] and [[Christopher Durang]].

==Career==
Auburn wrote several short plays, collectively grouped as ''Fifth Planet and Other Plays''. The plays, called "cockeyed and engaging little one-act comedies", were presented at Beowulf Alley Theatre Company, Tucson, Arizona, in January and February 2008.<ref name=reel>Reel, James. [http://www.tucsonweekly.com/tucson/confronted-with-issues/Content?oid=1090292&mode= "Confronted With Issues"] ''Tucson Weekly'',  January 24, 2008</ref> The plays are: ''Fifth Planet'', ''Miss You'', ''Are You Ready'', ''Damage Control'', ''Three Monologues'', ''What Do You Believe About The Future?'' and ''We Had A Very Good Time''.<ref>Auburn, David. [http://www.dramatists.com/cgi-bin/db/single.asp?key=3022 "''Fifth Planet and Other Plays'' Short Comedy Collection"] dramatists.com, accessed September 12, 2015</ref> ''Fifth Planet'' is a two-person play with 44 short scenes. ''Miss You'' is a "telephone play about love and unfaithfulness" <ref name=reel/> with a two-person cast, with each actor playing 2 roles, named "man" and "woman".<ref name=plays/> ''We Had A Very Good Time'' follows a  married couple at the end of a vacation in an unnamed foreign country.<ref name=reel/> ''Damage Control'' concerns a political consultant preparing his political boss  for a speech about a scandal the politician is involved in.<ref name=reel/> ''[[What Do You Believe About The Future?]]'' appeared in ''[[Harper's Magazine]]'' and has since been adapted for the screen.<ref name="Encyclopedia of Arkansas"/>

Auburn's first full-length play, ''[[Skyscraper (play)|Skyscraper]]'', ran [[Off Broadway]] in September – October 1997. It concerns a group attempting to save a historic skyscraper from being demolished.

Auburn is best known for his 2000 play ''[[Proof (play)|Proof]]'',<ref>{{cite web|author=The Broadway League |url=http://www.ibdb.com/person.php?id=73915 |title=David Auburn &#124; IBDB: The official source for Broadway Information |publisher=IBDB |access-date=August 6, 2010}}</ref> which won the 2001 [[Tony Award]] for Best Play, as well as the 2001 [[Pulitzer Prize for Drama]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.pbs.org/newshour/bb/media/jan-june01/auburn_04-20.html |title=Online NewsHour: Pulitzer Prize Winner – April 20, 2001 |publisher=Pbs.org |date=April 20, 2001 |access-date=August 6, 2010}}</ref> He adapted it into a [[Proof (2005 film)|film]], which was released in 2005.<ref>{{cite web |last=Weber |first=Bruce |url=http://topics.nytimes.com/topics/reference/timestopics/people/a/david_auburn/index.html |title=David Auburn News – The New York Times |publisher=Topics.nytimes.com |access-date=August 6, 2010}}</ref>

His play ''[[The Columnist]]'' had its world premiere in a production by the [[Manhattan Theatre Club]] on [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]], running from April 3, 2012 through June 3, 2012 and starring [[John Lithgow]] with [[Boyd Gaines]], [[Margaret Colin]], [[Stephen Kunken]], [[Marc Bonan]], [[Grace Gummer]] and [[Brian J. Smith]], with direction by [[Daniel J. Sullivan|Daniel Sullivan]].<ref>{{cite web | url = http://www.tcg.org/tools/newplays/details2011a.cfm?ShowID=164 | title = 'The Columnist' Listing | publisher = Theatre Communications Group.com | access-date = March 26, 2012 | url-status = dead | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20131101155505/http://www.tcg.org/tools/newplays/details2011a.cfm?ShowID=164 | archive-date = November 1, 2013 }}</ref> ''Lost Lake'' premiered Off-Broadway in a Manhattan Theatre Club production at New York City Center—Stage 1, running from November 11 to December 21, 2014. Directed by Daniel Sullivan, the two-person cast starred [[John Hawkes (actor)|John Hawkes]] and [[Tracie Thoms]].<ref>[http://www.broadway.com/buzz/178323/david-auburns-lost-lake-starring-john-hawkes-tracie-thoms-opens-off-broadway/ "David Auburn's 'Lost Lake', Starring John Hawkes & Tracie Thoms, Opens Off-Broadway"] broadway.com, November 11, 2014</ref> The play was developed at the Eugene O’Neill Playwrights Conference and presented at the Sullivan Project at the University of Illinois in February 2014.<ref>[http://www.krannertcenter.com/performance.aspx?id=201371895910741128174106178 "The Sullivan Project. 'Lost Lake'"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150818232432/http://www.krannertcenter.com/performance.aspx?id=201371895910741128174106178 |date=2015-08-18 }} krannertcenter.com, accessed September 2, 2015</ref><ref>Stasio, Marilyn. [https://variety.com/2014/legit/reviews/off-broadway-review-lost-lake-john-hawkes-1201353612/ "Off Broadway Review: ‘Lost Lake’ Starring John Hawkes"] ''Variety'',  November 11, 2014</ref> The first reading of ''Lost Lake'' was done at the O'Neill Center Rose Theater Barn July 26–27, 2013, directed by [[Wendy C. Goldberg]] and starring Frank Wood and Elsa Davis.<ref>Saifi, Sophia. [http://www.playbill.com/celebritybuzz/article/playbill.coms-brief-encounter-with-david-auburn-on-lost-lake-at-the-annual--208177# "Playbill.com's Brief Encounter With David Auburn, on 'Lost Lake', at the Annual Playwrights Conference"] August 2, 2013</ref>

Auburn has been awarded the Helen Merrill Playwriting Award and a [[Guggenheim Fellowship]].<ref>{{cite web | url = http://www.gf.org/fellows/537-david-auburn | title = David Auburn – John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation | publisher = Gf.org | access-date = August 6, 2010 | url-status = dead | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20110622011122/http://www.gf.org/fellows/537-david-auburn | archive-date = June 22, 2011 }}</ref> He received the [[Kesselring Prize]] in 2000 for ''Proof''; the prize is given to a playwright who shows the most promise and comes with a $10,000 monetary award.<ref name=kess>Ehren, Christine. [http://www.playbill.com/news/article/proofs-david-auburn-wins-kesselring-prize-92409# "Proof's David Auburn Wins Kesselring Prize"] Playbill, October 11, 2000</ref>

Following ''Proof'', he wrote the screenplay for the movie ''[[The Lake House (film)|The Lake House]]'', released by [[Warner Bros.]] in 2006. In 2007, he made his film directorial debut with ''[[The Girl in the Park]]'', for which he also wrote the screenplay.<ref>{{IMDb name|1430632}}</ref> He has also directed stage works. He directed the play ''Sick'' by Zayd Dohrn at the [[Berkshire Theatre Festival]] from August 18 to September 6, 2009.<ref>[http://myvanwy.tripod.com/releases/davidauburn.html "David Auburn Press Release"] myvanwy.tripod.com, April 21, 2009, accessed September 2, 2015</ref> He directed ''Anna Christie'' by [[Eugene O'Neill]] at the Berkshire Theatre Festival in July 2013.<ref>Dorsey, Christina. [http://www.theday.com/article/20130726/ENT12/307269981# "Pulitzer winner David Auburn develops new play the O’Neill"] theday.com, July 26, 2013</ref> He directed the play ''Side Effects'' by [[Michael Weller]] in June and July 2011 at the Off-Broadway MCC Theater.<ref>[http://www.lortel.org/lla_archive/index.cfm?search_by=show&id=5740 " 'Side Effects' Listing"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121017140826/http://www.lortel.org/LLA_archive/index.cfm?search_by=show&id=5740 |date=2012-10-17 }} lortel.org, accessed September 2, 2015</ref>

==Personal life==
Auburn currently resides in [[Manhattan]], [[New York (state)|New York]]. He has a wife and two daughters.
