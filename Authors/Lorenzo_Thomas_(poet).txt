'''Lorenzo Thomas''' (August 31, 1944 &ndash; July 4, 2005) was an [[American poet]] and [[critic]]. He was born in the [[Republic of Panama]] and grew up in [[New York City]], where his family immigrated in 1948. In 1973, Thomas moved to [[Houston]], [[Texas]]. Thomas had a two decade career as a professor at the [[University of Houston–Downtown]].<ref name=HoustonChronicle-Obit-2005>{{cite news|last1=Lanham|first1=Fritz|title=Deaths: Lorenzo Thomas, professor and poet|url=http://www.chron.com/news/houston-texas/article/Deaths-Lorenzo-Thomas-professor-and-poet-1674144.php|accessdate=15 March 2016|work=[[Houston Chronicle]]|date=5 July 2005}}</ref><ref name=HoustonChronicle-Editorial-ImportantWriter-2005>{{cite news|title=Editorials: Lorenzo Thomas: Houston loses an important resident writer|url=http://www.chron.com/opinion/editorials/article/Lorenzo-Thomas-Houston-loses-an-important-1933912.php|accessdate=15 March 2016|work=[[Houston Chronicle]]|date=6 July 2005}}</ref>

== Early life ==
Thomas was born in [[Panama]] to [[Afro-Caribbean]] parents. His father, a pharmacist, was from [[Saint Vincent and the Grenadines|Saint Vincent]] and his mother, Luzmilda, a community activist, was [[Jamaican peoples|Jamaican]], born in [[Costa Rica]].<ref name=Callaloo-InMemoriam-2006>{{cite journal|last1=Gery|first1=John|title=In Memoriam: Lorenzo Thomas (1944-2005)|journal=[[Callaloo (journal)|Callaloo]]|date=Winter 2006|volume=29|issue=1|pages=1–7|doi=10.1353/cal.2006.0053|url=http://scholarworks.uno.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1061&context=engl_facpubs|accessdate=15 March 2016}}</ref><ref name=Callaloo-Interview-1981>{{cite journal|last1=Rowell|first1=Charles H.|title=Between the Comedy of Matters and the Ritual Workings of Man: An Interview with Lorenzo Thomas|journal=[[Callaloo (journal)|Callaloo]]|date=February 1981|issue=11/13|pages=19|doi=10.2307/3043810|jstor=3043810}}</ref> Her sister Sadie Clemencia Dolphy was the mother of musician Eric Dolphy.<ref>"Eric Dolphy; A Musical Biography", Vladimir Simosko, 1996, Da Capo Press; https://vimeo.com/12369541</ref> As a child in NYC, he attended [[Duke Ellington Elementary School]] ([[P.S. 140]];)  [[Edgar D. Shimer Junior High School]] ([[P.S. 142]];) and [[Andrew Jackson High School (Queens, New York)|Andrew Jackson High School]].

Thomas was a graduate of [[Queens College]] in New York with a B.A. in English Literature, and minor in History & Communication Arts (Radio and Television.) He pursued graduate work towards an M.L.S. at the [[Pratt Institute]]. During his years in New York, he joined the [[Umbra poets|Umbra Workshop]], and was one of the youngest members. The Umbra Workshop drew young writers to the [[Lower East Side]] of New York City in search of their artistic voices. It served as a crucible for emerging black poets, among them [[Ishmael Reed]], [[David Henderson (poet)|David Henderson]] and [[Calvin C. Hernton]]. The workshop was one of the currents that fed the [[Black Arts Movement]] of the '60s and '70s, the first major African-American artistic movement after the [[Harlem Renaissance]].<ref name=CaliforniaNewsreel-FuriousFlower-Bio-1998>{{cite web|title=Furious Flower: African American Poetry, 1960-1995. A Facilitator's Guide: Lorenzo Thomas|url=http://www.newsreel.org/guides/furious/thomas.htm|website=[[California Newsreel]]|accessdate=15 March 2016|date=1998}}</ref>

==Naval service==

From 1968 to 1972 Thomas served in the U.S. Navy, attaining the rate of 2nd Class Petty Office (E-5) Radioman. He served in Vietnam (in-country); attended Navy schools for electronics, radio, and Vietnamese language; had experience as a platoon leader, radio and computer operator, master-at-arms, and supervisor of civilian employees. He was honorably discharged in 1972.

== Career ==
In 1973, Thomas moved to Houston as writer-in-residence at [[Texas Southern University]]. At [[Texas Southern University|Texas Southern]], he helped edit the journal ''Roots.'' Beginning in 1984 and for more than two decades as a professor of English at the [[University of Houston–Downtown]]. From 1973 to 1979 he served as Writer in Residence at [[Texas Southern University]], [[Florida A & M University]]; the State of Arkansas; and the state of Oklahoma.

Thomas also made important contributions to the study of [[African-American literature]].<ref name=TheNewJournal-Talking-2001>{{cite journal|title=Talking to Lorenzo Thomas (Virtually)|journal=The New Journal|date=2001|url=http://www.thenewjournal.com/html/interviews/thomas_interview.htm}}</ref>

In 2000, he published ''Extraordinary Measures: Afrocentric Modernism and 20th-Century American Poetry'', his overview of the work of [[James Fenton]] and [[Amiri Baraka]], among others.<ref name=HoustonChronicle-Obit-2005 />

== Death ==
Thomas died in July 2005 at Texas Medical Center Hospice from [[emphysema]].
