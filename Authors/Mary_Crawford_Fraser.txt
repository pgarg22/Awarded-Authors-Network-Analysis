'''Mary Crawford Fraser''' (April 8, 1851 – 1922), usually known as '''Mrs. Hugh Fraser''', was a writer noted for her various memoirs and historical novels.<ref>{{cite magazine|title=FRASER, Mrs. Hugh|magazine=Who's Who|year=1907|volume= 59|page=636|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=yEcuAAAAYAAJ&pg=PA636}}</ref>

==Early life==
[[File:Portrait of Mary Crawford Fraser.jpg|thumb|right]]
Mary Crawford was born in Italy on April 8, 1851.<ref>{{cite book |author=Hugh Cortazzi |author-link=Hugh Cortazzi |year=1982 |title=A Diplomat's Wife in Japan |publisher=Weatherhill |pages=xiii| isbn=0-8348-0172-8}}</ref> She was the daughter of American sculptor [[Thomas Crawford (sculptor)|Thomas Crawford]] and Louisa Cutler Ward.  She was sister to novelist [[Francis Marion Crawford]] and the niece of [[Julia Ward Howe]] (the American abolitionist, social activist, and poet most famous as the author of "[[The Battle Hymn of the Republic]]").<ref name="FMC1897">{{cite news |title=F. MARION CRAWFORD. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1897/12/19/117909056.pdf |access-date=24 February 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=December 19, 1897}}</ref>  After her father's death in 1857, her mother remarried to Luther Terry, with whom she had Mary's half-sister, [[Margaret Terry Chanler|Margaret Ward Terry]], who later became the wife of [[Winthrop Astor Chanler]].<ref name="MrsWACObit1952">{{cite news |title=MRS. W.A. CHANLER, AUTHOR, MUSICIAN; Niece of Julia Ward Howe and the Half-Sister of F. Marion Crawford Dies at 91 |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1952/12/20/84380058.pdf |access-date=24 February 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=December 20, 1952 |language=en}}</ref>

Her father died when she was young, and she was raised in Italy, as well as in England and [[New Jersey]]. She was educated at a girls' boarding school run by the Sewell sisters, famous for their contribution to Victorian educational literature, on the [[Isle of Wight]].<ref>Mrs Hugh Fraser, ''A Diplomatist's Wife in Many Lands'', 1911</ref>  The school received a number of pupils whose parents lived or worked in the British colonies and the sisters also took their charges on a number of foreign trips.<ref name="ReferenceA">Fraser, ''A Diplomatist's Wife in Many Lands'', 1911</ref>  She credits the school with providing her with many of the skills necessary to be successful as a diplomat's wife, including proper correspondence and social graces.<ref name="ReferenceA"/>

==Career==
As the wife of British diplomat, she followed her husband to his postings in [[Peking]], Vienna, Rome, [[Santiago, Chile|Santiago]], and Tokyo. In Rome in 1884, over the opposition of her mother, she converted to [[Roman Catholic|Catholicism]].<ref>{{cite book |author=Jozef Rogala|year=2001 |title=''A Collector's Guide to Books on Japan in English''|publisher=Routledge |pages=xiii| isbn=1-873410-90-5}}</ref>

In 1889, her husband Hugh Fraser was posted to Japan as "[[Plenipotentiary|Minister Plenipotentiary and Envoy Extraordinary]] (head of the British Legation) to Japan—a diplomatic ranking just below that of full Ambassador. before the establishment of full and equal relations between Britain and Japan which Fraser was, in fact, negotiating. A month before the signing of the final treaty, her husband died suddenly in 1894, leaving her a widow after twenty years of marriage.<ref name="1904Japan">{{cite news |title=Mrs. Hugh Fraser on Japan. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1904/03/26/120284845.pdf |access-date=24 February 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=March 26, 1904}}</ref>

Still under her married name of Mrs. Hugh Fraser, she was the author of ''Palladia'' (1896), ''The Looms of Time'' (1898), ''The Stolen Emperor'' (1904), ''The Satanist'' (1912, with J. I. Stahlmann, the pseudonym of one of her sons, John Crawford Fraser)<ref>''Punch'' – Volume 142 1912 – Page 125 "I suspect that Mrs. Hugh Fraser is responsible for the plot of ''The Satanist'' (Hutchinson), Mr. J. I. Stahlmann for the curious information, and Providence for the very happy combination of the two".</ref> [[Peter Haining (author)|Haining]] (1971)<ref>Peter Haining ''A circle of witches: an anthology of Victorian witchcraft'' 1971 p220 "THE SATANIST Mrs Hugh Fraser Mrs Hugh Fraser (1864–1925). With the death of Queen Victoria and the end of her long and restrictive reign, a great many aspects of the social climate changed : not the least of these being in the world of literature. Of course, there had been the occasional outspoken writer... To close, then, I have selected the following story of Satanism with its quite chilling scenes and vivid descriptions of a black mass. "The Satanist", along with several other stories of the same period set the standards for today's occult fiction and can be seen mirrored in the tales of August Derleth, Dennis Wheatley and, ..."</ref> considered that Fraser's "The Satanist" was one of the stories of the period which set the standards for 1960s occult fiction and is reflected in the stories of [[August Derleth]] and [[Dennis Wheatley]].

==Personal life==
In 1874, she was married to [[Hugh Fraser (diplomat)|Hugh Fraser]], son of Sir John Fraser and Lady Charlotte Fraser.<ref name="Walford1864">{{cite book |last1=Walford |first1=Edward |title=The County Families of the United Kingdom, Or Royal Manual of the Titled and Untitled Aristocracy of Great Britain and Ireland. 2. Ed. Greatly Enl |date=1864 |publisher=Hardwicke |page=388 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=n9pv_1xLploC&pg=PA388 |access-date=24 February 2019 |language=en}}</ref>  Hugh, through his paternal grandmother, Isabel ([[née]] Skinner) Fraser, was a descendant of General [[Cortlandt Skinner]] and [[Stephanus Van Cortlandt]].<ref name="Burke's1847">{{cite book |title=Burke's Genealogical and Heraldic History of the Landed Gentry |date=1847 |publisher=H. Colburn |page=1362 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=0NEKAAAAYAAJ&pg=PA1362 |access-date=24 February 2019 |language=en}}</ref> Together, they were the parents of two sons:<ref name="Cortazzi2013">{{cite book |last1=Cortazzi |first1=Hugh |title=Britain and Japan: Biographical Portraits |date=2013 |publisher=Routledge |isbn=9781136641404 |page=42 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=OWQH2mxsqY0C&pg=PA42 |access-date=24 February 2019 |language=en}}</ref>

* John Fraser (1875–1931)<ref name="Cortazzi2013"/>
* Hugh Crawford Fraser (1876–1915), who became a [[Second lieutenant]] with the 3rd Battalion, the [[Royal Fusiliers]] in 1897.<ref name="1897HCF">{{cite news |title=3rd Battalion, the Royal -Fusiliers (City of London YEOMANRY CALVALRY. Regiment) |url=https://www.thegazette.co.uk/London/issue/26869/page/3636/data.pdf |access-date=24 February 2019 |work=[[The London Gazette]] |date=2 July 1897}}</ref>

Mary's husband died in Tokyo in 1894.  Mary died twenty-eight years later in 1922.
