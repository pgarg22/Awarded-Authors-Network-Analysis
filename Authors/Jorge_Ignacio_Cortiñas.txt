'''Jorge Ignacio Cortiñas''' is an American playwright and director. He first studied playwriting with [[Octavio Solis]], [[Cherríe Moraga]] and [[María Irene Fornés]]. His numerous awards include fellowships from the New York Foundation for the Arts (NYFA)<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.nyfa.org/Content/Show/Past-Fellows/ |title=Past Fellows | publisher=NYFA |website=nyfa.org |access-date=July 23, 2018}}</ref> and the Guggenheim Foundation.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.gf.org/fellows/all-fellows/jorge-ignacio-cortinas/ |title=Fellows | publisher=Guggenheim Foundation |website=gf.org |access-date=July 23, 2018}}</ref>  He received an MFA from [[Brown University]] and lives in [[New York City]].

==Plays==
Possibly his highest profile play to date is ''Blind Mouth Singing'', which was produced by the [[Chicago]] based [[Teatro Vista]] in 2005 <ref>{{cite web|title = chicagocritic.com|url = http://www.chicagocritic.com/html/blind_mouth_singing.html?ref=theater}}</ref> and by the National Asian American Theater Company (NAATCO) in New York in 2007. The NAATCO production was designated a Critic's Pick by ''The New York Times''. Writing for that newspaper [[Neil Genzlinger]] called the play "strange and beautiful".<ref>{{cite news|title = The New York Times |url = http://theater2.nytimes.com/2007/09/22/theater/reviews/22blin.html?ref=theater | first=Neil | last=Genzlinger | date=2007-09-22 | accessdate=2010-05-07}}</ref>

In 2010 ''Blind Mouth Singing'' was translated and produced in Havana.<ref>{{cite news|title = The New York Times |url = https://www.nytimes.com/2010/07/25/world/americas/25cuba.html | first=Victoria | last=Burnett | date=2010-07-25 | accessdate=2012-12-28}}</ref>

In 2012 the author directed a production of his play ''Bird in the Hand'' at [[Theater for the New City]]. That play was produced by Fulcrum Theater and was also designated as a Critic's Pick by ''The New York Times''.<ref>{{cite news|title = The New York Times |url = http://www.theater.nytimes.com/2012/09/14/theater/reviews/jorge-ignacio-cortinass-bird-in-the-hand-at-the-new-city.html | first=Catherine | last=Rampell | date=2012-09-14 | accessdate=2012-12-28}}</ref>

His 2017 play, ''Recent Alien Abductions'' received its premiere at the Humana Festival of New American Plays at the Actors Theater of Louisville directed by [[Les Waters]]. Louisville's weekly cultural newspaper, The LEO Weekly, called it "an extraordinary play".<ref>{{cite news |last1=Rosen |first1=Marty |title=Do not miss 'Recent Alien Abductions' and 'A Doll House' |url=https://www.leoweekly.com/2017/03/recent-alien-abductions/ |accessdate=July 23, 2018 |publisher=LEO Weekly |date=March 22, 2017}}</ref>

His short play ''Look! A Latino'' was produced by [[Ma-Yi Theater Company]] in New York in October 2004.<ref>{{cite news|title = Variety|url = https://www.variety.com/review/VE1117925290.html?categoryid=33&cs=1&query=%22savage+acts%22?ref=theater | first=Marilyn | last=Stasio | date=2004-10-17}}</ref> The play was presented during an evening of shorts that also included plays by Kia Corthron, [[Han Ong]], and Sung Rno. The title of the play was inspired by [[Frantz Fanon]]'s well known essay, "The Fact of Blackness" in which Fanon recounts being hailed by a French girl with the phrase, "Look! A Negro!" (see <ref name="Black Skin, White Masks">{{cite book | last= Fanon |first= Frantz |title= [[Black Skin, White Masks]] |publisher= Grove Press, Inc. |year= 1967 |isbn= 0-394-17990-0 }}</ref> ).  ''Look! A Latino'' was subsequently published in the anthology ''Savage Stage: Plays by Ma-Yi Theater Company'' (see <ref name="Savage Stage: Plays by Ma-Yi Theater Company">{{cite book |last= Barrios-LeBlanc, ed. |first= Joi Barrios |title= Savage Stage:Plays by Ma-Yi Theater Company |publisher= [[Ma-Yi Theater Company]] |year= 2006 }}</ref> ) and by Playscripts, Inc. (see <ref name="Great Short Plays: Volume 7">{{cite book |last= Cortiñas|first= Jorge Ignacio |title= Great Short Plays: Volume 7 |publisher= Playscripts, Inc. |year= 2009 |display-authors=etal}}</ref> ).

==Politics==
Before he began writing plays, Cortiñas worked as a community organizer for a variety of leftist or progressive causes. Most notably, he was a high-profile participant in AIDS activist groups,<ref>{{cite news |title= Activists: It's Time to Act-Up Against AIDS |newspaper= Miami Herald |date= 1990-07-01 }}</ref> including the SF based group [[Proyecto ContraSIDA por Vida]], and other organizations seeking to advance the rights of immigrants and [[queer]] Latino organizations.<ref>{{cite book |last= Rodríguez |first= Juana María |title= Queer Latinidad: Identity Practices, Discursive Spaces |publisher= [[NYU Press]] |year= 2003 |isbn= 0814775500}}</ref>
