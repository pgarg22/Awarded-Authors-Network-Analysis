'''Frank Michael Howson''' (born 1952)<ref name="APRA">{{cite web|title=Australasian Performing Right Association |publisher=[[Australasian Performing Right Association|APRA]] |url=http://www.apra.com.au/site/public/searchworksresult.stm |accessdate=14 September 2007 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20070828224726/http://www.apra.com.au/site/public/searchworksresult.stm |archivedate=28 August 2007 }}</ref><ref name="ASCAP">{{cite web |title=The American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers |publisher=[[American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers|ASCAP]] |url=http://www.ascap.com/ace/search.cfm?requesttimeout=300 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20000510070043/http://www.ascap.com/ace/search.cfm?requesttimeout=300 |url-status=dead |archive-date=10 May 2000 |accessdate=15 September 2007 |df=dmy-all }}</ref> [[Melbourne]]) has had a career in entertainment. He directed ''[[Flynn (film)|Flynn]]'' (1996) on the early life of [[Errol Flynn]] and ''[[Hunting (film)|Hunting]]'' (1991). Howson, with Peter Boyle, helped establish [[Boulevard Films]] which produced thirteen films from ''[[Boulevard of Broken Dreams (film)|Boulevard of Broken Dreams]]'' (1988) to ''Flynn''; besides producing for Boulevard Films, Howson often wrote scripts and directed.

==Early years==
Howson was born in Melbourne started in show business when he was seven.<ref name="IMDb">{{cite web|title= Internet Movie Database entry on Frank Howson (I)|website= [[Internet Movie Database|IMDb]] |url= https://www.imdb.com/name/nm0398271 |accessdate= 14 September 2007 }}</ref> After leaving school, [[Christian Brothers College, St. Kilda]] (1963–1967), Howson's first job was with Melbourne radio station [[3UZ]] as office boy. Eventually promoted to panel operator, he worked on John McMahon's popular weekly show Radio Auditions (see [[3UZ]]). Whenever not enough acts showed up, young Frank was summoned to perform under made up names. During this period Howson was nicknamed 'Magical Frank'<ref name="3RRR">{{cite web|title=Film Buffs' Forecast (2 July 2005) podcast |publisher=[[Paul Harris (film critic)|Paul Harris]] and Brett Cropley of radio [[3RRR]]|url= http://www.boxcutters.net/bcrss/?p=94 |accessdate=14 September 2007 }}</ref> when asked to perform on a pilot for a TV talent show by 3UZ's [[Jimmy Hannan]].

===Recording artist===
DJ [[Stan Rofe]] signed Howson to a recording deal and produced his first single: "[[Seventeen Ain't Young]]" (written by [[Jeff Barry]])<ref name="APRA" /> / '' Hide and seek'' ([[Richie Adams]], Mark Barkan)<ref name="APRA" /> performed by "Frankie Howson" (1969)<ref name="NFSA">{{cite web| title=''The Sixties: Australian rock & pop recordings, 1964–1969'' PDF p 134| publisher=Ross Laird (1999), accessed from [[National Film and Sound Archive]] on-line| url=http://www.nfsa.afc.gov.au/docs/collectionguide_thesixties1964-1969.pdf| accessdate=14 September 2007| url-status=dead| archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20080513004404/http://www.nfsa.afc.gov.au/docs/collectionguide_thesixties1964-1969.pdf| archivedate=13 May 2008| df=dmy-all}}</ref> who was seventeen during recording, it became a Top 40 hit in Melbourne.

Howson released two other singles ''This Night'' (Howson, Kenneth Firth, Miller)<ref name="APRA" /> and 1983 ''The Heart Is A Lonely Hunter'' (John Capek, Howson).<ref name="APRA" /><ref name="OzLib">{{cite web |title= Australian Library Collections |publisher= Libraries Australia, National Library of Australia |url= http://librariesaustralia.nla.gov.au/apps/kss |accessdate= 15 September 2007 }}</ref>

===Music publisher===
Howson was manager of the Australian branch of German-based I.C. Records to publish musical works (including his own) distributed by [[EMI]]. He co-wrote [[John Paul Young]]'s 1984 album ''One Foot in Front''. He signed [[New romantic]] band [[Pseudo Echo]] and co-wrote their songs '’Autumnal Park'’ and '’Destination Unknown'’ with Tony Lugton,<ref name="ASCAP" /> they later had a 1987 No. 1 hit in Australia with their version of ''Funky Town''. I.C. Records scored 3 national hits in Australia in its first 12 months of operation. Howson and Peter Boyle were Executive Producers<ref name="OzLib" /> for [[John Paul Young]]'s 1983 hit ''Soldier of Fortune'' (John Capek, Marc Jordan)<ref name="APRA" /> reaching No. 15.

Howson and [[Allan Zavod]] wrote ''Time Can't Keep Us Apart''<ref name="APRA" /> which won the 1987 Asian Popular Song contest performed by [[Kate Ceberano]]<ref>{{cite web | title=Kate Ceberano wins 1987 Asian Popular Song Contest | publisher= Ralph Carr Management | url= http://www.std.com.au/pdfs/kateCeberano.pdf | archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20110706132511/http://www.std.com.au/pdfs/kateCeberano.pdf | url-status= dead | archive-date= 6 July 2011 | accessdate= 20 September 2007 }}</ref> to an estimated TV audience of 500 million.

===Theatre Works===
Frank Howson began his career as stage actor, singer and dancer, and appeared in 21 major productions (including the Australian production of ''[[Oliver!]]'') before turning 21.<ref name="AusStage">{{ cite web | title= AusStage – Gateway to the Australian Performing Arts | publisher= AusStage (contributor Frank Howson) | url= http://www.ausstage.edu.au/indexdrilldown.jsp?xcid=59&f_contrib_id=245201|accessdate= 20 September 2007 }}</ref> In the early 1970s, Howson met fellow actor Barry Ferrier while they were both appearing in the original Australian production of ''[[Jesus Christ Superstar]]'', and the two men subsequently collaborated on a number of theatre-related projects.  The first of these was a children's musical entitled ''The Faraway Land of Magical Frank'', which was produced at the Toral Theatre in Melbourne in January 1976.<ref>{{cite web | title= Australian Stage Database cites ''The Faraway Land of Magical Frank'' performed at the Total Theatre as reviewed by Sally White, (1 February 1976), ''The Age'' p2| publisher= AusStage |url= http://www.ausstage.edu.au/custom/files/2004531046932/agedb.xls |format=XLS | accessdate= 20 September 2007 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20070926224953/http://www.ausstage.edu.au/custom/files/2004531046932/agedb.xls |archivedate=26 September 2007}}</ref> Later that year, Howson and Ferrier released a concept recording of a follow-up children's musical, entitled ''The Boy who dared to dream'', performed by [[Trevor White (singer)|Trevor White]], pop singer [[Mark Holden]] and actors [[John Waters (actor)|John Waters]] and [[Tommy Dysart]].  The first staged production of the show (featuring some of the actors from the concept recording) was mounted in Melbourne in January 1978, with a second production in May 1981.<ref name="OzStage">{{ cite web | title= Gateway to the Australian Performing Arts | publisher= AusStage (contributor Frank Howson) | url= http://www.ausstage.edu.au/default.jsp?xcid=27 |accessdate= 20 September 2007 }}</ref>

Frank Howson went on to create two more children's musicals, without Ferrier's involvement: ''Aladdin and his Wonderful Lamp'' (music by Robert Gavin, 1981) and ''Sinbad the Sailor: The Last Adventure'' (music by Ian McKean, 1982).  Both were initially staged at the Alexander Theatre at [[Monash University]]..

==Filmmaking==
[[Boulevard Films]] was a production company on Errol St, [[North Melbourne, Victoria|North Melbourne]].<ref>{{ cite web | title= ''Actors Feature Film Award 1979'' | publisher= [[Australian Industrial Relations Commission]] | url= http://www.airc.gov.au/consolidated_awards/at/at817364/asframe.html | accessdate= 20 September 2007 }}</ref>

Boulevard's notable productions include:
* 1988 ''[[Boulevard of Broken Dreams (film)|Boulevard of Broken Dreams]]'' (Howson screenwriter, cameo) won two [[Australian Film Institute Awards|AFI Awards]]:  [[John Waters (actor)|John Waters]] "Best Actor" and [[Kym Gyngell]] "Best Supporting"; nominated for five others including "Best Picture".<ref name="IMDb" /> Its soundtrack (with nine Howson co-compositions)<ref name="APRA" /> was nominated for an ARIA Award for Best Soundtrack Album of the Year.
* 1989 ''''[[Heaven Tonight (film)|Heaven Tonight]]'''' (Howson co-wrote the screenplay as well as songs for this film. It was nominated for an AFI Award. It starred [[John Waters (actor)|John Waters]], [[Kim Gyngell]], [[Rebecca Gilling]], [[Guy Pearce]] and [[Sean Scully]].
* 1990 ''Friday on My Mind'' with [[Guy Pearce]]
* 1990 ''[[What the Moon Saw]]'' (Howson screenwriter, cameo) won an AFI Award for Rose Chong "Best Costume Design".<ref name="IMDb" /> It revolves around a child absorbed with the story of ''Sinbad''.<ref>{{cite web | title= IMDb plot summary for ''What the Moon Saw'' |website= [[Internet Movie Database|IMDb]] | url= https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0100915/plotsummary | accessdate=21 September 2007 }}</ref>
* 1991 ''[[Hunting (film)|Hunting]]'' starred [[John Savage (actor)|John Savage]], [[Kerry Armstrong]] (nominated for AFI [[AACTA Award for Best Actress in a Leading Role|Best Actress]]) and [[Guy Pearce]]; Howson wrote and directed.<ref name="IMDb" />
* 1996 ''[[Flynn (film)|Flynn]]'' starred Pearce, [[Steven Berkoff]], [[Claudia Karvan]] and Savage; Howson directed and co-wrote.<ref name="IMDb" /> Problems occurred while making this film with Boulevard Films collapsing acrimoniously.<ref name="3RRR" />

Some Boulevard Films listed at Internet Movie Database did not have a general release, e.g. ''[[The Final Stage]]'' (1995)<ref>{{cite web | title= IMDb ''The Final Stage'' entry| url= https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0462677 |accessdate= 20 September 2007 }}</ref> had its 'World Premiere' ten years later at [[Melbourne Underground Film Festival]] 2005.<ref name="MUFF2005">{{cite web|title= Melbourne Underground Film Festival 2005 Director's statement|publisher= [[Richard Wolstencroft]]|url= http://www.muff.com.au/2005/content/directors.html|accessdate= 14 September 2007|archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20070927202156/http://www.muff.com.au/2005/content/directors.html|archive-date= 27 September 2007|url-status= dead}}</ref>

Howson moved to Hollywood in 1997 after a falling out with his business partner and the collapse of Boulevard Films.<ref name="Age2006">{{cite news|title=Something to shout about |publisher=[[The Age]] Lawrence Money, Bridie Smith (20 July 2006)|url=http://www.theage.com.au/news/national/something-to-shout-about/2006/07/19/1153166455636.html |accessdate= 14 September 2007 | location=Melbourne | date=20 July 2006}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Howson has been married three times:
# [[Lisa Waters]] ([[floruit|fl.]] 1976)
# [[Lynn Murphy]] (1983–1999) Their son, Oliver Howson was born in 1991.
# [[Terri Garber]] (2001–2002)

Howson has been debilitated, at times, by [[Spasmodic Dysphonia]] which affects his voice; it is periodically mitigated by [[Botulinum toxin|Botox]] injections into his larynx.<ref>[http://www.theage.com.au/national/melbourne-life/showbiz-veteran-bounces-back-from-brink-20110522-1eyso.html Lawrence Money, 'Showbiz veteran bounces back from brink', ''The Age'' 23 May 2011] accessed 17 October 2012</ref>

His gravelly voice can be heard in a 2005 radio interview with [[Paul Harris (film critic)|Paul Harris]] and Brett Cropley of [[3RRR]] on ''Film Buffs' Forecast''.<ref name="3RRR" />

[[John-Michael Howson|John Michael Howson]] is his cousin.

==Subsequent career==
===Hollywood Hiatus===
Howson was on the Crystal Prix Jury for the 1997 Cannes Film Festival. From 1997 to 2005 Howson worked and lived in Hollywood. He served on the board of directors for the Starlight Children's Foundation of California.

''Mr. Insincere'', (written / performed by Howson)<ref name="APRA" /> appears in the Disney movie ''[[Burn Hollywood Burn]]'' (1998), starring [[Eric Idle]] (as [[Alan Smithee]]) and [[Ryan O'Neal]]; with [[Whoopi Goldberg]], [[Jackie Chan]], [[Sylvester Stallone]], and [[Robert Evans (producer)|Robert Evans]].<ref>{{cite web | title=IMDb entry on ''An Alan Smithee Film: Burn Hollywood Burn'' (1998)|website= [[Internet Movie Database|IMDb]] |url= https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0118577/fullcredits#cast |accessdate= 21 September 2007 }}</ref> Howson was asked by [[Steven Berkoff]] to direct the TV version of Berkoff's international hit theatre show ''[[Shakespeare's Villains]]'' (2002).<ref name="IMDb" />

Recently Howson has completed writing his memoirs on the film industry<ref name="3RRR" /> ''A Life in the Circus'', and wrote songs for [[Steve Housden]]'s ([[Little River Band]] lead guitarist) solo album.

===MUFF & More===
[[Richard Wolstencroft]] formed [[Melbourne Underground Film Festival|MUFF]] in 2000 and appointed Howson as President of the Jury.<ref name="3RRR" /> Boulevard Films' ''The Intruder'' (directed by Wolstencroft) and ''The Final Stage'' (directed by Howson) were both presented at the 2005 festival.<ref name="3RRR" /> The following year saw Howson's film ''Remembering Nigel'' tie with ''Penny'' by John King for MUFF's 2006 ''Best Short Film'' award.<ref name="BadLit">{{cite web|title= Underground Film Journal 'Even More MUFF'|publisher=Mike Everleth (22 July 2006)|url= http://www.undergroundfilmjournal.com/even-more-muff/|accessdate=14 September 2007}}</ref>
He acted and was a script editor in a Wolstencroft film, ''[[The Beautiful and Damned (film)|The Beautiful and Damned]]''.

Howson currently serves as a board member of [[Open Channel]], a non-profit organisation that supports young filmmakers. Recently Howson was invited to direct the premiere production of the short play ''[[The Replacement Son]]'' for the ''[[Short and Sweet (festival)|Short and Sweet]]'' festival at Melbourne's Arts Centre in December '07.

Some excerpts from Howson's film ''[[Flynn (film)|Flynn]]'' can be seen in the new documentary on the life of Errol Flynn, ''[[The Tasmanian Devil]]''.

The new release book ''[[The Actors' Handbook]]'' by Marnie Hill, published by AT2, contains an interview with him.

Howson in 2012 and 2013 wrote and directed two sell-out seasons of the musical ''"Genesis To Broadway"'' at Chapel Off Chapel, Melbourne. Cast member Fem Belling was nominated for a Green Room Award for Best Performance in a Musical.
