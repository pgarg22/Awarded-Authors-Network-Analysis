'''Ron Padgett''' (born June 17, 1942, [[Tulsa, Oklahoma]]) is an American [[poet]], essayist, fiction writer, [[translator]], and a member of the [[New York School (art)|New York School]]. ''Great Balls of Fire'', Padgett's first full-length collection of poems, was published in 1969. He won a 2009 [[Shelley Memorial Award]].<ref>[http://www.coffeehousepress.org/authors/ron-padgett/#author-awards "About the Author: Ron Padgett,"] Coffee House Press. Accessed May 31, 2014.</ref> In 2018, he won the [[Robert Frost Medal|Frost Medal]] from the [[Poetry Society of America]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/awards/frost_and_shelley/frost_winners/|title=Frost Medalists - Poetry Society of America|website=www.poetrysociety.org|access-date=2018-10-23}}</ref> 

== Early life and education ==
Padgett’s father was a bootlegger in Tulsa, Oklahoma.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=https://www.poetscritics.org/2017/03/13/its-alright-students-not-to-write-what-ron-padgetts-poetry-can-teach-us-by-jeremy-over-writing-in-education-71-2017/|title="'It's alright, students, not to write': What Ron Padgett's Poetry Can Teach Us," by Jeremy Over, Writing In Education 71 (2017)|date=2017-03-13|website=A Collective History of American Poetry and Poetics|language=en-US|access-date=2019-08-14}}</ref> He influenced many of Padgett's works, particularly in the writer's taste for independence and a willingness to deviate from rules, even his own.<ref name=":1" /> This would later be described as a stubborn streak of boyishness, allowing a wry innocence in his poetry.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2015/07/09/books/review-looking-inward-in-poetry-books-from-ron-padgett-and-nick-flynn.html|title=Review: Looking Inward in Poetry Books From Ron Padgett and Nick Flynn|last=Gordinier|first=Jeff|date=2015-07-08|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-09-07|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> 

Padgett started writing poetry at the age of 13.<ref name=":2">{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=9DteqBjjvLIC&pg=PT381&dq=ron+padgett+bootlegger#q=ron%20padgett%20bootlegger|title=Good Poems for Hard Times|date=2006-08-29|publisher=Penguin|isbn=9781440684494|language=en}}</ref> In an interview, the poet said that he was inspired to write when a girl he had a big crush on did not return his affection.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.townandcountrymag.com/leisure/arts-and-culture/news/a8972/ron-padgett-poet-paterson/|title=Meet the Poet Behind Adam Driver's New Film 'Paterson'|last=Lopez|first=Natalina|date=2016-12-29|website=Town & Country|language=en-US|access-date=2019-09-07}}</ref> In high school, Padgett became interested in visual arts while continuing to write poetry. He befriended [[Joe Brainard]], the visual artist who also became a well-known writer.<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|url=https://believermag.com/logger/ron-padgett/|title=An Interview with Poet Ron Padgett|website=Believer Magazine|language=en-US|access-date=2019-08-14}}</ref> They, with fellow [[Central High School (Tulsa, Oklahoma)|Central High]] student [[Dick Gallup]], co-founded the [[avant-garde]] literary journal ''The White Dove Review''. Padgett and Gallup solicited work for ''The'' ''White Dove'' from [[Black Mountain poets|Black Mountain]] and [[Beat Movement]] writers such as [[Allen Ginsberg]], [[Jack Kerouac]], [[LeRoi Jones]], [[Paul Blackburn (poet)|Paul Blackburn]], [[Gilbert Sorrentino]], and [[Robert Creeley]]. After five issues, Padgett, on his way to college, retired the ''White Dove.'' 

In 1960, Padgett left Tulsa to study at Columbia College fin New York City. At that time he was interested in Pound, Rimbaud, the Black Mountain poets, and the Beats<ref name=":3" /> but soon he fell under the spell of the [[New York School (art)|New York School]],<ref>Kline, Joshua. [http://www.thislandpress.com/11/12/2010/white-dove-review/ ''The White Dove Review: How a Group of Tulsa Teens Created a Literary Legend''] This Land Press (2010).</ref> particularly the poetry of [[Frank O'Hara]], [[John Ashbery]], [[James Schuyler]], and [[Kenneth Koch]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.college.columbia.edu/cct/issue/summer17/article/poet-laureate-paterson|title=The Poet Laureate of Paterson|last=Meyer|first=Eugene|date=2017-07-06|website=Columbia College Today|language=en|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-09-06}}</ref> In an interview, Padgett said that he went to Columbia partly because Ginsberg and Kerouac had gone there.<ref name=":3" /> After receiving his B.A. (1964), Padgett briefly studied [[creative writing]] at [[Wagner College]] with [[Kay Boyle]], [[Howard Nemerov]], and Koch. On a [[Fulbright Program|Fulbright Fellowship]]<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/educational-magazines/padgett-ron-1942|title=Padgett, Ron 1942- {{!}} Encyclopedia.com|website=www.encyclopedia.com|access-date=2019-08-26}}</ref> (Paris, 1965-66) he studied 20th-century French poetry.

== Career ==
From 1968 to 1969 Padgett was a workshop leader for [[Poetry Project|The Poetry Project]] at [[St. Mark's Church in-the-Bowery]] in New York City. With David Shapiro, Padgett co-edited ''An Anthology of New York Poets'', published by Random House in 1970. He founded ''The Poetry Project Newsletter'' in 1972. Padgett also worked in poet-in-the-school programs nationally from 1969 to 1976. After directing The Poetry Project for two and a half years, he became the publications director of [[Teachers & Writers Collaborative]]<ref name=":5">{{Cite web|url=https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/ron-padgett|title=Ron Padgett|last=Foundation|first=Poetry|date=2019-08-26|website=Poetry Foundation|language=en|access-date=2019-08-26}}</ref> (1980-2000), where he also edited ''The Teachers & Writers Collaborative Newsletter''.

Padgett was a cofounder, publisher, and editor of Full Court Press from 1973 to 1988, bringing out books by Ginsberg, Brainard, O'Hara, Edwin Denby, Tom Veitch, William S. Burroughs, Larry Fagin, Philippe Soupault, John Godfrey, and others. At the same time, he lectured and taught at educational institutions, including [[Atlantic Center for the Arts]], Brooklyn College, and [[Columbia University]]. He also hosted a poetry radio series and the designer of computer writing games. Padgett's [https://beinecke.library.yale.edu/article/ron-padgett-papers papers] are held by the Beinecke Rare Book and Manuscript Library at Yale University.

== Poetry ==
Padgett is the author of more than twenty poetry collections, including ''Great Balls of Fire'' (1969, reissued 1990); ''You Never Know'' (2001); ''How to Be Perfect'' (2007); ''How Long'' (2011); and ''Collected Poems'' (2013). Seven of Padgett's poems are featured in [[Jim Jarmusch]]'s 2016 film [[Paterson (film)|''Paterson,'']]<ref>Lopez, N., [http://www.townandcountrymag.com/leisure/arts-and-culture/news/a8972/ron-padgett-poet-paterson/ "Meet the Poet Behind Adam Driver's New Film ''Paterson''"], ''Town & Country'', Dec 29, 2016.</ref> including three written expressly for the film. Like Padgett, Jarmusch studied poetry under Kenneth Koch at Columbia University.<ref name=":6">{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/01/26/nyregion/how-the-poet-ron-padgett-spends-his-sundays.html|title=How the Poet Ron Padgett Spends His Sundays|date=2017-01-26|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-08-21|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>

Padgett collaborated with poet [[Ted Berrigan]] and artists [[Jim Dine]], [[George Schneeman]], Bertrand Dorny, [[Trevor Winkfield]], and [[Alex Katz]], along with Joe Brainard.

== Other works ==
Padgett is also the author of nonfiction works, including ''Blood Work: Selected Prose'' (1993), ''Ted: A Personal Memoir of Ted Berrigan'' (1993), ''Creative Reading'' (1997), and ''The Straight Line: Writing on Poetry and Poets'' (2000), ''Oklahoma Tough: My Father, King of the Tulsa Bootleggers'' (2003), and ''Joe: A Memoir of Joe Brainard'' (2004).<ref name=":5" /> Padgett’s novella ''Motor Maids across the Continent'' appeared in 2017 from Song Cave. His numerous works on education and writing include ''The Teachers & Writers Handbook of Poetic Forms'' (editor), ''The Teachers & Writers Guide to Walt Whitman'' (editor), and ''Educating the Imagination'' (co-editor). He was also the editor of the three-volume reference work, ''World Poets'' (2000).<ref name=":4">[http://www.poets.org/poet.php/prmPID/614 "Ron Padgett"], Poets.org</ref> 

Padgett also translated [[French language|French]] poets [[Blaise Cendrars]], [[Max Jacob]], [[Pierre Reverdy]], [[Valery Larbaud]], and [[Guillaume Apollinaire]].<ref name=":5" /> Book-length collections of his own work have been translated into French, Spanish, Portuguese, Polish, Faroese, German, Finnish, Norwegian, and Italian. 

==Awards and honors==
His grants, fellowships. and awards include a Guggenheim (1986), a grant from the [[Foundation for Contemporary Arts]] (1996),<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.foundationforcontemporaryarts.org/recipients?utf8=%E2%9C%93&year=1996&grant_discipline_id=&grant_type_id=|title=Grant Recipients :: Foundation for Contemporary Arts|website=www.foundationforcontemporaryarts.org|access-date=2019-08-20}}</ref> the French Ministry of Culture and Communication (2001), the Civitella Ranieri Foundation, and the [[Academy of American Poets]] (2016). His book ''How Long'' was a finalist for the Pulitzer Prize in 2012<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/entertainment/books/tracy-k-smith-this-weeks-pulitzer-winning-poet/2012/04/17/gIQAD4vtOT_story.html|title=Book World: Tracy K. Smith's 2012 Pulitzer-winning poems are worth a read|last=Jollimore|first=Troy|date=April 17, 2012|website=Washington Post|language=en|access-date=2019-08-21}}</ref> and his ''Collected Poems'' won the [[Los Angeles Times Book Prize|L.A. Times Book Prize]] in 2013.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.latimes.com/about/pressreleases/la-mediagroup_04112014-story.html|title=2013 Los Angeles Times Book Prize Winners Announced|date=2014-04-11|website=Los Angeles Times|language=en-US|access-date=2019-08-20}}</ref> He was also the recipient of grants and awards for his translations, which include those given by the National Endowment for the Arts, the New York State Council on the Arts, and Columbia University’s Translation Center.<ref name=":4" />

== Personal life ==
Padgett and his wife, Patricia Padgett, who also grew up in Tulsa, have lived in the same [[East Village, Manhattan|East Village]] apartment since 1967.<ref name=":6" /> They also have a rustic retreat in [[Vermont]] where they spend their summers.<ref name=":7">{{Cite web|title=Ron Padgett Biography|url=http://famouspoetsandpoems.com/poets/ron_padgett/biography|access-date=2019-09-06|website=famouspoetsandpoems.com}}</ref> The couple's son Wayne was born in 1966.<ref name=":7" />
