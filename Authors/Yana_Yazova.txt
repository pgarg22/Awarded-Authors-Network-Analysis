'''Yana Yazova''' ([[Bulgarian language|Bulgarian]]: Яна Язова) was the pen name of '''Lyuba Todorova Gancheva''' ([[Bulgarian language|Bulgarian]]: Люба Тодорова Ганчева) (1912 &ndash; August 1974), a [[Bulgaria]]n intellectual and writer. Her name is also transcribed as '''Liuba Gantcheva'''.<ref name=chance>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=5QrnjT2NT5MC&pg=PA502 |title=Women Medievalists and the Academy |pages=502–503 |last=Chance |first=Jane |isbn=0299207501 |year=2005 |publisher=University of Wisconsin Press}}</ref>

== Life ==
She was born in [[Lom, Bulgaria|Lom]]<ref name=lom>{{cite web |url=http://adm.lom.bg/en/distinguished.html |title=Notable Lom People |publisher=Municipality of Lom}}</ref> and received a Master's degree in [[Slavic studies|Slavic philology]] from [[Sofia University]] in 1935.<ref name=chance/> Gancheva also studied French [[philology]] at the [[Sorbonne]]. In 1940 she published a historical drama ''The Last of the Pagans'' and a novel ''The Captain''. From 1942 to 1943, she co-edited the children's magazine ''Blok'' with [[Nikola Balabanov]], brother of Prof. [[Alexander Balabanov]], her mentor and lover. Gancheva married another man in 1943. Later in life, she was pressured to write poetry promoting [[communism]] but she instead chose to become a recluse.<ref name=chance/>

Her poetry was translated into Esperanto, Czech, Serbian and Ukrainian. She travelled extensively in Europe and the Near East and wrote about her travels.<ref name=lom/>

Her historical novel ''Alexander of Macedon'', the trilogy ''Balkans'' and the anti-communist novel ''Salt Gulf'' were published after her death.<ref name=chance/>

She was found murdered in her home in [[Sofia]] in 1974.<ref name=chance/>
