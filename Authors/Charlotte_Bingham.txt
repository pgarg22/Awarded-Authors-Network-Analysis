The Hon. ''' Charlotte Bingham''' (born 29 June 1942) is an English novelist who has written over 30 mainly historical [[romance novel]]s and has also written for many television programmes including ''[[Upstairs, Downstairs (1971 TV series)|Upstairs, Downstairs]]''; ''[[Play for Today]]''; and ''[[Robin's Nest (TV series)|Robin's Nest]]''. In her television work, she often worked with her husband, [[Terence Brady (writer)|Terence Brady]].

== Biography==

===Early life===
'''The Honourable Charlotte Mary Thérèse Bingham''' was born on 29 June 1942 in [[Haywards Heath]], Sussex.<ref name="Official Website">{{cite news|url=http://www.charlottebingham.com/childhood.html|title=Charlotte Bingham – Childhood|publisher=CharlotteBingham.com|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20070308010002/http://www.charlottebingham.com/childhood.html|archivedate=8 March 2007|df=dmy-all}}</ref> Her father, [[John Bingham, 7th Baron Clanmorris]], wrote detective stories and was a secret member of [[MI5]]. Her mother, Madeleine Bingham, née Madeleine Mary Ebel, was a playwright. Bingham first attended a school in London, but from the age of seven to 16, she went to the Priory of Our Lady's Good Counsel school in Haywards Heath.<ref name="Official Website"/> After she left school, Bingham went to stay in Paris with some French [[Aristocracy (class)|aristocrats]] with the intention of learning French. She had written since she was 10 years old and her first piece of work was a [[Thriller (genre)|thriller]] called ''Death's Ticket''.<ref name="Official Website"/> Bingham wrote her humorous autobiography, called ''Coronet Among the Weeds'', when she was 19, and not long before her twentieth birthday a literary agent discovered her celebrating at the [[Hôtel Ritz Paris|Ritz]]. He was a friend of her parents and he took off the finished manuscript of her autobiography.<ref name="Official Website"/> In 1963, this was published by [[Heinemann (publisher)|Heinemann]] and was a best seller.<ref name="Official Website"/>

===TV work===
In 1966, Charlotte Bingham's first novel, ''[[Lucinda (book)|Lucinda]]'', was published. This was later adapted into a TV screenplay. ''Coronet Among the Grass'', her second autobiography (1972), dealt with the first ten years of her marriage to fellow writer [[Terence Brady (writer)|Terence Brady]]. The couple, who have two children, later adapted ''Coronet Among the Grass'' and ''Coronet Among the Weeds'', into the TV sitcom ''[[No, Honestly]]''. Bingham and her husband collaborated on the scripts for three early episodes of ''[[Upstairs, Downstairs (1971 TV series)|Upstairs, Downstairs]]'', "[[Board Wages]]", "[[I Dies from Love]]" and "[[Out of the Everywhere (Upstairs, Downstairs)|Out of the Everywhere]]". They later wrote an accompanying book called ''[[Upstairs, Downstairs (1971 TV series)#Novelisations|Rose's Story]]''. They also wrote the episodes of ''[[Take Three Girls]]'' featuring Victoria ([[Liza Goddard]]). In the 1970s, Brady and Bingham wrote episodes for the TV series ''[[Play for Today]]'', ''[[Three Comedies of Marriage]]'', ''[[Yes, Honestly]]'' and ''[[Robin's Nest (TV series)|Robin's Nest]]''. During the 1980s and 1990s, they continued to write for the occasional TV series and adapted [[Jilly Cooper]]'s novel ''[[Riders (Cooper novel)|Riders]]'' for the [[television film]] ''[[Riders (1993 film)|Riders]]'' (1993).

===Later work===
Since the 1980s, Bingham has become a romantic novelist, writing novels including ''To Hear a Nightingale'', ''The Business'' and ''In Sunshine or in Shadow''. Most of her books are set in the 19th and 20th centuries. In 1996, she won the [[Romantic Novel of the Year Award]] from the [[Romantic Novelists' Association]].
