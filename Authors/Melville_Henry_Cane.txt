'''Melville Henry Cane''' (April 15, 1879 &ndash; March 10, 1980) was an American poet and [[lawyer]]. He studied at [[Columbia University]], and was the author of the influential book, ''Making a Poem'' (1953).<ref name="Alan2009">{{cite book|author=Filreis, Alan|title=Counter-revolution of the Word: The Conservative Attack on Modern Poetry, 1945-1960: Easyread Edition|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=sL1RfybY0_QC&pg=PA53|accessdate=28 June 2013|date=17 September 2009|publisher=ReadHowYouWant.com|isbn=978-1-4587-2314-7|pages=53â€“}}</ref>

==Early life and education==
As a [[Columbia University]] student in 1900,<ref name="col00">{{cite book|author=Columbia University, Class of 1900|author2=Class of 1900 Staff |author3=Melville Cane |author4=William Churchill De Mille |title=The Naughty-Naughtian: Being the Book of the Class of 1900, in Columbia College|url=https://archive.org/details/bub_gb_SBATAAAAIAAJ|accessdate=28 June 2013|year=1900|publisher=Class of 1900.}}</ref> Cane worked as a [[reporter]] at the ''[[New York Evening Post]]'' and also wrote poetry. He also co-wrote the 1900 [[Varsity Show]] at Columbia, writing lyrics for libretto ''The Governor's Vrouw'' (1900), a two-act comic opera  he co-wrote with [[Henry Sydnor Harrison]], while [[John Erskine (educator)|John Erskine]] write the music.<ref>{{cite web |title=The Governor's Vrouw libretto cover|url=https://exhibitions.cul.columbia.edu/items/show/4185 |publisher=CU Libraries Exhibitions |accessdate=June 27, 2013}}</ref> Cane earned his law degree in 1905 and later specialized in copyright law.

==Career==
Cane was a legal counsel to notable writers like [[Sinclair Lewis]], [[Upton Sinclair]], [[Ayn Rand]], [[William Saroyan]], and [[Thomas Wolfe]] and also served on the board of directors for Harcourt Brace Jovanovich Inc. Cane was also awarded the [[Frost Medal]] by the Poetry Society of America in 1971 for lifetime achievement.

Some of Cane's works are: ''January Garden'' (1926), ''Behind Dark Places''' (1930), ''And Pastures New'' (1956) and ''Snow Towards Evening'' (1974).
