'''Holly Black''' ''[[née]]'' '''Riggenbach'''<ref name="Locus" /> (born 1971) is an American writer and editor best known for her Children's and Young Adult Fiction. Her most recent work is the New York Times Bestselling Young Adult the Folk of the Air series. She is also well known for ''[[The Spiderwick Chronicles]]'', a series of children's fantasy books she created with writer and illustrator [[Tony DiTerlizzi]], and her debut trilogy of Young Adult novels officially called the ''Modern Faerie Tales''.<ref>{{cite web|title=The Modern Faerie Tales Archives|url=http://blackholly.com/series/the-modern-faerie-tales/|website=Holly Black|access-date=17 January 2016}}</ref>

==Early life and education==

Black was born in [[West Long Branch, New Jersey]]<ref name="Locus">{{Citation | last =Locus | author-link = Locus (magazine) | title =Holly Black: Through the Maze | journal =Locus | volume =56, 5 | issue =544  | pages =84 | date =May 2006 | url =http://www.locusmag.com/2006/Issues/05Black.html |access-date=2007-12-13 }}</ref> in 1971, and during her early years her family lived in a "decrepit Victorian house."<ref name="AboutHolly">{{Citation
 |last        = Black
 |first       = Holly
 |title       = About Holly
 |url         = http://www.blackholly.com/aboutholly.htm
 |access-date  = 2007-12-13
 |url-status     = dead
 |archive-url  = https://web.archive.org/web/20071105231508/http://www.blackholly.com/aboutholly.htm
 |archive-date = November 5, 2007
 |df          = mdy-all
}}</ref>
Black graduated with a B.A. in [[English studies|English]] from [[The College of New Jersey]] in 1994. She worked as a production editor on medical journals including ''The Journal of Pain'' while studying at [[Rutgers University]]. She considered becoming a librarian as a backup career, but writing drew her away. She edited and contributed to the [[Role-playing game|role-playing]] culture magazine ''[[d8 (magazine)|d8]]'' in 1996.{{Citation needed|date=July 2020}}

In 1999 she married her high school sweetheart, Theo Black, an illustrator and web designer.<ref name="Locus" /> In 2008 she was described as residing in [[Amherst, Massachusetts]].<ref>[http://www.canada.com/edmontonjournal/news/culture/story.html?id=5486cf2f-6676-4419-b842-f179f5a29e06&k=35229 "Author's fairy tale comes true"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121107074842/http://www.canada.com/edmontonjournal/news/culture/story.html?id=5486cf2f-6676-4419-b842-f179f5a29e06&k=35229 |date=November 7, 2012 }}, ''[[Edmonton Journal]]'', February 14, 2008. Accessed February 20, 2008. "Today, Holly lives in West Long Branch, New Jersey with her husband of 10 years, working as a full-time writer and an avid collector of rare folklore volumes, spooky dolls and outrageous hats."</ref>

==Literary career==

'''Modern Faerie Tales'''

Black's first novel, ''[[Tithe: A Modern Faerie Tale]]'', was published by [[Simon & Schuster]] in 2002. There have been two sequels set in the same universe. The first, ''[[Valiant: A Modern Tale of Faerie|Valiant]]'' (2005), won the inaugural [[Andre Norton Award]] for Young Adult Science Fiction and Fantasy. By vote of ''Locus'' readers for the [[Locus Awards]], ''Valiant'' and ''[[Ironside (Black novel)|Ironside]]'' (2007) ranked fourth and sixth among the year's young-adult books.<ref>{{Cite web|title=sfadb : Locus Awards All Nominees|url=https://www.sfadb.com/Locus_Awards_All_Nominees|access-date=2021-05-05|website=www.sfadb.com}}</ref>

'''The Spiderwick Chronicles'''

In 2003, Black published the first two books of ''[[The Spiderwick Chronicles]]'', a collaboration with artist [[Tony DiTerlizzi]]. The fifth and last book in the series reached the top of the [[New York Times Bestseller|''New York Times'' Bestseller list]] in 2004.{{Citation needed|date=July 2020}} A [[The Spiderwick Chronicles (film)|film adaptation of the series]] was released in 2008,<ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0416236/ | title=The Spiderwick Chronicles | publisher=[[IMDb]] | access-date=March 4, 2013}}</ref> of which Black was co-executive producer.{{Citation needed|date=July 2020}}

'''The Curse Workers'''

''[[White Cat (book)|White Cat]]'', the first in her Curse Workers Series, was published in 2010. ''White Cat'' was followed by ''[[Red Glove (book)|Red Glove]]'' (2011) and the trilogy concluded with ''[[Black Heart (Black novel)|Black Heart]]'' in 2012. In 2011, Black stated that the Curse Workers books had been optioned by Vertigo Pictures and producer Mark Morgan.<ref>{{cite news|date=May 9, 2011|title='Spiderwick' Author Holly Black Gets Unexcited For 'White Cat' Movie|work=MTV Hollywood Crush|url=http://hollywoodcrush.mtv.com/2011/05/09/holly-black-white-cat-movie/|access-date=March 4, 2013}}</ref> 

'''Magisterium'''

In 2012, [[Scholastic Corporation|Scholastic]] acquired a five-book series written by Black and [[Cassandra Clare]] to be called ''[[The Magisterium Series|Magisterium]]''. Its first volume, ''[[The Iron Trial]]'', was published on September 9, 2014.<ref>{{cite news|date=April 19, 2012|title=Scholastic Acquires Five-Book Middle Grade Series by Bestselling Authors Holly Black and Cassandra Clare|work=Scholastic|url=http://mediaroom.scholastic.com/node/562|access-date=March 4, 2013}}</ref> The final book in the series, ''[[The Golden Tower]]'', was published in 2018. 

'''The Folk of the Air'''

The Cruel Prince published in 2017. The first book of The Folk of the Air was critically acclaimed and nominated for the [[Locus Award for Best Young Adult Book|Locus Award]]<ref>{{Cite web|last=locusmag|date=2019-06-29|title=2019 Locus Awards Winners|url=https://locusmag.com/2019/06/2019-locus-awards-winners/|access-date=2020-12-11|website=Locus Online|language=en-US}}</ref>  and the [[Lodestar Award for Best Young Adult Book|Lodestar Award]].<ref>{{Citation|title=Lodestar Award for Best Young Adult Book|date=2020-12-08|url=https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lodestar_Award_for_Best_Young_Adult_Book&oldid=993014691|work=Wikipedia|language=en|access-date=2020-12-11}}</ref> The sequel, The Wicked King (2018) debuted at the #1 position of the [[The New York Times Best Seller list|New York Times Bestseller List]].<ref>{{Cite news|title=Young Adult Hardcover Books - Best Sellers - Books - Jan. 27, 2019 - The New York Times|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/books/best-sellers/2019/01/27/young-adult-hardcover/|access-date=2020-12-11|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> The Wicked King was also nominated for the Lodestar Award.<ref>{{Cite web|date=2020-04-07|title=2020 Hugo Awards|url=http://www.thehugoawards.org/hugo-history/2020-hugo-awards/|access-date=2020-12-11|website=The Hugo Awards|language=en-US}}</ref> The Queen of Nothing released in November 2019. With that release the series debuted at #3 on the New York Times Bestseller List. <ref>{{Cite news|title=Children's Series Books - Best Sellers - Books - Dec. 8, 2019 - The New York Times|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/books/best-sellers/2019/12/08/series-books/|access-date=2020-12-11|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>

'''Standalones'''

A standalone novel, ''[[The Coldest Girl in Coldtown]]'', was released by [[Little, Brown]] in September 2013.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/childrens/childrens-book-news/article/56107-fall-2013-sneak-previews.html | title=Fall 2013 Sneak Previews | publisher=[[Publishers Weekly]] | access-date=March 4, 2013}}</ref> Black published a short story of the same name in the vampire anthology ''The Eternal Kiss: 13 Vampire Tales of Blood and Desire''. ''The Coldest Girl in Coldtown'' was a Nebula Finalist in 2013.<ref>{{Cite web|last1=Fictions|first1=© 2019 Science|last2=America|first2=Fantasy Writers of|last3=SFWA®|first3=Inc|last4=Fiction|first4=Nebula Awards® are registered trademarks of Science|last5=America|first5=Fantasy Writers of|last6=SFWA|first6=Inc Opinions expressed on this web site are not necessarily those of|title=The Coldest Girl in Coldtown|url=https://nebulas.sfwa.org/nominated-work/the-coldest-girl-in-coldtown-2/|access-date=2020-12-11|website=The Nebula Awards®|language=en-US}}</ref>

''[[Doll Bones]]'' was published in May 2013, and was awarded a Newbery Honor<ref name="edition.cnn.com">[http://edition.cnn.com/2014/01/27/living/newbery-caldecott-awards-2014-kate-dicamillo-books/ "And the Newbery, Caldecott award winners are&nbsp;..."], Ashley Strickland, CNN, January 27, 2014.</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=http://books.simonandschuster.com/Doll-Bones/Holly-Black/9781416963981 | title=Doll Bones | publisher=[[Simon and Schuster]] | access-date=March 4, 2013}}</ref> and a [[Mythopoeic Fantasy Award]].<ref name="mythopoeic">{{cite web |url=http://www.mythsoc.org/awards/winners/ |title=Mythopoeic Awards |publisher=Mythopoeic Society |access-date=10 October 2014 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141005231047/http://www.mythsoc.org/awards/winners/ |archive-date=October 5, 2014 |url-status=dead |df=mdy-all }}</ref> 

''[[The Darkest Part of the Forest]]'' was published in 2015. 

Black has also written dozens of short works and co-edited at least three anthologies of [[speculative fiction]].{{Citation needed|date=July 2020}}
