'''Moisés Kaufman''' (born November 21, 1963) is a Venezuelan theater [[Theatre director|director]], filmmaker, [[playwright]], founder of [[Tectonic Theater Project]], based in New York City, and co-founder of [[Miami New Drama]] at the [[Colony Theatre]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=OUR TEAM|url=https://www.colonymb.org/mind-team|access-date=2020-07-30|website=colonytheatre|language=en}}</ref> He was awarded the 2016 National Medal of Arts by President Barack Obama. He is best known for creating ''[[The Laramie Project]]'' (2000) with other members of [[Tectonic Theater Project]]. He has directed extensively on Broadway and Internationally, and is the author of numerous plays, including ''[[Gross Indecency: The Three Trials of Oscar Wilde]]'' and ''[[33 Variations]]''.

Born and raised in [[Caracas]], [[Venezuela]], he moved as a young man to [[New York City]] in 1987.<ref name="hurwitt">{{Cite news  | last = Hurwitt  | first = Robert  | title = The 'Laramie' process  | newspaper = San Francisco Chronicle  | date = 2001-05-20  | url = http://www.sfgate.com/cgi-bin/article.cgi?f=/c/a/2001/05/20/PK65537.DTL}}</ref>

==Biography==
Kaufman is of [[Romanian Jews|Romanian-Jewish]] and [[Ukrainian Jews|Ukrainian-Jewish]] descent, and was born in [[Caracas, Venezuela]].<ref name=NYT>{{cite news|author=Robert Myers|title=Nothing Mega About It Except the Applause|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1997/05/25/theater/nothing-mega-about-it-except-the-applause.html|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=25 May 1997|access-date=6 July 2009}}</ref> He is an alumnus of Venezuela's [[Universidad Metropolitana]], where he began to study theatre.<ref>[http://www.unimet.edu.ve/obama-condecorara-al-unimetano-moises-kaufman/]</ref> After immigrating to the United States, he went to college in New York and graduated from [[NYU]].<ref name="accolades">[http://www.nyu.edu/about/news-publications/news/2016/september/ArtsAccoladesSeptember.html]</ref>

In 2005 he described himself in an interview by saying, "I am Venezuelan, I am Jewish, I am [[gay]], I live in New York. I am the sum of all my cultures. I couldn't write anything that didn't incorporate all that I am."<ref>{{cite news|last=Orozco|first=Jose|title=True To Reality: An Interview with Moises Kaufman|url=http://www.morphizm.com/recommends/interviews/orozco_moises.html|access-date=6 August 2012|newspaper=Morphizm|date=March 21, 2005|quote=I am Venezuelan, I am Jewish, I am gay, I live in New York. I am the sum of all my cultures. I couldn't write anything that didn't incorporate all that I am.}}</ref>

Kaufman was awarded a [[Guggenheim Fellowship]] in 2002, following the premiere of ''[[The Laramie Project]]'', which was based on extensive interviews with residents and commentators in and around Wyoming who were involved with the aftermath of the murder of gay student [[Matthew Shepard]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.gf.org/fellows/7637-moises-kaufman |title=Moisés Kaufman |access-date=2009-03-03 |publisher=John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation |year=2002 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110622020816/http://www.gf.org/fellows/7637-moises-kaufman |archive-date=2011-06-22 }}</ref>

He made his [[Broadway (theatre)|Broadway]] directing debut in the 2004 production of ''[[I Am My Own Wife]]'' by [[Doug Wright]], for which he received a [[Tony Award]] nomination for Best Direction of a Play.

On September 22, 2016, Kaufman was awarded the [[National Medal of Arts]] and Humanities in a ceremony conducted by U.S. president [[Barack Obama]]. He is the first Venezuelan to receive the honor.<ref name="accolades"/>
